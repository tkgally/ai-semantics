- Plain-language: today the picture experiment that had been waiting for a fresh budget day finally ran its deciding step, and for the first time it points the way the project predicted. The experiment asks a model to pick, out of ten images, the one matching an ambiguous word's intended sense. The project's bet is that seeing a picture helps only in the cases the words leave unsettled. By describing each picture by its visual form only — without naming what it is — the experiment left a real set of unsettled cases, and there, actually seeing the picture chose right about 45% of the time (far above the one-in-ten of guessing), while adding nothing where the description already settled it. A control confirms the picture is helping with word meaning, not just catching the eye (with the word removed, the models score only at chance). Three cautions were kept up front: one model got worse overall with pictures, one model's no-information baseline was slightly above chance, and the size of the effect is partly manufactured by stripping names from the descriptions — so this shows the pattern of the bet, not that the gap is wide in ordinary language.
