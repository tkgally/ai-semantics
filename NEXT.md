It added a freely available standard reference article on these words and wrote a short essay drawing one borrowed distinction (from the philosopher David Kaplan) through the project's central question. Kaplan splits such a word into two layers: a fixed rule ("I" means whoever is speaking) and the actual thing the rule lands on this time (this speaker). The essay's point is that the two layers fall on opposite sides of the project's main divide — the fixed rule is just the kind of standing, convention-set pattern a text-trained system picks up for free, but the second layer needs a situation the system is actually *in* (a speaker it is, a clock it reads), and a text-only model has none of these; it only ever has the situation the text *describes*. So for such a model what "now" or "here" lands on is always supplied by the words, never read off a situation it occupies. Nothing was measured; no comparison to people was drawn.
