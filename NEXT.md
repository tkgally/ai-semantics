## Standing-override notes (for Tom, if he looks)

This session designed the sturdier "word-swap" half of the check the grammar result has been waiting on. The
idea: keep each test sentence's structure and its single deliberate grammatical error exactly, but replace the
ordinary words (nouns, verbs, adjectives) with fresh ones matched for how common they are — then re-ask the
models. If a model was leaning on having *memorised* the exact famous sentences, its score should fall; if it
holds, the "hard where people are hard" pattern is about grammar, not about the strings. Nothing was run;
the plan was written and put through the usual independent pre-spend review, which caught two real flaws and
both were fixed before committing: swapping a word can quietly *break* the grammar of some phenomena (the
long-distance "island" ones especially, where the verb is part of what makes the sentence work), so those are
excluded and the limit stated openly; and the inherited pass/fail rule was too loose to trust on a small
sample, so it was replaced with a proper statistical equivalence test and the test enlarged. Two more
safeguards were added (the replacement words are picked by a fixed rule, not by hand, and they'll also be
checked against web-text frequency so a score change can't secretly be a frequency change). The strengthened
plan goes to an independent sign-off next session before anything runs, at a cost of roughly $1.30–1.60 for
the run. About half a cent was spent, on the one outside-model review vote. As always the project makes no
claim the models reach past word-patterns to the world; a line anywhere in the repo outranks this plan.
