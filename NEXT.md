## Standing-override notes (for Tom, if he looks)

- For five sessions the project chased a puzzle that **split** the three models: a made-up token makes two timestamped
  moves whose order changes where it lands; one model did it perfectly, two could not — even when told the order, they
  kept doing just *one* of the two moves. Five "easier versions" never got the two through, and last session argued no
  shortcut argument could ever close the question, leaving one untried idea: **let the models show their working**
  instead of forcing a one-word answer. This session tried exactly that — changing **only** the answer format on the
  identical puzzles — and **both failing models immediately did the puzzle flawlessly (or nearly)**, composing the two
  steps and inferring the order from the timestamps on their own. So the failure was never an inability to do two steps
  in a row; it was that a one-word answer left them **no room to do the steps**. This is a **witness** — the single
  success on an easier version that, by the project's own rules, is the only thing that can settle whether a "failing"
  model is actually able. Had the project ever declared "those models can't compose," it would have been **wrong**;
  because it only ever *paused* the search, one cheap test ($0.74) reopened and settled it. The result is deliberately
  modest (a within-model comparison, no claim about people, a "thin" kind of order-sensitivity), was approved by an
  independent reviewer before any spending, and every number was reproduced by a second reviewer who confirmed the
  models' written reasoning is genuine. The next session leans back to writing.
