# Firth 1957 — A Synopsis of Linguistic Theory, 1930-55

## What it is

The source of the single most-quoted line in distributional semantics: **"You shall know a word by the company it keeps."** J.R. Firth, "A Synopsis of Linguistic Theory, 1930-55," in *Studies in Linguistic Analysis* (Special Volume of the Philological Society), Oxford: Blackwell, 1957, pp. 1-32. Firth's "meaning by collocation" — that one *mode of meaning* of a word is to be found in its habitual collocations, the word-material in which it is most characteristically embedded — is, alongside [`source/harris-1954-distributional-structure`](harris-1954-distributional-structure.md), one of the two founding statements of the distributional tradition the [`meaning-senses.md`](../../meaning-senses.md) `distributional` tag calls "The Firth/Harris tradition."

**This is a thin, secondary-sourced page.** The 1957 primary is not openly accessible: it is a chapter in a 1957 Blackwell Philological Society volume, behind library walls, and no legitimate open-access full text was reachable (see "Reachability" below). The quotes below were therefore **read in a secondary source, not in the Firth primary**, and every one is flagged as such. The page exists so the project can cite the Firth line with a real, fetched, checkable provenance chain — never to present a secondary quote as primary.

## Provenance and reachability

- **Primary not consulted.** No open-access full text of Firth 1957 was found. Routes tried (2026-06-24): the Taylor & Francis / publisher channels (the chapter is in a print Blackwell volume, no OA PDF); a faculty link surfaced by search (`www.lel.ed.ac.uk/homes/patrick/firth.pdf`) returned HTTP 404; the Internet Archive holds related Firth volumes (*Papers in Linguistics 1934-51*; the Palmer-edited *Selected Papers*) only as controlled-lending scans, not open full text; a study-notes page (czlwang.com) reproduced only the famous line as paraphrase with no primary locator. No primary scan was opened, so no quote here is verified against Firth's own text.
- **Secondary source actually read (verbatim quotes below come from here):** Mikael Brunila and Jack LaViolette, "What company do words keep? Revisiting the distributional semantics of J.R. Firth & Zellig Harris," arXiv:2205.07750 (2022). Read from the arXiv PDF (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2205.07750, fetched and text-extracted 2026-06-24). This is a computational-linguistics paper doing a close reading of the Firth and Harris primaries; it quotes Firth verbatim with primary page locators (their citation `Firth 1957c` = the Synopsis).
- **Cross-confirmation of the famous line:** Quote Investigator (https://quoteinvestigator.com/2022/09/18/word-company/, fetched 2026-06-24) independently reproduces the line and supplies a fuller sentence, citing p. 179 of the 1968 Palmer-edited reprint (*Selected Papers of J.R. Firth 1952-59*). Two independent secondaries giving the identical wording is the basis for treating the line itself as reliably attributed; it is still not primary-verified here.

## The famous line (quoted via Brunila & LaViolette 2022; primary NOT consulted)

As reproduced in Brunila & LaViolette (2022), Introduction, attributing it to the Synopsis at p. 11:

> "You shall know a word by the company it keeps"

— quoted by Brunila & LaViolette as "Firth (1957c, 11)". Quote Investigator gives the same line with a closing exclamation point and a fuller surrounding sentence ("In these examples, the word *ass* is in familiar and habitual company, commonly collocated with you silly—, he is a silly—, don't be such an—. You shall know a word by the company it keeps!"), locating it at p. 179 of the 1968 *Selected Papers* reprint. Both are secondary; the project has not seen either Firth original.

## Meaning by collocation (quoted via Brunila & LaViolette 2022; primary NOT consulted)

Brunila & LaViolette report, with Synopsis page locators, that Firth held one mode of meaning to be "meaning by collocation," found by examining the —

> "habitual collocations"

— of words and the —

> "word-material"

— in which they are —

> "most characteristically embedded"

— their citation `(Firth, 1957c, 11-12)`. They add that collocation for Firth went beyond "mere juxtaposition," stating instead —

> "an order of mutual expectancy"

— and "mutual prehension" (their citation `Firth, 1957c, 12`).

(All five quoted fragments in this section are quoted *via* Brunila & LaViolette 2022; the Firth primary was not consulted. The fragments appear inside Brunila & LaViolette's own sentences, so the locators are theirs, not independently verified against Firth.)

## Bearing on this project

- **[`concept/distributional-meaning`](../concepts/distributional-meaning.md).** The concept page opens with "The canonical formulation is Firth's: 'you shall know a word by the company it keeps' (1957; not in-repo)." This page upgrades that "not in-repo" to a real, fetched provenance chain — but only a *secondary* one, so the concept page should still not claim the Firth line as primary-verified. The distinction matters for the project's no-fabrication discipline: the line is reliably *attributed*, not primary-*read*.
- **The Firth/Harris split.** Brunila & LaViolette's thesis (read here) is that Firth and Harris, routinely bundled as co-founders of distributional semantics, in fact diverge: Harris's distribution is internal to linguistic form, while Firth's "collocation" is one mode among several and is always situated in a broader "context of situation." For this project that is a caution against treating "the distributional hypothesis" as a single doctrine: a probe instantiating Harris-style form-internal distribution is not thereby instantiating Firth's wider contextual notion. This is a secondary-sourced historiographic claim, useful for framing, not an empirical result.
- **The `distributional` caveat.** Firth's notion, on this reading, is *less* form-internal than the embedding tradition that quotes him — his "company" includes situational and cultural context, not just neighboring tokens. That sharpens the [`meaning-senses.md`](../../meaning-senses.md) warning that distributional structure is silent on reference/truth: the NLP appropriation of Firth arguably narrows his notion to exactly the form-internal residue that *is* so silent.

## What it can ground

- The reliably-attributed wording of the founding Firth line ("You shall know a word by the company it keeps") and the "meaning by collocation" framing — **as an attribution carried by named secondary sources**, suitable for a concept/essay page that says "Firth's canonical formulation," never for a claim that rests on having read the primary.
- The historiographic point (via Brunila & LaViolette 2022) that the Firth and Harris versions of distributionalism diverge, and that NLP's use of Firth narrows his contextual notion.

## What it cannot ground

- Anything requiring the Firth primary: no page may cite this as a primary reading of Firth 1957, because no one in this project read Firth 1957. Quotes here are secondary.
- Any precise textual claim about Firth's surrounding argument, his exact pagination, or wording beyond the short fragments the secondaries reproduce.
- Any LLM/empirical claim: like Harris 1954, this grounds a *theory*, never a model result.

## Known limits

- **Secondary-only.** Every quote is via Brunila & LaViolette (2022) or Quote Investigator; the Firth original was unreachable as open access. `status: secondary-only` records this. If a later session reaches a legitimate full text (e.g. controlled-lending scan, library copy), it should re-verify each quote against Firth's own pages, correct any locator, and only then consider promoting the status.
- **Locator provenance is second-hand.** The page numbers (Synopsis pp. 11-12; reprint p. 179) are the secondaries' attributions, not read by this project. Brunila & LaViolette's `1957c` refers to the Synopsis chapter; that mapping is theirs.
- The fuller "ass" sentence from Quote Investigator and the page-179 locator come from the 1968 *Selected Papers* reprint, not the 1957 first printing; pagination differs between the two, which is why the famous line carries two different page numbers (11 in the 1957 Synopsis per Brunila & LaViolette; 179 in the 1968 reprint per Quote Investigator).
