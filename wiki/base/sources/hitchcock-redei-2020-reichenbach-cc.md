# Hitchcock & Rédei 2020 — Reichenbach's Common Cause Principle (SEP)

## What it is

Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry on **Reichenbach's Common Cause Principle (RCCP)**, by Christopher Hitchcock (Caltech) and Miklós Rédei (LSE), first published Mon Jan 13, 2020. It is a **methodological / philosophy-of-science source**, not a human-labeled linguistic resource. Its subject is exactly the inferential move the project's convergence-ladder essay informally reinvents: when two events are correlated and neither causes the other, what licenses inferring that they **share a common cause** rather than being a coincidence — and what conditions (screening-off / conditional independence) that inference requires, plus the known counterexamples that bound it.

## Key passages (verbatim, double-fetch-confirmed)

**The principle, stated (§2):**

> "Reichenbach's Common Cause Principle says that when such a probabilistic correlation between _A_ and _B_ exists, this is because one of the following causal relations exists: _A_ is a cause of _B_; _B_ is a cause of _A_; or _A_ and _B_ are both caused by a third factor, _C_."

**Screening-off / conditional independence (§2):**

> "Line (2) says that _A_ and _B_ are _conditionally independent_, given _C_."

> "In Reichenbach's terminology, _C_ screens _A_ off from _B_."

**Why the principle matters — correlation licenses causal inference (§2):**

> "The principle is significant because it posits a connection between causal structure and probabilistic correlations, thus facilitating causal inference from observed correlations."

**Counterexamples bound the principle (§5):**

> "A number of authors have proposed counterexamples to the Common Cause Principle."

(The entry's §5 develops these — including a Cartwright "factory" example in which two co-produced outputs are correlated though "Neither _C_ nor _P_ cause the other"; that em-dashed sentence fragment was returned by only one fetch and is therefore noted here as a paraphrase of the example, not quoted as verbatim.)

## Bearing on this project

- **[`essay/cross-level-convergence-ladder`](../../findings/essays/cross-level-convergence-ladder.md) — the principal connection.** The convergence ladder's central question — when several findings "rhyme," do they **share a cause** or merely coincide? — is, in abstract form, Reichenbach's question: the ladder's R1→R2 step (move from each leg under its *own* native instrument to *one shared instrument equalized across levels*) is the behavioral analogue of the **screening-off / conditional-independence** requirement that licenses a common-cause inference. The ladder's standing caveat that "shared priors are not independent witnesses" is the behavioral form of RCCP's demand that the putative common cause render the legs **conditionally independent** rather than non-independent through a shared method.
- **It locates the licensing bar somewhat differently from the ladder, and that sharpens rather than refutes the ladder.** The ladder is deflationary by default: below R2 a cross-level rhyme is *not* a mechanism claim. RCCP, by contrast, is in one respect **more permissive** — per the entry, a stable correlation between two events neither of which causes the other already, under the principle, *licenses* positing a common cause ("facilitating causal inference from observed correlations"). The ladder withholds the mechanism verdict until the instrument is equalized; RCCP says a robust correlation itself motivates the common-cause hypothesis. The honest relation is: RCCP supplies the **independence condition** the ladder's R2 operationalizes, while the ladder's *higher* bar is a deliberate behavioral conservatism (n=3 commercial models, instruments confounded with levels), not a correction of Reichenbach.
- **The counterexamples are the literature's own version of the ladder's deflationary residue.** That "a number of authors have proposed counterexamples" — correlations that resist a clean screening-off common cause — is the methodology literature's acknowledgment that common-cause inference is defeasible, mirroring the ladder's insistence that a rhyme can dissolve at R2.

## What it can ground

- A citation for the **common-cause-vs-coincidence** inference pattern as a recognized methodological problem (RCCP, screening-off, conditional independence) — grounding the convergence ladder's claim that its R1→R2 move is the behavioral analogue of a real independence requirement, not an ad-hoc invention.
- A citation for the point that **a robust correlation can license a common-cause hypothesis** (the entry's "facilitating causal inference from observed correlations"), used to show the external literature locates the licensing bar more permissively than the ladder's deflationary default.
- A citation that **common-cause inference is bounded by counterexamples** (§5), supporting the ladder's defeasibility / "rhyme can dissolve" stance.
