# Kratzer 2021 — "Situations in Natural Language Semantics" (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy)

## What it is

The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy (SEP) survey entry on **situation semantics**, by **Angelika Kratzer** (University of Massachusetts Amherst). First published Mon Feb 12, 2007; substantive revision Wed Oct 13, 2021. SEP is **open-access** (free to read, no paywall). It is a reference-quality survey of the program in natural-language semantics on which linguistic expressions are evaluated relative to **situations** — *parts* of worlds — rather than relative to complete possible worlds, organized around the Barwise–Perry tradition and Kratzer's own possibilistic development of it, with sections on perception reports, Austinian topic situations, implicit domain restriction, donkey sentences, minimality/exemplification, and the relation to Davidsonian event semantics.

This is a philosophy-of-language / formal-semantics source about **human language**. It makes no claim about LLMs. The project uses it only as an a-priori interpretive frame; see *What it grounds*.

## What it provides

### What situation semantics is, and what a situation is

The framing claim (Introduction):

> "Situation semantics was developed as an alternative to possible worlds semantics."

> "In situation semantics, linguistic expressions are evaluated with respect to partial, rather than complete, worlds."

The entry is explicit that the ontology is unsettled (Introduction):

> "There is no consensus about what situations are, just as there is no consensus about what possible worlds or events are."

On the possibilistic version's situations-as-parts-of-worlds (§7, "Minimality and exemplification"):

> "In possibilistic versions of situation semantics, possible situations are parts of possible worlds."

### The Austinian topic / described situation vs. the utterance situation

The load-bearing distinction for the project. The entry attributes to Austin the idea that utterances are *about* particular situations (§3, "Austinian topic situations"):

> "A core feature of many actual analyses of natural language phenomena within situation semantics is the idea attributed to John L. Austin 1950 that utterances are about particular situations, with the actual world being the limiting case."

Austin's truth condition, as the entry states it (§3):

> "As Austin put it, a statement is true when the actual situation to which it refers is of the type described by the statement."

The entry's own terminology, naming the topic situation and its synonyms (§3):

> "Austinian topic situations (also referred to as "focus situations", "described situations", or "reference situations" in the literature)"

And — crucially for the project — the topic situation does **not** in general coincide with the situation the sentence's content describes (§3):

> "topic situations do not always coincide with the situations described by the main predication of a sentence."

### Reference / predicate evaluation relativized to situations

On resource situations as what predicate evaluation depends on (§4, "Situation semantics and implicit domain restrictions"):

> "In a possibilistic situation semantics resource situations are the kind of entities that the evaluation of any predicate routinely depends on."

## What it grounds in this project (relevance)

This source supplies an **a-priori** semantic frame for a question the project's indexicality essay leaves slightly under-named: *what kind of object is the "context" an utterance is anchored to or about?* Situation semantics gives a standard answer — a **situation**, a part of a world — and, with the Austinian apparatus, a standard distinction the project's [`essay/indexical-character-learnable-content-supplied`](../../findings/essays/indexical-character-learnable-content-supplied.md) is already groping toward in its own vocabulary of a **described** vs. an **occupied** origo:

- The essay distinguishes a context the *text supplies* / the model *conditions on* (a **described** origo) from a context an agent is *in* (an **occupied** one). Kratzer's entry gives the literature's nearest pre-existing seam: the **topic / "described" / "reference" situation** that a statement is *about* (§3), as against the **utterance situation** an actual speaker is in. The entry's own gloss that "topic situations do not always coincide with the situations described by the main predication of a sentence" (§3) shows the literature already pries apart *the situation talked about* from *what the content describes* — a finer structure than "context of utterance" alone, useful when the project asks which situation a text-only model can reach.
- Austin's truth condition — "a statement is true when the actual situation to which it refers is of the type described by the statement" (§3) — locates truth in a relation between a statement and *an actual situation it refers to*. The project's essays observe that a text-only model has, on their reading, no occupied/actual situation of its own; situation semantics makes precise the *role* such a situation plays in interpretation, which is the role the essay says the model can only ever fill from the described channel. **The source reports this structure; it makes no claim that any model does or does not occupy a situation** — that application is the essay's, not this page's.
- "Resource situations are the kind of entities that the evaluation of any predicate routinely depends on" (§4) supplies a third notion: interpretation is relativized to situations not just globally but predicate-by-predicate. This is offered as background texture for the relativized-to-situations theme, not as anything the project has yet operationalized.

The frame is a-priori: it tells the project what *kind* of semantic object the "context" in the described/occupied distinction is, and that the literature already separates the situation-talked-about from the content-described — *before* any probe. The essay owns any application to LLMs.

**Honest bounds (these matter):**

1. **This is a survey of human-language semantics, not a claim about LLMs.** The entry concerns how natural-language expressions are interpreted relative to situations; it says nothing about machine systems. The project applies the situation/topic-situation apparatus as an interpretive frame only; the indexicality essay owns the application and makes **no human-vs-LLM empirical claim**.
2. **Survey strength, not primary.** Situation semantics is the program of **Barwise & Perry** (*Situations and Attitudes*, 1983) and of **Kratzer's** own primary papers; this entry surveys and develops it. Those primary works are **not** open-access and stay on the wish-list ([`wanted.md`](../wanted.md)); the apparatus is therefore carried at *reliable-survey* strength. (The entry is by Kratzer herself, so for *her* development it is closer to authorial-survey than third-party survey; treat it as survey nonetheless, since it compresses and updates the primaries.)
3. **The ontology is openly unsettled.** The entry itself states there is "no consensus about what situations are" (Introduction) and surveys rival relational/possibilistic construals. The "situation = part of a world" gloss the project leans on is the **possibilistic** version's commitment (§7), not a framework-neutral theorem; cite it as a well-motivated frame, flagged to its variant.

## What it cannot ground

- Any claim that a particular model *represents*, *occupies*, or *computes evaluation relative to* a situation (vs. producing text consistent with a situation-semantic description). The entry is about human-language semantics; the text-consistency-is-not-mechanism caution lives in the findings pages, not here.
- Any human acceptability or human-meaning-judgment baseline — this is a survey article, not a human-anchored dataset; it provides no labeled resource.
- The empirical magnitude or direction of any model effect.

## Known limits

- A reference survey, not primary research: it reports, organizes, and (being authored by Kratzer) advances the literature, and where the field disagrees on the ontology of situations it says so rather than deciding. Treat the apparatus as a frame, not a verdict.
- The Austin attribution ("Austin 1950") is the entry's citation to Austin's primary, reproduced here for fidelity; the project has not verified it against Austin's text (not in-repo, OA status unchecked).
