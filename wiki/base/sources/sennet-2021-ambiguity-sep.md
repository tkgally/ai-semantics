# Sennet 2021 — "Ambiguity" (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy)

## What it is

The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy (SEP) survey entry on **ambiguity**, by **Adam Sennet** (UC Davis). First published Mon May 16, 2011; substantive revision Sat May 22, 2021. SEP is **open-access** (free to read, no paywall). It is a reference-quality survey of *ambiguity* in the philosophy-of-language / linguistics sense: what it is to bear "more than one meaning," how lexical ambiguity (homonymy) relates to and differs from **polysemy**, how ambiguity is to be told apart from **vagueness** and from **sense-generality / under-specification**, and what diagnostic **tests** (conjunction reduction / zeugma being the central one) are used to detect it.

This is a philosophy-of-language source about **human language**. It makes no claim about LLMs and provides no human-annotated dataset. The project uses it only as an a-priori interpretive frame; see *What it grounds*.

## Why it bears on this project (the scalar-vs-disjunctive axis)

The project's lexical wedge rests on a distinction between **polysemy** (related senses, graded/scalar) and **homonymy** (unrelated senses sharing a form by accident, discrete/disjunctive). Until this ingestion, [`concept/polysemy`](../concepts/polysemy.md) carried that distinction on "common knowledge in the field" — its own *Honest gaps* section flags the canonical lexicographic sources (Cruse, Murphy, Lyons) as **not yet in-repo**, so the lexicographer's contrast had no citable in-repo source. The essay [`essay/ambiguity-kind-not-level`](../../findings/essays/ambiguity-kind-not-level.md) leans on exactly this contrast — a **scalar** ambiguity (a similarity midpoint, with a nearer pole, e.g. a polysemy bridging item) vs. a **disjunctive** ambiguity (two complete readings, no nearer pole, e.g. a balanced homonym) — and that essay's *own provenance note* names "a future ingest of a semantics/lexicography source on the polysemy-vs-homonymy ... ambiguity distinction" as its **weakest provenance point**, observing that "the contrast stands on the in-repo [`concept/polysemy`] and the result's caveat alone."

Sennet's entry is a reference-quality, open-access philosophy source that states the **homonymy/polysemy carving** directly (lexical ambiguity = "two or more lexical entries that correspond to the same word"; polysemy = "a single lexeme" with "interestingly related" meanings) and separately states the **ambiguity-vs-vagueness/generality** distinction and the **zeugma diagnostic**. It thereby supplies the in-repo source the concept page's *Honest gaps* and the essay's provenance note both call for — grounding the *framing* of the scalar/disjunctive contrast, not any empirical result.

A caveat the project should carry forward (Sennet's, not the project's): the homonymy/polysemy line is **traditional but not crisp**. Sennet calls it "one traditional carving" and says polysemy is related senses "in some hard to specify sense" — which is consistent with, and arguably reinforces, the project's gradience framing (the boundary itself is fuzzy), but it means the source grounds the *distinction as a recognized one*, not as a sharp formal partition.

## Key passages (verbatim, double-fetch-confirmed)

**The opening characterization of ambiguity (§1 Introduction):**

> "Ambiguity is generally taken to be a property enjoyed by signs that bear multiple (legitimate) interpretations in a language or, more generally, some system of signs."

**The homonymy/polysemy carving (§1 Introduction) — the load-bearing passage for this project:**

> "One traditional carving is that ambiguity in words is a matter of two or more lexical entries that correspond to the same word and polysemy a single lexeme that has multiple meanings."

**Polysemy as *related* senses (§1 Introduction):**

> "'polysemy' refers to a phenomenon that is closely related to ambiguity, but often is characterized as a term with multiple meanings that are, in some hard to specify sense, interestingly related."

**Lexical ambiguity defined (§3.1 Lexical Ambiguity):**

> "The lexicon contains entries that are homophonous, or even co-spelled, but differ in meanings and even syntactic categories."

**Ambiguity vs. vagueness, by example (§2.1 Vagueness):**

> "'bat' is not vague but it is ambiguous. 'Is bald' looks to be vague but not ambiguous."

**Zeugma / conjunction reduction as the diagnostic (§4.1 Conjunction Reduction):**

> "The reduced sentence is zeugmatic for obvious reasons. This is evidence for ambiguity (or polysemy) in 'light'."

**The diagnostics are reliable in clear cases, fragile in hard ones (§4.7 Contextual Resolution and Zeugma):**

> "The bottom line is that in clear cases, the tests work great. In controversial cases, one must be very careful and run many of them and hope for the best."

## What it grounds in this project (relevance)

- **[`concept/polysemy`](../concepts/polysemy.md) — the principal connection.** This source discharges the concept page's *Honest gaps* note that "the lexicographic treatment of ... sense-relation vocabulary is pending" and that "references to 'the lexicographer's picture' rest on common knowledge in the field, not on a citable in-repo source." Sennet supplies a citable, open-access, reference-quality statement of the **polysemy (related senses) vs. homonymy/lexical-ambiguity (distinct entries) carving** and of the **ambiguity-vs-vagueness/generality** distinction the concept page also invokes. It does **not** replace the still-wanted canonical lexical-semantics monographs (Cruse, Murphy, Lyons) — it is a philosophy-of-language survey, not a lexicology text — but it removes the "no in-repo source at all" gap for the basic distinction.
- **[`essay/ambiguity-kind-not-level`](../../findings/essays/ambiguity-kind-not-level.md) — strengthens the essay's flagged weakest point.** The essay's scalar-vs-disjunctive contrast maps onto Sennet's polysemy-vs-homonymy carving: a polysemy bridging item (related senses) is the *scalar* case with a nearer pole; a balanced homonym (distinct lexical entries, unrelated senses) is the *disjunctive* case with none. Sennet grounds the linguistic reality of that two-way carving the essay's framing presupposes. **It grounds the framing, not the essay's empirical re-reading** (which is `internal-contrast-only` and asserts no human comparison).

## What it cannot ground

- **Any empirical claim about LLMs.** A philosophy survey of human-language ambiguity, with no labeled resource — it cannot serve as the `anchors:` resource for any claim or result, and it bears on the *framing* of the polysemy/homonymy distinction, not on any model behavior.
- **The graded/scalar *measurement*.** The graded, human-rated usage-similarity instrument the lexical wedge needs is still [`resource/wic-graded-usage-similarity`](../resources/wic-graded-usage-similarity.md) / the DWUG anchor — Sennet provides the *conceptual* carving, not a graded scale or annotations.
- **Regular / systematic / Apresjan-style polysemy.** The brief asked specifically about regular/systematic polysemy; the entry, as fetched, does **not** develop that terminology (no "regular polysemy", "systematic polysemy", or Apresjan treatment was found in the sections fetched). That part of the want is **not** discharged by this source and remains open (a dedicated Apresjan / regular-polysemy OA source would be a separate ingestion).

## Known limits

- **The §4.7 quote was trimmed for double-fetch disagreement.** The live-URL fetch returned a longer tail ("... and hope for the best; it will sometimes involve sifting through degrees of zeugma rather than triumphantly producing an indisputable result"), but the archived-edition fetch returned only "... and hope for the best." Because the tail did not reproduce character-for-character across both URLs, **only the double-confirmed portion is quoted above**; the trailing "degrees of zeugma" clause is **not** certified verbatim here (its sense — that hard cases require sifting degrees of zeugma — is reported as a paraphrase only).
- **The zeugma *example* sentences are single-fetch only.** The illustrative pair/triple for the §4.1 test ("The colours are light." / "The feathers are light." / "?The colours and the feathers are light.") was returned by one fetch and **not** re-confirmed against the archived edition; it is therefore **not** quoted as verbatim above. The §4.1 *conclusion* sentence ("The reduced sentence is zeugmatic ...") is double-confirmed and is quoted.
- **Vagueness section, finer quotes not certified.** A §2.1 sentence on vagueness as "lack of precision in the meaning or reference of a term" and a §2.3 note treating "sense generality" as vagueness in the cognitive-linguistics literature (citing Dunbar 2001) appeared in single fetches and were **not** double-confirmed; they are deliberately omitted from the verbatim block. The double-confirmed vagueness evidence is the `'bat'`/`'Is bald'` example pair quoted above.
- **A reference survey, not primary research.** Sennet reports and organizes the literature and flags where the field disagrees (e.g. the homonymy/polysemy line is "one traditional carving" and relatedness holds "in some hard to specify sense"). Treat the carving as a recognized frame, not a settled formal partition.
