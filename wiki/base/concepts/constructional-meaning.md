# Constructional meaning

**Stub.**

Construction Grammar (Goldberg 1995, 2006; Croft 2001; Fillmore et al.): the basic unit of grammatical knowledge is the **construction** — a conventional pairing of form and meaning, ranging from morphemes through idioms to argument-structure patterns (the ditransitive, the *way*-construction, the AANN ("a beautiful three days") pattern, etc.).

Why this is the project's sharpest wedge (§1 of charter):

- Constructions have meanings that are not just the sum of their parts' meanings (the partial idiomaticity is the point).
- Function words, inflection, and argument-structure constructions are under-theorized at the *meaning-theory* level — most semantics work focuses on content words.
- Constructions are unusually tractable to probe: minimal pairs across constructional variants give clean experimental contrasts.
- A growing CxG-probing literature (Weissweiler; Tayyar Madabushi; Scivetti) shows the line is alive but small enough to contribute to.

Operationalizes naturally through:

- Acceptability contrasts between licit and illicit instantiations of a construction.
- Inferential probes: does the model draw the construction-specific inferences (e.g., the "caused-motion" reading of the *way*-construction)?
- Cross-construction substitution: substituting an item from outside the construction's licit set; does behavior degrade in construction-appropriate ways?
