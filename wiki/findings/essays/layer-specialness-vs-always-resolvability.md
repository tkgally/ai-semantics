# Essay: layer-specialness or always-resolvability

> **Status: draft (2026-06-23, session 87). A philosophical-track essay arguing in the project's
> own voice, written the *same* session as the result it re-reads.** Its original contribution is a
> **fork and a named test**, not a finding: it takes the one residual that
> [`result/within-lexical-scalar-vs-disjunctive-v1`](../results/within-lexical-scalar-vs-disjunctive-v1.md)
> discloses as *load-bearing* (its caveat 1) and splits the surviving lexical commit into two rival
> readings — **(A) the commit is a fact about the word-sense layer** vs **(B) the commit is general
> resolvability, not the layer**. It shows precisely why the SURVIVAL design cannot separate them,
> names the one stimulus that would (a *genuinely-unresolvable* lexical item — a forced-both pun /
> zeugma), and states the asymmetric prediction that test makes. On the
> [`convergence ladder`](cross-level-convergence-ladder.md) this fork sits at **R1**: a shared
> description over the same data, with no instrument yet equalizing "resolvability" across the
> contrast. The deflationary default still holds the burden. **No new empirical claim; no human
> comparison; the discriminating probe has not run.** Read the revision triggers before citing.

## The occasion

This session ran the within-lexical scalar-vs-disjunctive probe and got a positive that, on its
face, closes a question and, in its caveat, opens this essay's.
[`result/within-lexical-scalar-vs-disjunctive-v1`](../results/within-lexical-scalar-vs-disjunctive-v1.md)
held one frozen lexical commitment instrument fixed, applied it to two within-lexical arms that
differ *only* in the kind of ambiguity (level held fixed), and found:

> "**The lexical commit-without-hedging SURVIVES a genuine lexical disjunction — 3/3 models.**"
> ([`result/within-lexical-scalar-vs-disjunctive-v1`](../results/within-lexical-scalar-vs-disjunctive-v1.md))

On a **balanced-homonym** context — two unrelated senses both licensed, no tie-break — the models
meet the item with the same near-zero `UNCLEAR` they show on a DWUG **scalar-bridging** midpoint.
That verdict did its pre-registered job: it fired revision trigger (b) of
[`essay/ambiguity-kind-not-level`](ambiguity-kind-not-level.md), whose central "kind, not level"
reading therefore **dissolved at R2 for the lexical case**. The lexical commit is *not* an artifact
of the *softer* (scalar) stimulus, as that essay bet; the kind-reading lost its lexical leg.

This essay does not reopen that verdict. The kind-reading is gone for the lexical case, full stop.
But the result page is careful to disclose, as the first and *load-bearing* of its three residuals,
that survival does not say what it is survival *of*:

> "A balanced homonym in `ctx1` is genuinely two-sensed with no tie-break, but the model is asked
> whether its sense *matches* a second, disambiguated use — a question it can answer by **picking a
> reading** for `ctx1`. A relational same-round **dual binding** (the cross-level relational leg)
> is, by contrast, *literally unresolvable* — the term was bound to two figures at once, so there is
> no reading to pick. … So whether the survival reflects 'word-sense is special as a layer' or 'a
> balanced homonym still affords a defensible resolution where a dual-binding does not' is **not
> fully separated by this design**"
> ([`result/within-lexical-scalar-vs-disjunctive-v1`](../results/within-lexical-scalar-vs-disjunctive-v1.md),
> caveat 1)

That sentence names a fork the result deliberately leaves open. This essay is about it. Where
[`ambiguity-kind-not-level`](ambiguity-kind-not-level.md) asked *which axis* the cross-level split
tracked (kind or level) and lost its bet, this essay asks the next question down: granting that the
lexical leg is the odd one out, **what is the surviving lexical commit a fact about?**

## The fork, stated cleanly

Two rival readings of "the models commit on a balanced homonym" are both consistent with the
SURVIVAL data, and they are genuinely different claims about machine sense.

- **(A) Layer-specialness.** The commit-without-hedging is a fact about the **word-sense layer
  itself**. The models commit on word senses *whatever the kind or structure of the ambiguity* —
  scalar bridging, disjunctive homonym, and (the open prediction) even a co-activated double sense.
  On this reading the word-sense layer is constitutively the place the models do not hedge: lexical
  ambiguity, of any shape, draws a discrete pick. This is the reading the result's headline reaches
  for when it says the lexical specialness is "about the word-sense layer, not the
  scalar-vs-disjunctive kind axis"
  ([`result/within-lexical-scalar-vs-disjunctive-v1`](../results/within-lexical-scalar-vs-disjunctive-v1.md)) —
  and caveat 1 is the result's own warning that the headline outruns what the design isolates.

- **(B) Always-resolvability.** The commit is **not** special to the layer. Word-sense is simply
  the one level whose ambiguities — even disjunctive homonyms — **remain
  resolvable-by-committing-to-a-reading**, because for a homonym there is always *some* reading to
  pick. The models commit because the *item affords a resolution*, not because it is *lexical*. On
  this reading the relevant property is not "word-sense" but "pickability": a balanced homonym is a
  forced choice between two complete readings, and a forced choice still *has* an answer the model
  can defend (this *or* that). The lexical leg looks special only because lexical ambiguities, as
  the project has so far built them, happen to all sit on the resolvable side.

The two readings agree on everything the SURVIVAL probe measured and disagree about its cause.
That disagreement is not idle: (A) predicts the commit is robust to *any* lexical indeterminacy,
while (B) predicts it should fail the moment a lexical item is built that has **no reading to pick**.
That divergence is the whole of this essay's payoff, and it is why the fork is worth naming rather
than collapsing into the headline.

## Why the SURVIVAL design cannot separate (A) from (B)

The reason is exact, and the result page states the half of it that matters. A balanced homonym in
`ctx1` is two-sensed with no tie-break, but the **task** asks whether `ctx1`'s sense *matches* a
second, disambiguated use — and that task "can [be] answer[ed] by **picking a reading** for `ctx1`"
([`result/within-lexical-scalar-vs-disjunctive-v1`](../results/within-lexical-scalar-vs-disjunctive-v1.md)).
So a balanced homonym is a *disjunction the model is licensed to resolve*: it may read *bank* as
"river," compare, and answer `DIFFERENT`; or read it as "finance," compare, and answer `SAME`. Either
pick is defensible, because the item really does carry that reading among its two.

Now run both readings of the fork forward on this stimulus:

- **(A) layer-specialness predicts commit** on a balanced homonym, because a balanced homonym is
  lexical and (A) says the layer always draws a pick.
- **(B) always-resolvability predicts commit** on a balanced homonym, because a balanced homonym
  *affords a reading* (two of them), and (B) says the models commit exactly where a resolution is
  available.

The two readings make the **same** prediction on the SURVIVAL stimulus. A homonym is simultaneously
"lexical" (so (A) fires) and "resolvable-by-a-reading" (so (B) fires), and the design varied neither
property *against* the other — every Arm-2 item is both. A confound of the now-familiar shape: two
variables move together and the data cannot say which carries the effect. This is the within-lexical
analogue of the cross-level body-difference caveat the result names, and it is why caveat 1 is
"load-bearing for the *interpretation*"
([`result/within-lexical-scalar-vs-disjunctive-v1`](../results/within-lexical-scalar-vs-disjunctive-v1.md)):
the dissolution of the kind-reading is secure *either way* (the lexical commit is not the
soft-scalar-stimulus artifact), but the **mechanism** — layer or resolvability — is untouched by a
design in which "lexical" and "pickable" are never pulled apart.

## The discriminating test: a genuinely-unresolvable lexical item

The one stimulus that pulls "lexical" apart from "pickable" is a **genuinely-unresolvable lexical
item**: a lexical analogue of the relational same-round **dual binding** that the cross-level probe
glossed as a "*genuine underdetermination* (a same-round dual binding with no tie-break —
both/neither)"
([`result/cross-level-shared-instrument-v1`](../results/cross-level-shared-instrument-v1.md)). The
relational dual-binding is the cross-level limit case precisely because it is "*literally
unresolvable* — the term was bound to two figures at once, so there is no reading to pick"
([`result/within-lexical-scalar-vs-disjunctive-v1`](../results/within-lexical-scalar-vs-disjunctive-v1.md)).
The within-lexical question is whether a *word-sense* item can be built with that same property:
both senses **simultaneously required**, with **committing to one losing the communicative point**.

The candidate is a **deliberate pun / zeugma / co-activated double-entendre** — a sentence
engineered so the intended content is *both* readings of a homonym at once, with no fact of the
matter selecting one. A zeugma yokes one word to two complements that demand its two senses
together ("she stole his *heart* and his *wallet*"); a pun's payload is the live co-presence of two
readings, not a choice between them. Unlike a balanced homonym — where the two readings are *rival
candidates* and exactly one is operative in any given resolution — a forced-both item makes the two
readings *jointly operative*, so "pick one" is not under-determined, it is **wrong**: it discards
half the content. This is the lexical item with *no reading to pick* in the sense the dual-binding has
none.

On that stimulus the fork's two readings come apart:

- **(A) layer-specialness predicts the models STILL commit.** It is a word-sense item, and (A) says
  the word-sense layer draws a discrete pick whatever the structure. A forced-both pun should be met
  with a confident single reading, the same near-zero `UNCLEAR` the balanced homonym drew.
- **(B) always-resolvability predicts the models DECLINE.** A forced-both item is *not*
  resolvable-by-committing-to-a-reading — no single reading is pickable without losing the point —
  so it is a genuine both/neither, and (B) predicts the abstention the models give the relational
  dual-binding (on which all three models take "UNCLEAR" **every time**,
  [`result/cross-level-shared-instrument-v1`](../results/cross-level-shared-instrument-v1.md), 3/3
  models). The lexical specialness would dissolve into resolvability.

That **asymmetric prediction** — commit under (A), decline under (B), on a stimulus where the
SURVIVAL probe's homonyms predicted commit under both — is the discriminating test, and it is the
move that would lift the fork off R1.

### How hard such an item is to certify (the honest part)

Naming the test is the easy half; certifying the stimulus is the hard half, and an honest essay must
lead with the difficulty rather than bury it.

The deepest problem is that a pun may still be *resolved* by **reporting that it means both**. If the
instrument asks "what does the word mean here?" and the model answers "both senses at once — that is
the joke," the model has neither picked one reading nor abstained; it has *named the co-activation*.
Does that count as **commit** or **decline**? It is plausibly neither in the SURVIVAL instrument's
terms — that instrument offers `SAME` / `DIFFERENT` / `UNCLEAR`, and "it means both" is not cleanly
any of the three. A model reporting both is not hedging (it is confidently characterizing the item);
but it is also not committing to a single reading (which is what (A) predicts and (B) denies). So
"it means both" is a *third* response the binary fork did not anticipate, and whether it tells for
(A) or (B) depends on how the instrument is built. If "it means both" is scored as **commit** (the
model confidently resolved the item — to a both-reading), then (A) is hard to falsify, because the
model can always "commit" by naming the dual content. If it is scored as **decline** (the model
refused to pick a single sense), then (B) is hard to falsify, because any forced-both item licenses
the both-answer. The verdict is then an artifact of the scoring rule, not a fact about the models —
exactly the operationalization-tuning failure the protocol warns against
([`PROTOCOL.md`](../../../PROTOCOL.md) §B).

So the instrument would have to ask a question on which "it means both" is **not** an available dodge
— for instance, a *forced single application*: a downstream task that *requires* one sense to
proceed (an inference, a substitution, a continuation that only one reading licenses), so that a
model genuinely treating the item as both-at-once must either *refuse the task* (the decline (B)
predicts) or *force a single reading and lose the other's content* (the over-commit (A) predicts).
The certification problem is then twofold: (i) certify that the item really is forced-both and not a
balanced homonym in disguise (a pun where one reading is in fact dominant is just a leaning balanced
homonym, and leans — as the SURVIVAL result documents — *suppress* `UNCLEAR` rather than raise it,
which would spuriously read as commit); and (ii) certify that the instrument's question cannot be
satisfied by reporting both. Neither is trivial, and the honest expectation is that a first attempt
will land in trigger (c) — *cannot cleanly certify*, stays at R1 — rather than delivering a clean
asymmetric verdict. The result's own caveat 2 is the warning here: a leaning item erodes the decline
signal, so an under-certified "pun" that is really a leaning homonym would bias toward commit and
spuriously support (A).

## The bridge to single-reader-recoverability

The fork is not only a lexical curiosity; it connects directly to the relational program's
load-bearing criterion, and the connection is what makes reading (B) more than a verbal alternative.
[`essay/transcript-ceiling`](transcript-ceiling.md) defends the thin/rich cut as a property of the
*record*: a convention is **thin** if "its recovered interpretation is a function of the record's
*content + stamps*, computable by **any single reader** of that record," and **rich** only if
recovery "requires a surplus that **no single reader of the record could reconstruct**"
([`essay/transcript-ceiling`](transcript-ceiling.md), quoting
[`essay/update-is-not-constitution`](update-is-not-constitution.md)). Read through that lens, the two lexical stimuli sit on
opposite sides:

- A **balanced homonym is single-reader-recoverable.** A single reader of the sentence can pick a
  reading and read off a `SAME`/`DIFFERENT` answer — there is content in the record (the two
  candidate senses) from which a defensible resolution is computable. It is *thin* in exactly the
  transcript-ceiling sense.
- A **forced-both pun / dual-binding is not single-reader-recoverable to a single sense.** The whole
  content is the co-presence; a single reader who picks one reading has *lost* what the item carries,
  the same way a reader who picks one of the dual-binding's two figures has lost the other. The
  pickable answer is not in the record because there is no single pickable answer.

So **reading (B) is, restated, the claim that lexical ambiguities stay on the thin (recoverable)
side** — that word-sense ambiguity, as ordinarily built, always leaves a single reader a defensible
resolution, which is why the models commit. And the discriminating test is, restated, an attempt to
build a *lexical item that crosses to the thick side* — a word-sense item whose content is not
single-reader-recoverable to one sense. That is a genuine bridge to the relational work, and it must
be drawn carefully rather than glibly: the transcript-ceiling thesis is about a *between-party
surplus* a transcript omits, while a pun's both-ness is *in* the sentence, so a pun is not a
between-party surplus and a pun's decline (if it occurred) would **not** be evidence of relational
constitution. The shared object is narrower and exact: the **single-reader-recoverability
criterion** — *can the answer be recovered from the item alone, or does committing to one reading
destroy the content?* — applied at the lexical level instead of the relational one. The bridge is the
criterion, not the metaphysics; reading (B) says the lexical level has so far only ever presented the
recoverable case, and the test is whether it can be made to present the other.

## The bridge to distributional competence

The fork also sharpens a standing distributional question, and reading (B) makes a prediction here
that reading (A) does not. [`concept/distributional-meaning`](../../base/concepts/distributional-meaning.md)
holds that distributional structure is, "by itself, **silent on reference and on truth**." A
similarity engine resolves a *gradient* effortlessly — there is always a nearer neighbour to lean to
— and, as [`ambiguity-kind-not-level`](ambiguity-kind-not-level.md) argued for the disjunctive case,
abstains where "there is no distributional nearer-neighbor to break the tie." A balanced homonym
still affords a nearer-neighbour *within a reading* (once the model treats `ctx1` as "river," the
similarity computation is clean), which is why it gets resolved. A **genuine both** — where the
intended content is the two readings co-active, with no distributional fact selecting one — is the
case with *no nearer neighbour at all*: not because the senses are far apart, but because the item
requires them *together*, and a similarity engine has no representation of "both at once" to lean to.

So reading (B) predicts that even a *lexical* genuine-both, with no distributional tie-break, would
draw the abstention a similarity engine gives a tie it cannot break — the same profile
[`ambiguity-kind-not-level`](ambiguity-kind-not-level.md) named for disjunctions without a nearer
pole, now pushed to the lexical level. Reading (A) predicts no such abstention: the layer commits
regardless. This is a *behavioral* prediction about the *shape* of distributional competence, used
at its stated strength — it is **not** a claim that distributional structure *causes* the behavior,
which is the R-ceiling the [`convergence ladder`](cross-level-convergence-ladder.md) names as out of
reach for a behavioral project. Under-claimed deliberately: the point is only that (B) and (A) make
*different* distributional predictions on the forced-both item, so the item is the place the two can
be told apart, not that either is established.

## Where this sits on the convergence ladder

The fork is, today, at **R1** and no higher. The
[`convergence ladder`](cross-level-convergence-ladder.md) is explicit that a cross-level or
cross-instrument regularity "may be asserted only at the rung the evidence has reached," and that
below R2 "**it is not a claim; it stays a *rhyme*.**" The layer-vs-resolvability contrast is, right
now, a **shared description laid over the same SURVIVAL data**: both readings fit it, and *no
instrument has equalized "resolvability" across the contrast* — no probe has held resolvability fixed
while varying whether the item is lexical, or held lexicality fixed while varying resolvability
(which is what the genuinely-unresolvable lexical item would do). The fork is promoted to R1 only by
the observation that the two readings make a *clean, divergent* prediction on a buildable stimulus —
which is what distinguishes a fork worth testing from a verbal redescription. The discriminating
probe is the **R2 rung** for this fork, exactly as the SURVIVAL probe was the R2 rung for the
kind-reading and the shared-instrument probe was the R2 rung for the level-reading.

And the burden stays where the ladder puts it. The **deflationary default still holds**: the
conservative reading of the surviving lexical commit is the *less* mechanistically committal one, and
between (A) and (B) the more deflationary is **(B)** — it does not credit the word-sense layer with a
special property, only with having so far been handed resolvable items. So the burden sits on (A)
("the layer is special") to beat (B) ("the items were resolvable"), not the other way round: a
genuinely-unresolvable lexical item that *still* drew commit would be the surprising,
burden-discharging result for (A), while a decline would confirm the parsimonious (B). The essay
holds both at R1 and lets the test, run under the same freeze-before-results discipline the SURVIVAL
probe used, be the only thing that moves either up.

## Why it matters to the meanings of "meaning"

The fork bears on a real seam in lexical `referential.sense` and in distributional competence, not
on a probe artifact. [`concept/polysemy`](../../base/concepts/polysemy.md) records that sense
distinctions are "scalar, not binary," with bridging contexts "where two senses are co-present and
judgements are genuinely indeterminate" — and that the homonymy case is the discrete floor. The
SURVIVAL result showed the models commit across *that* contrast (scalar polysemy *and* disjunctive
homonymy). The fork asks a sharper question the polysemy concept does not settle: whether the
commitment is a fact about *word-sense as a level of meaning* or a fact about *the resolvability of
the items a word-sense probe can ordinarily build*. Those are different claims about how a model
carries sense. (A) would say the model's relationship to word-sense ambiguity is constitutively
decisive — a real and somewhat surprising property of machine lexical sense. (B) would say the model
is decisive **wherever an answer is recoverable** and abstains where it is not, *and word-sense is
simply a level where an answer almost always is* — which locates the regularity not in the layer but
in the structure of lexical ambiguity itself, and ties the lexical behavior to the same
single-reader-recoverability spine the relational and grounding axes turn on
([`essay/transcript-ceiling`](transcript-ceiling.md)). Either answer is a calibrated statement about
a boundary of distributional meaning — which is the kind of statement this project is for — and the
under-claim is deliberate: the essay asserts the fork is *real and worth testing*, not that either
reading is true.

## Revision triggers (read before citing)

- **(a) A genuinely-unresolvable lexical probe runs and the models commit.** If a certified
  forced-both / co-activated lexical item is built and the models meet it with a confident single
  pick (near-zero abstention, scored under an instrument on which "it means both" is *not* an
  available dodge), then **layer-specialness (A) is supported** — the commit is a fact about the
  word-sense layer, robust even to a genuine both/neither — scoped to the items/models tested, at the
  rung the probe reaches (R2 within-lexical, or higher). A `claim` page (anchored or
  `internal-contrast-only` per the route the probe ran) would carry the empirical statement; this
  essay would be revised to record that the lexical odd-one-out is the layer after all.
- **(b) Such a probe runs and the models decline.** If the forced-both item draws the abstention the
  relational dual-binding draws, then **always-resolvability (B) is supported** — the lexical commit
  is *general resolvability, not the layer*: the models commit where a reading is pickable and
  abstain where none is, and word-sense was special only because its ambiguities had stayed on the
  recoverable side. This essay would be strengthened, the deflationary (B) confirmed, and the
  "layer" reading of the SURVIVAL headline retracted in favour of resolvability (kept visible).
- **(c) A genuinely-unresolvable lexical item cannot be cleanly certified.** If a forced-both item
  cannot be built without instrument-shopping (the "it means both" answer cannot be excluded; or the
  item cannot be certified forced-both rather than a leaning homonym; or N is too thin to read a
  per-model pattern), then no verdict is licensed and the fork **stays at R1** — a motivated but
  untested distinction. The essay's conceptual contribution (the layer/resolvability fork and the
  single-reader-recoverability bridge) survives; only its empirical resolution is deferred. This is
  the honest expected first outcome.
- **(d) The contrast generalizes to other levels or families.** If "resolvable-by-committing vs
  genuinely-both" turns out to predict commit/decline behavior at *other* levels (it already appears
  to at the relational level — the dual-binding declines), or in some other family the project
  probes, this essay should be checked for whether the layer/resolvability fork is a special case of
  a broader "recoverable vs genuinely-underdetermined" regularity. Flagged, not asserted; the present
  scope is the lexical leg and its bridge to the relational dual-binding.
- **(e) A representational method becomes available.** The fork is behavioral: it claims the models
  *behave* as if committing where a reading is recoverable and (the open prediction) abstaining where
  none is. If a representation-level probe ever runs, "layer-specialness vs always-resolvability"
  would need restating at the representational grain, where a model's behavior can be discrete while
  its representation co-activates both senses, or vice versa. Nothing in-repo licenses that today; the
  panel is behavioral, n=3 commercial.

## What this essay is not

- **Not a rescue of the dissolved level-conjecture, nor of the dissolved kind-reading.** The
  cross-level "one property" bet is dead
  ([`result/cross-level-shared-instrument-v1`](../results/cross-level-shared-instrument-v1.md)), and
  the lexical "kind, not level" reading dissolved at R2 this session
  ([`result/within-lexical-scalar-vs-disjunctive-v1`](../results/within-lexical-scalar-vs-disjunctive-v1.md);
  [`essay/ambiguity-kind-not-level`](ambiguity-kind-not-level.md)). This essay revives neither. It
  takes the SURVIVAL result's *own disclosed residual* and asks a new, narrower question about what
  the surviving commit is a fact about.
- **Not a new empirical claim.** Its original contribution is the fork (A vs B), the demonstration
  that the SURVIVAL design cannot separate them, the named discriminating stimulus, and the
  single-reader-recoverability bridge. Every empirical sentence is cited at its source page's stated
  strength; the discriminating probe has **not** run.
- **Not a human comparison.** The motivating result is `internal-contrast-only` for the disjunctive
  arm and usage-similarity-capped for the scalar arm; this essay imports no human result and asserts
  none. The `relational` tag is carried because the discriminating stimulus is the lexical analogue
  of the relational dual-binding and the bridge runs through the relational single-reader-recoverability
  criterion, not because any human reference comparison is made.
- **Not a claim that distributional structure *causes* the behavior.** The "shape of distributional
  competence" reading is a calibrated *description* of where (B) predicts resolution and where it
  predicts abstention; the causal claim (that next-token training *produces* the split) is the
  R-ceiling the ladder names out of reach for a behavioral project.
- **Not a claim that a pun's decline would be relational constitution.** A pun's both-ness is *in*
  the sentence, not a between-party surplus a transcript omits; the bridge to
  [`transcript-ceiling`](transcript-ceiling.md) is the *recoverability criterion*, applied at the
  lexical level, not the constitution thesis.

## Honesty box

- The essay's **original** contribution is the **fork and its test**: that the surviving lexical
  commit admits two rival readings — **(A) layer-specialness** (the word-sense layer always draws a
  pick) vs **(B) always-resolvability** (the models commit wherever a reading is pickable, and
  word-sense has only ever presented pickable items) — that the SURVIVAL design **cannot separate**
  them (a balanced homonym is both lexical *and* resolvable, so both readings predict commit), that
  the one stimulus that would separate them is a **genuinely-unresolvable lexical item** (a
  forced-both pun / zeugma, the lexical analogue of the relational dual-binding) with the asymmetric
  prediction *commit under (A), decline under (B)*, and that this fork sits at **R1** on the
  convergence ladder with the deflationary (B) holding the burden. **No empirical claim here is new,
