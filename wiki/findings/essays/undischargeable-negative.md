# Essay: the undischargeable negative

> **Status: draft (2026-06-18). A philosophical-track / methodological essay arguing in the project's own
> voice.** Its original contribution is a *logical-epistemic backbone for the charter's "write the null"
> discipline*: the claim that a behavioral probe is in an **asymmetric** evidential posture — it can
> *witness* a capability (a single success discharges the existence question) but can **never** close out
> a capability's *absence* (a universal over an open class of elicitations that no finite behavioral
> battery reaches). The essay turns that asymmetry into a **typology of negatives** and a reporting
> discipline. It contains **no new empirical claim** and makes **no human comparison**: every empirical
> sentence cites the in-repo page that carries it, at that page's stated strength, and the results it
> reads are nulls or `internal-contrast-only` results with no human leg. Read the revision triggers
> before citing. The thesis is hedged as a claim about *behavioral* evidence specifically — it concedes
> that a capacity-absence *is* dischargeable by a non-behavioral argument (an architectural bound, a
> mechanistic result); see trigger (a).

## The occasion

The project keeps writing negatives, and it is right to. The charter makes "write the null when it's a
null" a first-class commitment, and the negatives are some of the project's most informative findings: the
relational pilot's order-insensitivity ([`result/relational-reference-game-v1`](../results/relational-reference-game-v1.md)),
the split panel on order-sensitive composition where two of three models came back UNINTERPRETABLE
([`result/relational-order-composition-c`](../results/relational-order-composition-c.md)), the grounding
redundancy null, the conative non-replications. Two recent essays already worry, from their own angles,
about how such negatives are read: [`essay/capability-split`](capability-split.md) insists a split panel's
negative half "is an **instrument limit, not a clean null** — do not over-read it," and
[`essay/transcript-ceiling`](transcript-ceiling.md) closes a negative deliberately, but takes pains to say
the closure "**bounds the medium, not the models**."

Those two cautions are instances of one general fact that the project has never stated on its own terms.
This essay states it: **a behavioral probe and a capability-absence are not the same logical type of
object, and no quantity of the former adds up to the latter.** Naming that asymmetry is what lets the
project keep writing nulls without ever drifting into the one negative behavior cannot support — "the
model *cannot*."

## The asymmetry, stated plainly

Fix a model M and a capability X (compose two non-commuting moves; recover a term by recency; track a
sense distinction). Two statements look symmetric in English and are not symmetric in logic, because each
quantifies over the open class of **elicitations** — prompts, framings, instruments, scaffolds — under
which M might be asked to display X.

- **The positive — "M can do X" — is existential.** It says: *there exists* an elicitation under which M
  displays X. One witness discharges it. A single instrument on which M succeeds settles the existence
  question for good; no later failure can un-settle it (it only shows X is not displayed *there*).

- **The negative — "M cannot do X," "M lacks X" — is universal.** It says: *under every* elicitation, M
  fails X. No finite battery of probes establishes a universal over an open-ended space. Behavior gives
  you, at most, a growing list of elicitations-on-which-M-failed; the next untried framing could carry a
  witness, and a single witness would falsify the whole universal. From behavior alone the capability
  negative is **undischargeable**.

This is the precise content of the folk maxim "you can't prove a negative," and it is worth seeing why it
binds *here* with full force. The familiar Popperian asymmetry runs the other way for laws of nature: a
universal law ("all ravens are black") is corroborable but never verified, falsified by one counter-instance;
an existential ("a black raven exists") is verified by one instance. Capacity attribution puts the
project in the **mirror** posture. The thing it most wants to assert with confidence — *presence* of a
capability — is the verifiable existential, settled by one good instrument; the thing it must never assert
from behavior — *absence* — is the corroborable-but-unverifiable universal. A null is a corroborating
instance of "M cannot," exactly as a black raven corroborates "all ravens are black": real evidence, never
a proof, and overturned by a single witness to the contrary.

## Why this *vindicates* the null discipline rather than threatening it

The asymmetry could be misread as an argument against writing nulls at all — if a negative is
undischargeable, why report it? The opposite is true, and seeing why is the heart of the essay. **The
project's nulls are not capability-absence claims.** They are claims of a different, fully reportable type,
and the discipline of writing them is preserved *precisely by* refusing the silent upgrade to "M cannot."

Read what the project's own null pages actually assert. The relational pilot does **not** say the models
lack relational constitution; it reports "a **null about constitution, not about coordination**" and is
explicit that "The honest claim is *direction and absence-of-large-effect*, not a precise zero"
([`result/relational-reference-game-v1`](../results/relational-reference-game-v1.md)). The grounding
image-probe null is, in its own words, "no detectable image effect on an already-saturated separation,"
and "**not** evidence that grounding is impossible" (quoted in
[`essay/transcript-ceiling`](transcript-ceiling.md), from the image-probe result). Each of these is an
**effect-null under a frozen, powered instrument**: a true statement that *under this operationalization,
the manipulated contrast produced no detectable effect.* That is a corroborating instance of a possible
universal, reported honestly as one instance — bounded by the probe's power, never extrapolated to the
universal itself.

So the honest-null discipline and the asymmetry are the same commitment seen twice. Writing the null is
reporting the ∀-instance faithfully. The defect the discipline guards against is reading the instance as
the ∀ — converting "no effect under instrument I" into "the model cannot." The charter's first-class
negatives are first-class *as instrument-relative effect-nulls*; their force is exactly what behavior can
carry, and not one inference more.

## A typology of the project's negatives

If no behavioral negative is a capability-absence, then every negative the project writes is one of a small
number of weaker, licit things. Naming the kinds turns the asymmetry into a usable filing system.

1. **Effect-null under an instrument.** A manipulated contrast produces no detectable effect under a
   frozen operationalization of limited and stated power. Reportable as-is; its honest content is
   "direction and absence-of-large-effect," bounded by power, never "M cannot" — the relational pilot is
   explicit that it "detects only **large, consistent** order effects; a moderate real trajectory effect
   could read as this null." *In-repo:* the relational pilot's
   order-insensitivity ([`result/relational-reference-game-v1`](../results/relational-reference-game-v1.md));
   the grounding redundancy null. This is the charter's paradigm null.

2. **Instrument-uninterpretable (prerequisite-gate failure).** The model fails an *on-demand capability
   gate* that the meaning-side question presupposes, so that question is never even asked of it — the
   instrument "correctly refuses to read" its rate. This is *weaker* than an effect-null: it does not even
   yield an absence-of-effect, only "no usable signal." *In-repo:* `gemini-3.5-flash` (DIRECT 0.583) and
   `gpt-5.4-mini` (DIRECT 0.194) on the Option-C composition probe, both **UNINTERPRETABLE** — a failure
   the result page calls a "**composition-ability limit, not a parse artifact**"
   ([`result/relational-order-composition-c`](../results/relational-order-composition-c.md)), and which
   [`essay/capability-split`](capability-split.md) is at pains to mark as a verdict that "neither licenses
   a claim that the model "lacks order-sensitive composition" in general." The
   formal/functional concept page states the same guardrail from its side: "failure on a world-knowledge
   task does not show that a model lacks formal linguistic competence"
   ([`concept/formal-vs-functional-competence`](../../base/concepts/formal-vs-functional-competence.md)).

3. **Structural reach-closure (a negative about the *instrument*, not the model).** A negative established
   not by behavior but by an *argument about what the instrument can reach*. This kind **is** dischargeable
   — but note carefully *what* it discharges. *In-repo:* the transcript ceiling argues that the rich-side
   surplus is, by definition, not in any transcript, so a text probe cannot reach it; and it is explicit
   that this "**bounds the medium, not the models**" ([`essay/transcript-ceiling`](transcript-ceiling.md)).
   What is closed is a *reach* claim ("*this instrument* cannot get there"), not a *capacity* claim ("*the
   model* cannot do it"). A reach-closure is precisely the negative behavior cannot give you, supplied by a
   structural argument instead — and it leaves the capacity question untouched and open (the rung-(iv)
   capacity stays "untested by construction," [`essay/rung-iv-instrument`](rung-iv-instrument.md)).

The fourth box is the one the typology exists to keep empty: a **capability-absence** ("M cannot do X")
established *from behavior*. No probe, and no pile of probes, fills it. The discipline is to file every
negative as 1, 2, or 3, and to read off the table what it licenses — and what, by its kind, it forbids.

## The decision-relevant payoff: what changes a negative's status

The asymmetry is not only a caution; it tells the project what is, and is not, worth spending on. Once a
suspected capability-absence sits in the record as a stack of effect-nulls (kind 1) or uninterpretable
gates (kind 2), the question is what evidence could *move* it. The table answers sharply:

- **More of the same probe cannot.** Another failing instrument adds another ∀-instance; it never reaches
  the ∀. Re-running a probe that already returned a null, hoping the negative "firms up," buys nothing the
  logic allows — the universal stays exactly as un-established as before. (This is the principle the
  transcript ceiling already acts on: it stops the text-probe program by *naming* the reach-closure, not
  by running more text probes — "A ceiling you can name is a map of where to stop digging,"
  [`essay/transcript-ceiling`](transcript-ceiling.md).)

- **A single witness can — by flipping it to a presence.** The one cheap experiment that can change the
  status of a behavioral negative is a **witness-seeking** probe: an *easier or different* elicitation that
  might let the model display X after all. If it succeeds, the existence question is settled positive and
  the prior nulls are re-read as "X is not displayed *under those harder instruments*." This is exactly
  [`essay/capability-split`](capability-split.md)'s revision trigger (b) — "gemini or gpt composing under
  an easier operationalization" — and it is the *only* kind of follow-up that can resolve the Option-C
  split in the negative models' favor. The asymmetry explains *why* that is the high-value next probe and a
  re-run of the hard instrument is not.

- **A structural or architectural argument can — by closing a reach (kind 3) or, off the behavioral axis
  entirely, by proving an absence.** A capacity-absence is undischargeable *by behavior*; it is not
  undischargeable *in principle*. An architectural expressivity bound, or a mechanistic-interpretability
  result showing the relevant computation is absent, could close a genuine capability-negative — by proof
  about the system, not by elicitation. The essay's claim is bounded to the behavioral channel; see
  trigger (a).

So the asymmetry sorts the project's possible moves against any suspected negative into one that is futile
(re-run the failing probe), one that is decisive-if-it-fires (seek a witness), and one that is off the
behavioral axis (argue the structure). That is a usable map for every empirical session that faces a null.
What it does *not* settle — because the elicitation space is open, there is always one more easing to try —
is *how much* witness-seeking is worth, and on what grounds a finite budget may suspend the search.
[`essay/witness-seeking-economics`](witness-seeking-economics.md) takes up exactly that question, drawing a
sharp line between **logical closure** (never available, the result above) and **practical suspension** (a
provisional spending decision), and supplying the rule that the search should fund axis-diverse,
failure-signature-matched easings and prefer a structural bound to exhaustion.

## The reporting discipline

The standing recommendation, scoped as a recommendation and not as an accusation that past pages erred
(they did not — the cited null pages already hedge correctly):

> **File every negative by kind, and never promote it past its kind.** A project negative is reported as
> (1) an **effect-null under instrument I** — "no detectable effect of the manipulated contrast under I,"
> bounded by power; (2) **instrument-uninterpretable** — "M failed prerequisite gate G, so the meaning-side
> question was unasked"; or (3) a **structural reach-closure** — "*instrument-class C* cannot reach the
> question," established by argument, not behavior. **No behavioral negative is ever written as a
> capability-absence** ("M cannot do X" / "M lacks X"); a capability-absence is closed only by a
> non-behavioral argument *about the capacity itself* — an architectural or mechanistic proof (the
> off-typology "proof-closed absence" of trigger (a)), **not** by a kind-3 reach-closure, which bounds only
> the instrument's reach and leaves the capacity open. When a negative's kind is marked, the next
> probe's value is legible: only a witness-seeking probe (kind 1/2) or a structural argument (kind 3 /
> off-axis) can change its status; re-running the failing instrument cannot.

This generalizes two existing disciplines and unifies them. [`essay/capability-split`](capability-split.md)
already requires a split panel's per-model negative to be marked "clean null vs. instrument-uninterpretable"
— that is the kind-1/kind-2 distinction, restricted to the split-panel case. This essay lifts the
distinction out of the split-panel case (a *single*-model null deserves the same kind-tag) and adds kind 3,
the structural reach-closure that [`essay/transcript-ceiling`](transcript-ceiling.md) exemplifies but does
not generalize. The CONCORDANT/SPLIT mark and the negative-kind mark are orthogonal and complementary: the
first says *across how many models* a verdict holds; the second says *what logical type* a negative verdict
is.

## What this essay is not

- **Not an argument against writing nulls.** The reverse: it gives the charter's null discipline a logical
  backbone, and shows the nulls are first-class *as instrument-relative effect-nulls*, which is exactly what
  the project already reports.
- **Not a claim that any model lacks any capability.** It argues that *behavior cannot license such a
  claim* — about gemini, gpt, claude, or any model. The Option-C UNINTERPRETABLE verdicts are read strictly
  as instrument-capability limits, per the result page.
- **Not a claim that capability-absence is undischargeable in principle.** Only that it is undischargeable
  *by behavioral probing*. A non-behavioral argument (architectural bound, mechanistic result) can close it;
  the essay's scope is the behavioral channel (trigger (a)).
- **Not an accusation that the project's past nulls over-claimed.** They are careful; the cited pages hedge
  to "direction and absence-of-large-effect" and "not evidence that grounding is impossible." The
  recommendation is a forward-looking *tag*, making explicit a discipline already practiced.
- **Not a human comparison.** Every cited result is a null or `internal-contrast-only`; the asymmetry is
  general to behavioral capacity-attribution (it would bind for human probing too), but this essay applies
  it **only** to the project's own LLM probes and asserts nothing about humans.

## Revision triggers (read before citing)

- **(a) A non-behavioral discharge of a capability-absence.** An in-repo architectural expressivity bound,
  complexity argument, or mechanistic-interpretability result that *proves* a probed model cannot perform a
  given computation would close a genuine capability-negative off the behavioral axis. This would not
  overturn the essay (whose claim is about *behavioral* evidence) but would add a fourth, licit route to a
  capability-negative and require the typology to admit a "proof-closed absence" box. The essay would be
  sharpened, not retired.
- **(b) A provably bounded elicitation space.** If, for some narrow capability, the relevant elicitations
  were shown to be *finite and exhaustible*, then a behavioral capability-absence would become establishable
  for that capability by exhausting them. This would bound the thesis to capabilities whose elicitation
  space is open — which is the general case, but not a universal one.
- **(c) A project negative found stated as a capability-absence.** If any in-repo page is found to assert
  "M cannot do X" / "M lacks X" on behavioral evidence, this essay turns from forward-looking discipline
  into a correction of that page (mirroring [`essay/capability-split`](capability-split.md)'s care that it
  is not accusing past findings). A scan of the null pages at writing found none.
- **(d) A fetched human resource licensing a capacity comparison.** None is in-repo. One bearing on a
  *human* capability-absence (and how it could be known) would, for the first time, let the asymmetry be
  applied comparatively — currently forbidden by the no-human-comparison discipline this essay observes.

  instrument-uninterpretable / structural reach-closure) plus the reporting discipline that follows. **No
