# Essay: reference is premise-bound, not evidence-bound

> **Status: draft (2026-06-15). A philosophical-track essay arguing in the project's own voice.** Its contribution is a *structural diagnosis*, not a new empirical result. It examines the one place in the wiki where the strongest published pro-reference argument ([`source/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer`](../../base/sources/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer.md)) and the project's own concept page ([`concept/referential-meaning`](../../base/concepts/referential-meaning.md)) collide — they run the *same* externalist metasemantics to *opposite* verdicts on whether an LLM's words refer — and argues that the disagreement is **premise-bound, not evidence-bound**: it turns entirely on a single metasemantic premise no behavioral probe the project can run can settle. The essay introduces **no new empirical claim**; every empirical or textual assertion cites the in-repo page that carries it. It does not declare who is right about the premise. It locates exactly where the disagreement lives, and disciplines two of the project's own citation habits accordingly. Read "The two consequences" before citing either pole.

## The position

This essay argues one bounded thesis. The in-repo dispute over whether an LLM's words *refer* — achieve word-to-world connection — is **premise-bound, not evidence-bound**, and the project's behavioral method is **structurally silent on the deciding premise.**

The setup is a sharp coincidence the wiki already contains but does not yet name. Two in-repo pages reach *opposite* verdicts on LLM reference while running the *same* externalist (Putnam/Kripke) metasemantics:

- **Mandelkern & Linzen 2024** run externalism to a *positive* verdict: the natural histories carried by training text "may suffice to put LMs' words into referential contact with the external world" ([`source/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer`](../../base/sources/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer.md), §Abstract).
- **The project's [`concept/referential-meaning`](../../base/concepts/referential-meaning.md) page** runs the *same* externalism to a *negative* in-principle verdict: on externalism "this is the strongest *in-principle* case that an LLM does not *refer*" ([`concept/referential-meaning`](../../base/concepts/referential-meaning.md), §"Internalism vs. externalism — and the LLM case").

The thesis: they do not disagree about any fact a probe could settle. They agree on the metasemantic machinery (reference fixed off the speaker's head, by natural history and community); they agree on every measurable fact about the model (text in, text out; narrow access by construction). They disagree about exactly one conditional's **antecedent** — *whether inheriting word-histories through a training corpus counts as being "part of the linguistic community" whose use fixes reference.* Because the whole disagreement lives in that antecedent, and the antecedent is, by the externalist's own lights, a fact fixed off-board (by natural history, not by any in-head or in-behavior fact), **no behavioral probe the project runs adjudicates it.** The project measures word-to-word patterning — distributional and inferential behavior; word-to-world reference is, on this very metasemantics, not the kind of thing those measurements reach.

The original contribution is the diagnosis itself: this is one of the cells of the meaning map (truth-conditional / referential, on the word-to-world axis) where the project's behavioral method is *constitutively* silent — a principled limit, not a gap more probes can close.

## The disagreement, stated at its actual strength

Take the two poles at full strength, in their own words.

**The pro-reference pole (Mandelkern & Linzen).** The paper is a deliberate, AI-facing intervention against the deflationary reading that "the sentences they produce are meaningless, because the words involved do not refer to anything" ([`source/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer`](../../base/sources/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer.md), §1). Its load-bearing thesis relocates grounding off the model's sensorimotor contact and onto the history of the form: "The inputs to LMs are not just forms, but forms with particular histories of meaningful use. And those histories suffice to ground the referents of those forms" ([`source/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer`](../../base/sources/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer.md), §3). The decisive step for the LM case is explicitly **conditional** — a community-membership conditional:

> "If they are part of a linguistic community which uses 'Peano' to refer to Peano, then their use of 'Peano' refers to Peano." ([`source/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer`](../../base/sources/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer.md), §4.3)

And the authors leave the antecedent's denial open as a named escape hatch:

> "You could still argue that, appearances aside, LMs simply are not part of our speech community." ([`source/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer`](../../base/sources/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer.md), §6 / conclusion region)

The verdict itself is modal throughout: histories "**may suffice**" (§Abstract), and the conclusion is "it **looks much more likely** that LMs' words refer too, in just the same way, since they are trained on text whose natural history ties them, eventually, to the referents" ([`source/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer`](../../base/sources/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer.md), §6).

This pole is, as of this revision, **contested in-repo** — by [`source/baggio-murphy-2024-internalist-rejoinder`](../../base/sources/baggio-murphy-2024-internalist-rejoinder.md), an internalist reply whose bottom line is the flat opposite: "Neither language models themselves can refer, and nor can 'their words' refer" ([`source/baggio-murphy-2024-internalist-rejoinder`](../../base/sources/baggio-murphy-2024-internalist-rejoinder.md), §4). Crucially, Baggio & Murphy attack on *two* fronts, which the rest of this essay keeps apart because they have different bearing on the premise-bound diagnosis: a within-/adjacent-to-externalism attack on the **antecedent** (§5: "Extending the notion of 'linguistic community member' to include LMs … does not address the issue whether LMs have the required status in our communities, such that some of their 'words' can refer"), and a deeper attack on the **frame** (§4: "there appears to be nothing natural, nor historical, about linear strings of tokens"; "Facts about the mind-brain must be invoked to explain how words refer"). The first denies the §4.3 antecedent from inside the dispute the essay diagnoses; the second walks out of it.

And the pole is contested from a *third* direction — from inside externalism itself. [`source/ostertag-2025-externalist-reply`](../../base/sources/ostertag-2025-externalist-reply.md) (Ostertag 2025) is an externalist who grants the framework and faults only M&L's particular "naïve externalist" *route*: applying Lipton's contrastive-explanation test to the Twin Earth case, he argues the bare natural history cannot supply the difference-maker ("oxygen is equally implicated in the causal history of water," §2), so "externalism, as understood by Mandelkern and Linzen, cannot ground reference" ([`source/ostertag-2025-externalist-reply`](../../base/sources/ostertag-2025-externalist-reply.md), §3). This is not the internalist's frame-rejection and not a denial of the §4.3 membership antecedent; it is an attack on the *natural-histories-suffice* premise of M&L's §3, from a co-externalist. So the pro-reference pole, uncontested in-repo only one session ago, now faces three published objections: an internalist "wrong programme" (Baggio & Murphy), an externalist "right programme, wrong grounding mechanism" (Ostertag), and the project's own in-principle "no."

**The in-principle-no pole (the project's concept page).** The same externalism, applied by the project's own [`concept/referential-meaning`](../../base/concepts/referential-meaning.md) page, lands the other way. An LLM "has only narrow access by construction": "its states are functions of its training distribution and its context window — text in, text out. It has no causal-historical chain to a baptismal event, no membership in a community of experts, no environment it is embedded in independently of the corpus" ([`concept/referential-meaning`](../../base/concepts/referential-meaning.md), §"Internalism vs. externalism — and the LLM case"). So "on **externalism**, this is the strongest *in-principle* case that an LLM does not *refer*: the relation reference requires is precisely the form-to-world / form-to-community relation the corpus does not contain" ([`concept/referential-meaning`](../../base/concepts/referential-meaning.md), §"Internalism vs. externalism — and the LLM case").

The two poles are not crude opposites talking past each other. They share the externalist premise (reference fixed off the head, by natural history and community); they share every fact about the model (narrow access, text-only training). The source page itself diagnoses where the daylight is: Mandelkern & Linzen and the concept page "run the *same* externalist premise to *opposite* verdicts, because they disagree on whether inheriting word-histories through a text corpus counts as being 'part of the linguistic community' whose use fixes reference" ([`source/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer`](../../base/sources/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer.md), §"What this grounds / bears on"). That single clause is the whole essay's hinge.

## Why it is premise-bound, not evidence-bound

Lay the two arguments side by side and the structure is plain. They share an inference rule — externalist metasemantics — and they share the empirical minor premise — the facts about what an LLM is and does. They differ on a single classificatory premise: *does corpus-inherited use put the model inside the reference-fixing community?* The community-membership conditional ("If they are part of a linguistic community … then their use … refers," §4.3) is one both poles would accept. The pro-reference pole affirms its antecedent; the in-principle-no pole denies it. Everything downstream — the opposite verdicts — follows from that one classificatory move and nothing else.

The Baggio & Murphy rebuttal, now in-repo, sharpens this rather than disturbing it — but only along its first front. Their §5 is a **published denial of the antecedent on the externalist's own terms**: granting the conditional, they argue that "extending the notion of 'linguistic community member' to include LMs … does not address the issue whether LMs have the required status in our communities" ([`source/baggio-murphy-2024-internalist-rejoinder`](../../base/sources/baggio-murphy-2024-internalist-rejoinder.md), §5). That is the in-principle-no pole's classificatory move, now voiced by a published source instead of only by the project's concept page — which *confirms* the diagnosis: the live argument is over the antecedent's truth, an off-board status question, not over any measurement. But their §4 is a different kind of move, and the diagnosis must not absorb it: it does not deny the antecedent *within* externalism, it rejects the externalist machinery outright ("nothing natural, nor historical, about linear strings of tokens"; reference fixed by "facts about the mind-brain," not corpus histories). A frame-rejection is not premise-bound *inside* the frame — it changes the frame. So Baggio & Murphy occupy both a position the diagnosis covers (their §5, antecedent-denial) and one it explicitly does not (their §4, the internalist reframing the Honest-limits flagged as a live possibility). The premise-bound claim is a claim about the externalist dispute; §5 lives inside it, §4 outside it.

The Ostertag reply corroborates the *shape* of the diagnosis from inside the frame, and is worth stating carefully because the corroboration is partial. Ostertag stays externalist and locates his disagreement with M&L in a premise, not a measurement — explicitly: "my concern is not with the conditional, which I accept, but with the antecedent, about which I have doubts" ([`source/ostertag-2025-externalist-reply`](../../base/sources/ostertag-2025-externalist-reply.md), §4). That a published interlocutor, replying to M&L, frames the whole disagreement as turning on an antecedent he doubts is exactly the premise-bound structure this essay names. The honest qualification: Ostertag's antecedent is *not* M&L's §4.3 community-membership antecedent — his doubts attach to referential-intentions and to whether the bare natural history is a difference-maker at all (the §3 "cannot ground reference" verdict bites M&L's *natural-histories-suffice* premise, §3 of M&L). So the corroboration is not that Ostertag agrees the dispute hinges on *the same* premise; it is that he, too, finds the dispute hinges on *a premise* — and resolves it by conceptual argument (Lipton's contrastive explanation), not by any fact about a model. Seen across all three published parties, this is the strongest support the essay's central claim has: M&L argue *for* the verdict, Baggio & Murphy and Ostertag argue *against* it, and **not one of the three adduces a measurement** — every move is over a premise (community membership, the externalist frame itself, the natural-histories difference-maker), each settled off-board by conceptual argument. A dispute in which all parties argue premises and none reaches for evidence is the very definition of premise-bound, not evidence-bound. The diagnosis is now instanced by the disputants' own practice, not only asserted by the essay.

This is what "premise-bound, not evidence-bound" means. A dispute is *evidence-bound* when the parties would converge if shown the right measurement — when some fact, currently unknown, would settle it. This dispute has no such fact in it. The parties already agree on every fact a probe could supply: that the model is text-trained, that it has narrow access, that its outputs distribute as they do. The remaining question — whether corpus inheritance counts as community membership — is not a hidden empirical fact about the model. It is, on the externalist's own picture, a fact about reference-fixing that is settled **off-board**: by the natural history of the form and the social structure of the community, not by anything in the model's head or in its behavior. The externalist insists on exactly this. The §4.1 Twin Earth move makes the point: what fixes whether "water" refers to H₂O or to XYZ "is not the beliefs of the speakers about the liquids' respective chemical compositions" ([`source/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer`](../../base/sources/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer.md), §4.1) — it is the environment and the history. If even the *speaker's own beliefs* do not fix reference, then *a fortiori* nothing recoverable from the speaker's (or model's) behavior does. Reference is fixed where the probe cannot look.

So the project's instruments cannot reach the deciding premise. What the project measures is word-to-word structure: distributional neighborhoods, surprisal, inference-preservation — the company a form keeps and what it licenses (the inferential role [`concept/inferential-meaning`](../../base/concepts/inferential-meaning.md) names — a word or construction "means what it licenses you to infer to and from," its position in "what follows from what," not "any prior relation to objects"), not what it reaches out to beyond the text. That is the boundary the project's own tags draw: [`concept/distributional-meaning`](../../base/concepts/distributional-meaning.md) structure is "by itself … silent on reference and on truth" ([`meaning-senses.md`](../../meaning-senses.md), §`distributional`), and the reference (word-to-world) vs inferential-role (word-to-word) line is precisely "the boundary the project's `referential` vs. `inferential` tags draw" ([`source/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer`](../../base/sources/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer.md), §"What this grounds / bears on", on §5.1 of the paper). A behavioral probe lives entirely on the word-to-word side. The deciding premise — community membership, hence word-to-world reference — lives entirely on the other. The instruments and the question never intersect.

The concept page reaches the same conclusion from the methodology side, and the essay leans on it: a reference anchor "is not a fetch away," because a Twin-Earth-style diagnostic "needs a *controlled external-environment difference* between two otherwise-identical narrow states … But an LLM has no environment to vary independently of its text — there is no 'Twin Earth' to move it to — so the manipulation that operationalizes externalist reference has no purchase on it" ([`concept/referential-meaning`](../../base/concepts/referential-meaning.md), §"Why the reference-anchor gap is structural, not just a missing download"). That is the same silence this essay names, arrived at from the resource side rather than the dispute side: the manipulation that would test the deciding premise cannot be built for a text model, because the premise is about an off-board relation the model has no second copy of to contrast against.

## The two consequences (disciplining the project's own citation)

The diagnosis is not idle; it constrains how the project may cite both poles. Two rules follow.

**Consequence 1 — do not cite the grounding nulls as bearing against reference.** The project has two grounding results that came out negative ([`result/lexical-perceptual-grounding-moderation-v1`](../results/lexical-perceptual-grounding-moderation-v1.md); [`result/multimodal-grounding-image-v1`](../results/multimodal-grounding-image-v1.md)). It is tempting to read them as evidence that LLM words do not refer — adding a perceptual channel changed nothing, so the model is "stuck" symbol-to-symbol. That inference is *off-target*, and the source page already says why: Mandelkern & Linzen's "grounding-via-history route is orthogonal to the perception-channel route those nulls test, so it neither rescues nor is rescued by them" ([`source/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer`](../../base/sources/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer.md), §"What it does (and does not) establish — for this project's nulls"). The premise-bound diagnosis explains *why* this discipline follows and is not just a caveat. The externalist route to reference runs through *history*, not *perception* — §3 of the paper answers the grounding problem "by denying that grounding must run through the individual system's perception/action: the *history* of the form, not the model's sensorimotor contact, does the grounding work" ([`source/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer`](../../base/sources/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer.md), §"What this grounds / bears on", on §3). This is the history-grounds-reference vs perception-grounds-reference contrast that [`concept/symbol-grounding-problem`](../../base/concepts/symbol-grounding-problem.md) frames. The grounding nulls test the *perception* channel; the reference dispute is decided on the *history* channel. They are different channels, so a null on one is silent on the other. The project's grounding nulls therefore bear on the perceptual-surplus question they were built for, and on the deciding reference premise **not at all** — and they must be cited that way.

**Consequence 2 — do not cite Mandelkern & Linzen as establishing that LMs refer.** Their verdict is modal and premise-conditional, by their own framing: "may suffice" (§Abstract), "looks much more likely" (§6), with the speech-community escape hatch left explicitly open (§6). The source page records the bound: it is "a **conceptual argument that LM words *may* refer**, not a demonstration that they *do*" and "does **not** establish any empirical fact about any model" ([`source/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer`](../../base/sources/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer.md), §"What it does (and does not) establish — for this project's nulls"); it "cannot serve as an `anchors:` resource for a claim or result" ([`source/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer`](../../base/sources/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer.md), §"What it cannot ground"). The correct citation, which the diagnosis licenses, is the one the source page already names: cite it "as the strongest in-repo statement of *why the in-principle externalist 'no' the* [`concept/referential-meaning`](../../base/concepts/referential-meaning.md) *page records is contestable*" ([`source/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer`](../../base/sources/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer.md), §"What it does (and does not) establish — for this project's nulls"). The paper makes the concept page's "no" *contestable* — it shows the antecedent of the membership conditional is arguable, not absurd. It does not flip the "no" to a settled "yes." That is the most the premise-bound structure permits, because the paper's positive verdict is exactly as conditional as the disagreement it sits inside. As of this revision the discipline has an extra spur: the pole is now actively contested in-repo by [`source/baggio-murphy-2024-internalist-rejoinder`](../../base/sources/baggio-murphy-2024-internalist-rejoinder.md), so citing Mandelkern & Linzen as *settling* reference would not only overrun their own modal framing, it would now also ignore a published reply in the repo. Cite the pole as contestable-and-contested, never as decided.

Both consequences have the same shape: the project's descriptivist register — "describe, don't litigate" ([`concept/deflationary-and-eliminativist-llm-meaning`](../../base/concepts/deflationary-and-eliminativist-llm-meaning.md), §"The project's descriptivism: 'describe, don't litigate'") — already refuses to adjudicate the existence question for behavioral reasons. This essay shows that for *reference* specifically the refusal is not merely prudent but forced: the deciding premise is not the kind of thing the project's evidence can touch, so litigating it would be claiming evidence the method cannot produce.

## What this essay is not

- **Not a verdict on who is right about the premise.** The essay does not affirm or deny that corpus inheritance counts as community membership. It locates the disagreement in that antecedent and stops. Both poles remain standing; the essay ranks neither.
- **Not a claim that reference is unknowable in general.** The silence diagnosed here is the *project's behavioral method's* silence on the *externalist* premise for *text-trained* models. A different method — or a primary externalist source entering the repo that argued the membership question on independent grounds — could move the dispute without any probe. The claim is about this method, not about all possible argument.
- **Not the relational essays' thesis.** [`essay/inherited-not-constituted`](inherited-not-constituted.md) and [`essay/aggregation-not-constitution`](aggregation-not-constitution.md) argue about the *relational* axis — whether linguistic content is *inherited* vs. *constituted between agents*, and what a behavioral relational null does and does not deflate. This essay is about the *word-to-world* axis — whether a model's words *refer* — and about a *premise* no probe reaches, not about a *null* a probe produced. The two relational essays turn on a result the project ran; this essay turns on a result the project *cannot* run. That is the distinct angle, and it `refines` the [`concept/referential-meaning`](../../base/concepts/referential-meaning.md) page (sharpening its in-principle "no" into a premise-located one), not the relational essays.
- **Not a license to ignore reference.** Saying the method is silent on the deciding premise is not saying the question is unimportant or that the project should drop it. It is saying the project should mark this cell as a principled limit rather than pretend a probe could fill it.

## Honest limits

- **The poles are not symmetric in standing, and the essay does not pretend they are.** Mandelkern & Linzen is a published, two-author position piece whose verdict is "the authors' argued position, not a consensus" ([`source/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer`](../../base/sources/mandelkern-linzen-2024-do-words-refer.md), §"Known limits"). As of this revision its pro-reference verdict is contested in-repo by *two* published replies and the project's own reading: the internalist [`source/baggio-murphy-2024-internalist-rejoinder`](../../base/sources/baggio-murphy-2024-internalist-rejoinder.md) (a preprint/commentary whose internalist premises are "themselves contested," §"Known limits"); the peer-reviewed intramural-externalist [`source/ostertag-2025-externalist-reply`](../../base/sources/ostertag-2025-externalist-reply.md) (*Computational Linguistics* 51(2), whose verdict is likewise "Ostertag's argued position, not a consensus," §"Known limits", and which leans on "one framework (contrastive explanation / Lipton)"); and the concept page's in-principle "no." So the in-repo dialectic is now one published externalist "yes" against one published internalist "no", one published externalist "the route fails", and the project's reading — more balanced than one session ago, but a count of positions, not of settled authorities. The essay treats these as the sides whose collision *locates the premise(s)*, not as equally-weighted authorities, and it ranks none of them.
- **The externalist primaries are still not in-repo.** Putnam 1975 and Evans 1973 (and Kripke) remain in [`wanted.md`](../../base/wanted.md); the concept page flags that "even the *conceptual* grounding of `referential.externalist` is provisional pending those ingestions" ([`concept/referential-meaning`](../../base/concepts/referential-meaning.md), §"Live tension"). So the very metasemantics both poles run is in-repo only as a worked application (Mandelkern & Linzen) and the project's characterization, not as a primary source. The premise-bound diagnosis is robust to this — it is about the *structure* of the disagreement, which holds however the primaries read — but a reader should know the externalist machinery is not yet anchored to its sources here.
- **The diagnosis is conditional on accepting externalism as the frame — and that frame is now contested in-repo, not just hypothetically.** "Premise-bound, not evidence-bound" is a claim about the dispute *as the externalist poles frame it*. The original essay flagged that "an internalist or a deflationist reframing could relocate the disagreement"; with [`source/baggio-murphy-2024-internalist-rejoinder`](../../base/sources/baggio-murphy-2024-internalist-rejoinder.md) in-repo that reframing is **actual**: their §4 ("Deeper problems with externalism") rejects the externalist machinery and relocates reference to "facts about the mind-brain." The essay's scope is therefore explicitly the *externalist* dispute (M&L's "yes" vs. the concept page's / Baggio & Murphy's §5 antecedent-denial "no"); a reader who rejects externalism with Baggio & Murphy's §4 is in a different argument, on which this essay's premise-bound claim is silent — the diagnosis describes where *the externalist disagreement* lives, not which metasemantics is correct.
