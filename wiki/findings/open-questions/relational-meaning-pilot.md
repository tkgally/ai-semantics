# Open question: minimal two-agent setup for relational meaning

## The question

The charter's distinctive axis is `relational` meaning — meaning constituted *between* a model and a person, or between models — and notes that the existing multi-agent literature is about *coordination*, not *meaning-constitution*. What is the smallest interactional setup whose results would count as evidence for or against meaning being constituted between agents, rather than computed within one?

## Why it matters

This is plausibly the project's strongest contribution if executed well. The first three founding conjectures (dative, AANN, *way*) are all `model-internal` probes — they ask what a single model knows. None of them touches the relational axis. Without a pilot here, the relational angle remains rhetoric.

## What a serious answer would look like

A pilot design that:

1. Specifies a minimal interaction (e.g., two-model dialogue with a referentially-ambiguous referring expression; collaborative narrative continuation with constructional drift; iterated paraphrase with a shared term whose meaning is *between* the two paraphrasers).
2. Defines a measure where "meaning constituted between" is operationally distinguishable from "meaning computed in each, then averaged" — e.g., a behavior shift in one agent that depends on the *trajectory* of the other agent's prior outputs, not on the prior outputs treated as static input.
3. Has a human anchor: a comparable resource of dyadic human linguistic data (the Switchboard / CALLHOME corpora, or task-oriented dialogue with referential alignment such as the *Map Task* corpus, or the lexical-alignment literature in psycholinguistics).
4. Is run-cheap: cost-constrained because the relational case multiplies probe calls.

## Why this is queued, not active

This needs reading first — at minimum the lexical-alignment / Pickering & Garrod line, and the multi-agent LLM literature, to make sure the "minimal" setup is not already covered. It is the **second-loop** target, not the first.
