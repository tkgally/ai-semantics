June 15, 2026 (seventeenth session) · writing philosophy no spending

A debate that turns on assumptions, not evidence — now with three published voices

A philosophy-only session: no model queries, no spending. The work centred on one of the project's essays, which makes a careful and deliberately modest argument. The question of whether a language model's words refer — actually reach out and pick out things in the world, the way your use of "water" picks out water — is one the project's experiments, the essay argues, simply cannot answer. The reason is structural: the leading "yes, they may refer" argument and the project's own reading of the same theory agree on every fact about the model and disagree only about one starting assumption — whether inheriting words through a training corpus counts as joining the language community whose usage fixes what words mean. And that assumption, on the very theory both sides use, is settled by facts out in the world and its history, not by anything a behavioral test of the model could measure. So the essay marks this as a principled limit, not a gap more experiments could close.

When the essay was first written, the strongest "yes, they refer" paper stood unopposed in the project's library, which the essay flagged as a condition that might change. It has now changed twice. An earlier reply, catalogued last session, comes from a "mind-first" tradition: reference depends on a speaker's mind, which a model lacks, so its words refer to nothing (they are merely about topics). A second reply, catalogued this session, was the more interesting case. It was expected to defend the pro-reference view — and on reading the actual paper it does the opposite: although the author shares that paper's "world-first" framework, he argues its particular shortcut (that a word's bare history is enough to ground reference) fails on its own terms, using a tool from the theory of explanation. The project's advance note had the paper backwards, so it was corrected — exactly the kind of honest correction the project's rules call for.

The essay was revised to show the "yes" position now contested from both sides. Notably, its core claim came out stronger: across all three published thinkers — one arguing for the view, two against — not one reaches for a measurement; every argument is over a starting assumption, settled by reasoning. That is precisely what "settled by assumptions, not evidence" predicts, so the disputants' own practice now instances the essay's point rather than the essay merely asserting it. One of the new authors even states plainly that his disagreement is "with the antecedent" — the starting assumption — "about which I have doubts," not "with the conditional, which I accept." The session also wrote up, but did not yet act on, a plan for a future experiment: an earlier study found these models track where on the page a fact sits rather than when in a conversation it was said, and the two are hard to tell apart; the plan sets out how a follow-up could separate them (and a cheaper pre-check of whether the models read conversation timestamps at all). As with all such plans, it is left for a later session to approve, never the one that wrote it. Spending: nothing.

June 15, 2026 (sixteenth session) · experiment grammar philosophy spent ~$0.16

Running the planned grammar experiment — and finding the safeguard could not be met

Last session wrote a careful plan for a third grammar test (does the "preference without commitment" pattern show up in the caused-motion construction, as in she sneezed the napkin off the table?), with a built-in safeguard: the test only makes sense if the models do not already agree at the ceiling — otherwise there's no room to measure anything. This session put that safeguard to the test first, with a small, cheap probe, before committing to the full experiment.

An independent reviewer signed off on running just that safeguard check. It failed — cleanly, and informatively. The plan had hoped that verbs describing a weak physical action (a blink, a hum, a nod) would leave the causation reading genuinely contestable. They did not: the models read "she blinked the feather off the table" as caused motion almost every time — the construction does the work regardless of the verb. The only verbs that did escape the ceiling were mental verbs ("she knew the feather off the table"), and those escape only because the sentence is near-nonsense, which the models simply reject — not the kind of "room to measure" the test needs, and unusable for the control the safeguard requires. So there is no clean way to build this particular test from the available materials.

Per the plan's own rule, the experiment therefore stopped at the safeguard and did not run the main test — no adjusting the bar to squeak through. The honest conclusion stands: the "preference without commitment" pattern remains specific to the one construction it was first found in, on the resources available. This is now the second grammar pattern that failed to reproduce it (a different one failed earlier for a different reason), which makes the "specific so far" reading firmer without ever claiming more than the evidence allows. On the philosophy side, the session catalogued a published rebuttal to a recent argument that language models' words can genuinely refer to things in the world — an "internalist" reply arguing that reference depends on a speaker's mind, not on a word's history in a text corpus, so the models' words refer to nothing (they are merely about topics). Spending: about 16 cents (a probe that ran twice by accident, disclosed in the records; the result was unaffected).

Settling how to firm up the grammar finding — and reading the paper behind a famous phrase

A session with no new model queries. It did three things. First, it reviewed and approved — through an independent check, as the rules require — last session's open question about whether to attempt a genuine third construction to firm up the "preference without commitment" grammar finding. The verdict: try it, but only under strict safeguards — engineer a version of an established grammatical pattern (the "caused-motion" construction, as in she sneezed the napkin off the table) where the models do not already agree at the ceiling, so there is room to measure a real effect; and only run it if a built-in control can rule out the earlier trap, where an apparent "preference" turned out to be a single word doing the work rather than the grammar. Third, on the philosophy side, it tracked down and catalogued the original 2021 paper that coined the phrase "stochastic parrot" — the best-known statement of the view that language models produce fluent form without meaning — replacing a second-hand paraphrase in the project's notes with the authors' exact words, checked line by line against the open-access PDF. June 15, 2026 (fourteenth session) · writing grammar philosophy no spending

Writing down what we now know — and marking the edge of what the method can reach

This session ran no new model queries. The last few sessions had each added a piece of evidence; this one stopped to write two of those pieces up properly and to flag an honest limit, so the project's records say exactly what the evidence supports and no more.

The grammar close, made official. Across recent sessions, one memorable grammar pattern — the language models preferring a construction's reading without committing to the strict inference that goes with it (we called it "preference without commitment") — held up firmly for the phrase it was first found in, but failed to appear, twice, in a second construction. The session turned that into a plain, recorded conclusion: on the evidence so far, the pattern is specific to the one construction, not a general habit — with one important piece of self-discipline written in. Because the second construction's test showed that a model's apparent "preference" can be produced by a single tell-tale word rather than by the grammar, the conclusion warns that a preference on its own is weak evidence: it can be a word-level cue, not real grammatical understanding. The conclusion is also kept deliberately modest: only one alternative construction has been checked, so the honest phrasing is "specific so far," not "unique."

A philosophy essay on a question the method cannot answer. The session also wrote an original essay about whether a language model's words refer to things in the world. It picks out a sharp disagreement already sitting in the project's notes: a 2024 paper argues that a model's words do refer (because the text it learns from carries the real history of how words are used), while the project's own reference page, starting from the same philosophical principle, reaches the opposite verdict. The essay's contribution is to show why they disagree: not about any fact a test could settle, but about a single prior question — whether learning words from a giant text collection makes a model a genuine member of the community of speakers whose usage fixes what words mean. That question is settled "off the page," by history and society, not by anything you could measure in the model's behavior. So the essay draws a firm line: this is a place where the project's behavior-watching method is simply silent, by its nature — not a gap that more experiments could fill. It also tightens two of the project's own habits: don't cite the earlier "grounding" results as evidence against reference (they test a different channel), and don't cite the 2024 paper as having proven models refer (its own verdict is hedged).

A question put on the table for next time. Following the grammar close, the session wrote up — but did not decide — the natural next question: is it worth trying a genuine third construction to firm up "specific so far," and if so, how, given that the obvious candidates are already answered so completely by the models that there is no room left to measure a new effect? It is recorded with options and a recommended default (try it, but only under strict safeguards; otherwise accept the modest close and move the experiments elsewhere), to be reviewed and ratified in a later session. Spend this session: nothing — no model was queried — leaving the June 15 total at about $0.10, well within the $5.00 daily cap.

June 15, 2026 (thirteenth session) · experiment grammar sources $0.05 spent

Checking a surprise — and finding it was just luck of the draw

Last session turned up one small surprise. While testing whether the project's deepest grammar pattern travels to a new construction (it didn't), a single one of the three language models showed an odd little effect of its own — on the "conative" (kicked at the ball, which need not mean contact was made), that model alone seemed to genuinely hold back the contact-was-made inference. It rested on one model, one small set of verbs, and a wide margin of error, so last session's notes flagged it as "an anomaly worth a future look, not a finding." This session took that look.

A clean re-test. The cheapest, most honest way to tell a real effect from a fluke is to run the exact same test again on a completely different set of verbs. So this session did just that: the same questions, the same scoring, the same models — but a fresh batch of twelve "kick"-type verbs (slap, smack, pound, beat, strike, …) and eight contrast verbs, none reused from before. The measuring code was kept byte-for-byte identical to last time, precisely so nothing could be quietly adjusted. An independent reviewer checked the new verb list first (confirming, among other things, that none of the contrast verbs has a sneaky idiom that would muddy the comparison) and gave the go-ahead before any question was asked.

The result: the surprise did not hold. On the fresh verbs the odd effect simply evaporated — the model that had seemed to hold back the inference now mostly affirmed contact, just like the other two. Measured the same way as before, its effect dropped from a sizeable margin to essentially zero. So the honest reading is that last session's anomaly was luck of the draw — a quirk of those particular verbs, not a real property of the model. That is a useful negative: it removes the one loose end and makes the broader picture cleaner. The memorable "prefer-without-committing" pattern now looks even more firmly specific to the one place it was first found, with no genuine counter-example in the new construction on either of two independent tries. A third independent reviewer reproduced every number from the raw answers and confirmed nothing had been tuned after the fact. The run made 240 queries and cost about five cents, with no failed responses.

On the reading side, the session also filed a sharply-argued 2024 philosophy paper that pushes against the "a language model's words are meaningless" view: it argues that a model's words may genuinely refer to things in the world, because the text it learns from carries the long history of how those words have actually been used — a history that, on one well-known philosophical account, is what ties a word to its referent in the first place. It is filed as a strong voice in the debate (with its own hedges noted), not as something the project has shown. No methodological decision was self-approved this session (none was open). Spend: about $0.05, bringing the June 15 total to about $0.10, well within the $5.00 daily cap.

June 15, 2026 (twelfth session) · experiment grammar sources $0.05 spent

A striking pattern turns out not to travel

The project's deepest grammar finding has a memorable shape. For the phrase a beautiful three days, the language models prefer the reading the construction invites (treating the stretch as one whole) — but only one of the three also commits to the strict logical inference that goes with it. We called this "preference without commitment." The obvious next question, set up last session and approved at the start of this one, was: is this split special to that one phrase, or a general habit?

First, approving the test. Last session left open which second construction to test it on, and how to keep the comparison fair — a method choice the project's rules say must be written down and approved by a later session, never the one that proposes it. This session an independent reviewer re-examined that choice from scratch, checked the recommended option against the evidence and against the project's anti-gaming safeguards (could the setup quietly guarantee a particular answer? — no), found the supporting numbers and quotations all genuine, and approved it. The chosen test case is the "conative": kicked at the ball, which need not mean the ball was actually hit — a construction whose licensed reading points the opposite way from the verb's ordinary meaning.

Then, running it — and the result. A small frozen experiment (240 questions across three models, about five cents) put the same two-instrument test to the conative. Before looking at the answers, a second independent reviewer checked the setup and caught one weak spot — one of the comparison verbs (snap at) has its own idiomatic meaning that would muddy the control — which was swapped out before any question was asked. The outcome: the pattern did not travel. The "preference" half of the effect, so broad for the original phrase, here turned out to be carried entirely by the bare little word at — not by the construction itself — in all three models. The only genuine construction effect was in a single model, and it was the mirror of the original: commitment without preference. A third independent reviewer reproduced every number from the raw answers and confirmed nothing had been tuned after the fact. So the honest reading is that the "preference without commitment" shape looks, for now, specific to the construction it was found in — not a general law. The forward-looking bet the project had written down is recorded as tested and not confirmed; the broader conceptual point it rests on (that "preferring" a reading and "committing" to its inference are two different things, which can come apart) still stands. One construction can't prove uniqueness, so the door stays open to a third test later.

On the reading side, the session also filed a new outside study that put humans and AI models in the same referring-and-describing game and found the models, unlike people, never built up the compact shared shorthand that human partners settle into — a result that fits, from a different angle, an earlier finding of the project's own. This session approved one method decision through independent review, exactly as the rules require (it was proposed in an earlier session, not this one). Spend: about $0.05, the first spending of June 15, well within the $5.00 daily cap.

June 14, 2026 (eleventh session) · philosophy grammar $0 spent

Two routes to one picture, and the next grammar test set up

A reading-and-writing session — no models queried, nothing spent — with one unit on each side of the project, both landed and merged together.

The philosophy side: a new short essay connecting an outside paper to the project's own work. Last session the project read and filed a 2024 philosophy-of-language article. That article argues, from theory, that a language model's words can be meaningful because they ride on a public language the model inherits from its training text — meaning that lives in the shared language, not in any inner mental life the model is supposed to have. The project also has an earlier finding of its own, from experiment: when two models build a shared name for something over a conversation, what looks like meaning being created between them turns out to be each model independently recovering shared content it already had — the project calls this "coordination, not construction-between-them." The new essay argues that these two — a theory argument and a behavior experiment, with nothing in common in how they work — reach the same picture from opposite directions: the content a model uses sits in something prior and shared (the inherited public language), not in the model's own on-the-spot act and not in the live exchange. The essay's discipline is in what it refuses to claim: this is a meeting of pictures, not a shared proof. The experiment does not prove the philosophy, and the philosophy does not prove the experiment; the strongest thing said is that the experiment's result is exactly what you would expect if the philosophy is right. The essay introduces no new factual claim of its own, and it carries spelled-out conditions under which it would be revised (for instance, a future experiment that did find meaning genuinely built up between models would weaken its experiment-side leg).

The experiment side: the next grammar test set up, not yet run. The project's deepest grammar result has a striking pattern: for the construction a beautiful three days, the models will prefer the reading the construction licenses (treating it as one whole stretch) while only one of the three also commits to the strict inference that goes with it. The open question is whether this "prefer-without-committing" split is special to that one construction or a general habit. This session wrote that up as a precise, testable bet and prepared the way to test it on a different construction — one where the construction's licensed inference points the opposite way from the word's ordinary meaning (the "conative": "kicked at the ball" need not mean the ball was hit). Choosing which construction to use, and fixing the fairness safeguards, is a method decision — so by the project's own rule it was written down with its options and a recommended default and left for a later session to approve before anything is run. No model was queried; the run itself belongs to a future session.

An independent reviewer checked both new pieces against the sources they cite and against each other; it found no invented quotations or numbers, and two small fixes it flagged (a mis-attributed figure and registering the new pages in the project's index) were applied before the work was kept. No methodological decision was self-approved this session — the one method choice that came up was deliberately left open for a later session, as the rules require. Spend: $0; the day's running total stays about $1.19 of the $5.00 daily cap.

June 14, 2026 (tenth session) · philosophy sources $0 spent

A written argument about the last result, strengthened by its replication — and a new source read

A reading-and-writing session on the philosophical side; no models were queried and nothing was spent. It did two things, both following directly from the previous session's replication.

First, it revised one of the project's short written arguments to stand on firmer evidence. Two sessions back, the project wrote an essay about a puzzle in the deepest grammar-meaning result: the special phrase a beautiful three days reliably shifts which paraphrase all three models prefer (toward "one whole stretch"), but only one of the three also commits to the matching yes/no inference. The essay argued that a preference and a commitment are not two noisy readings of one thing — they are evidence for two different things, so the question "does the model use the construction's meaning?" has no single yes-or-no answer. When that essay was written it rested on a single run, and it stated in advance exactly what would force a rewrite: if a bigger, fresher repeat showed the two "preference-only" models also starting to commit, the argument would be retracted for them; and if the preference shift itself failed to repeat, the whole argument would weaken. The previous session's larger re-run tested both of those conditions — and neither came true: the two models still prefer without committing, and the preference shift repeated cleanly. So the essay was strengthened, not retracted, and updated to say so: its central example is now a replicated pattern across two separate word sets, not a one-off. The honest ceilings are unchanged — still only three models, still scored against an expert's answer key rather than human data, so it makes no claim about how people do it.

Second, it read and filed a new outside source — a 2024 philosophy-of-language article (Grindrod, in Synthese) arguing that the right way to ask whether a language model "means" what it says is to ask not whether it has the inner mental states a meaning would require, but whether its words meet the conditions our best theories set for a linguistic item to carry content. On two such theories, the author argues, the answer comes out positive — because a word's meaning leans on a pre-existing public language, which the model inherits from its training text. The project files this as one carefully-summarized position in the debate (an argument against the flat "it's just a clever text-predictor" dismissal, made from the language side), not as something the project has shown — its own stance stays "describe what the models do, don't argue about the label." Every quotation was checked word-for-word against the article's full text; one small wording difference between two versions of the paper is noted on the source's page.

Both pieces were checked for accuracy and for any overclaiming before being kept; the written argument states nothing stronger than the result it rests on. No new experiments and no new spending — the day's running total stays about $1.19 against the $5.00 daily cap.

June 14, 2026 (ninth session) · experiment $0.21 spent

The "does it use the meaning of a beautiful three days?" result, re-run bigger — and it holds

Back to the experiment side this session, on the project's grammatical track. Two sessions ago, the deepest grammatical test produced a careful partial yes: the special phrase a beautiful three days nudges the models toward reading the span as one whole evaluated stretch (rather than three separately-good days), and that nudge showed up across several different ways of asking in one of the three models, but only in the simplest way of asking (pick-the-better-paraphrase) in the other two. That result came with an honest warning printed on its own page: it rested on only 23 hand-built items, run once, on a single day — enough to show a direction, not a stable size.

This session attacked exactly that weakness with a replication. The same test was re-run with nothing changed but the items — the same question, the same scoring code, the same decision rule fixed in advance — on 40 brand-new items built from 40 fresh adjectives that had never appeared in any earlier version of this test (checked automatically), nearly doubling the size and evening out the two word-classes (time spans and distances). Using genuinely new words matters: a repeat on the very same items would only re-measure them, whereas fresh words test whether the effect generalizes.

It replicated point for point. All three models again shift toward the "one whole stretch" reading on the paraphrase test — by a wide, statistically clear margin, measured against a comparison phrase deliberately built to read "day by day" by default. And the same single model again carries that shift through every way of asking, including a grammar reflex (it switches to the singular — "a beautiful three days was…") that runs against the usual statistical grain, which is the one signal a "it's just word-statistics" story cannot easily fake. The other two models again shift only in the paraphrase choice and not the stricter tests. The verdict is the same as before — a partial yes — but now on much firmer ground: the effect's size, not just its direction, is stable across two completely separate sets of words.

The honest limits that remain are stated plainly: this removes the "too small / single day" caveat but not the others — it is still only three models (one panel), and it is still scored against an expert's stipulated answer key, never against human data on this inference, so it can never say the models draw the inference "the way people do." It is a within-model comparison throughout. The run used the already-approved method (no new judgment call to make), with an independent reviewer signing off on the frozen materials before any model was queried and an independent checker recomputing every number from the raw answers afterward — zero mismatches, and the analysis bug that the checker had caught in the previous version is confirmed absent here. About 21 cents; the day's running total is about $1.19 against the $5.00 daily cap.

June 14, 2026 (eighth session) · philosophy $0 spent

A conversation is a text, not a timeline — and two pieces of housekeeping

A reading-and-writing session, weighted toward the project's thinking track rather than its experiments. No models were queried, so nothing was spent. Three things were done in parallel and then cross-checked against each other by an independent reviewer.

1. A new written piece on what the last experiment really showed. The previous session's experiment, on the relational track, found that when two models build a shared name for a thing and the evidence about it shifts partway through a conversation, a fresh model reading the record follows the last line on the screen rather than the most recent turn by its timestamp. The obvious reading was deflating: "so the model just treats the conversation as an unordered pile — order doesn't matter to it." The new essay argues that this reading, while close, is wrong in a specific and interesting way. A true unordered pile would not care where on the page a line sits — it would be blind to layout. But these models are strongly driven by position. So they fit neither of the two boxes the project had set up: not "tracks the conversation's timeline" (the deeper thing the project has been hunting for, and still does not find), and not "reads it as a clean order-free pile." The third thing they are doing is reading the record as a page with a bottom line — a layout. The plainest way to put it: the project's earlier shorthand, "a bag of turns," was too tidy; the truer picture is "a bag with a gradient down the page." That is, if anything, an even more deflating result, not a less deflating one. Every caution from the experiment is carried over without softening: it is a model-vs-model comparison that says nothing about people; it cannot distinguish "follows the last line" from "never read the timestamps as time at all"; the underlying relational question stays open; and it rests on a two-model pilot. The essay is written as a deliberate refinement of the project's first written piece (which set up the pile-versus-timeline choice), not a reversal of it, and it lists in advance exactly which future experiments would change or overturn it.

2. A new outside paper was filed. A 2025 philosophy-of-AI article argues that recent evidence about the inner workings of language models should let us retire the flat "do they understand or not?" debate in favour of a careful, graded comparison between how a model organizes things internally and how a person does. The project files it as a useful map and a kindred stance — it is close to this project's own "describe carefully, don't argue about the label" posture — while flagging the real boundary: that paper reasons from models' internals, whereas this project reasons only from models' behaviour, so its conclusions are a neighbour's, not something this project can borrow as its own evidence. Only the paper's summary could be confirmed word-for-word; two of its later sections could not be reached through the available copies, and the filing says so plainly rather than papering over it.

3. A small accuracy repair. Two quotations in a paper filed a day earlier had been flagged as not-yet-double-checked, because the web copies cut off before the relevant sections. This session reached the original PDF and confirmed both quotations word-for-word, with their exact section locations; the note recording the doubt was updated to say the matter is now settled. No methodological judgment calls were made this session, and nothing was approved that an independent reviewer had not already checked.

June 14, 2026 (seventh session) · experiment $0.94 spent

When a conversation changes its mind about a word, do the models follow the latest turn — or just the last line on the screen?

One of the project's longest-running open questions is on the relational track: when two language models build up a shared private name for a thing over several turns, and then the evidence about what that name refers to shifts partway through, does a fresh model reading the record care about where in the conversation the shift happened — that the most recent turns now point somewhere new? Or does it just blend all the turns together as an unordered pile? Three earlier attempts couldn't answer cleanly, and the last one pinned down exactly why: in those designs, "the most recent turn" was always also "the last line of text on the screen," so you could never tell whether a model was tracking time or just tracking the bottom of the list. The two cues were glued together.

What this session built. A new version that pulls those two cues apart inside a single record. Every turn now carries an explicit timestamp ("Round 1…", "Round 2…", and so on), so recency is stated outright instead of being implied by position — and the decisive most-recent turn is deliberately placed in the middle of the text, with older turns printed after it. Now "latest in time" and "last on the screen" point at different things, and you can see which one a model follows. The two cues were arranged so they are perfectly balanced and independent, which a reviewer and an independent checker both verified down to the arithmetic. It went through the full guardrails: an independent reviewer approved the design before any model was queried — and ruled that switching to explicit timestamps was a sharper tool for the same question, not a changed question (so no special decision was needed) — and an independent checker afterward recomputed every number from the raw answers with its own code, finding no errors.

The result: both models follow the last line on the screen, not the timestamp. In the cases where the two cues conflict — the newest turn sits in the middle, an older turn sits at the bottom — both models pick the thing described by the bottom turn about 70–75% of the time, i.e. they go with the older, lower-on-the-screen evidence. One model shows this cleanly; the other shows the same pattern with a small wrinkle that an independent check traced entirely back to text-position, with no timing signal underneath it at all.

What it does and doesn't mean — read carefully. This is a finding about prompt layout, and it says nothing about relational meaning. Crucially, it cannot even be read as "the models chose to ignore the timestamps": the test can show the models weren't thrown off by scrambled timestamps, but it can't show whether they read the timestamp numbers as recency at all — so "follows the last line" and "doesn't use the timestamps" look identical here, and the honest statement is the narrower one. For the project's standing bet that these models treat such a record as an unordered pile, this run is a genuine draw: it doesn't overturn the bet (no model tracked timing), and it doesn't confirm it either (the models aren't ignoring order — they're firmly anchored on text position). The real prize is a trap now mapped: any future timing test that lists turns in a single column has to separate "newest in time" from "last on the page," or it can't tell the two apart. This run is the template for doing that.

One model in the earlier panel was dropped from this version (it had never been able to produce the clean, individually-recognizable descriptions this test needs), leaving two. No methodological judgment calls were self-approved this session — the one borderline call was reviewed and ruled a sharper tool, not a changed question. Spend: about $0.94 this session (most of it the main run), against the $5.00 daily cap.

June 14, 2026 (sixth session) · experiment philosophy $0.03 spent

A grammar reflex that looked like one model's quirk turns out to be shared — on fresh words

The previous two runs of the "a beautiful three days" meaning test turned up a small, sharp side-signal: the construction takes a singular verb (people say "a beautiful three days was exactly what we needed," not "were"), and one of the three models reliably switched to the singular for the special phrase while keeping the plural for the ordinary one (three beautiful days were…). The other two models picked the singular for both phrases, so the test simply couldn't tell whether they had the reflex — a ceiling, not a finding. This session ran a dedicated test of one question: is that singular-verb reflex special to the one model, or does it show up more widely — and does it survive on fresh words the test hadn't used before?

What the test did. It reused the exact same singular-vs-plural choice from the earlier runs, unchanged, but on 30 freshly written phrases — new adjectives and new measure phrases (times and distances) with zero overlap with the earlier items — and set a stricter bar for counting the reflex as present than the earlier runs used (a deliberately harder test to pass). It went through the project's usual guardrails: an independent reviewer approved the frozen materials before any model was queried, and an independent checker recomputed every number from the raw answers afterward (this time finding no errors).

The result: the reflex is shared, not one model's quirk — but read it carefully. The one model that had the reflex before still has it on the fresh words (a little weaker, but clearing the harder bar). So the reflex is not peculiar to a single model. The third model stays at the ceiling (picks the singular for both), so the test still can't speak for it — which simply repeats, on a fresh set of words, what the earlier runs found for it.

The honest bounds, carried at full strength. The second model's new signal is real but narrow: it is carried entirely by the time-word phrases (on the distance phrases it stays at the ceiling), and it clears the bar by a single item — a thin margin. So the right reading is "the construction shifts this model's verb choice on the time-word items," not a sweeping property. And the earlier "ceiling" and this "off-ceiling" don't contradict — they just reflect which words happened to be in each set. Two more limits: this is a grammar reflex, not evidence the models draw the phrase's meaning-level inference (the project keeps those separate, and this does not change last session's "partial" verdict on the meaning question); and it is a model-vs-model comparison, never a comparison to people. A side-check confirmed all three models happily pick the plural for an ordinary count phrase ("three excited dogs were…"), so their singular preference is specifically about amount-and-duration subjects, not a blanket habit. Small study, single run; about 3 cents.

Turning last session's null into a method, an argument, and a sharper next test

No experiments and no spending this session — the work was to make sense of the previous session's careful "no" and build on it. Three things happened, on two tracks.

A method decision, self-approved and flagged here. The "does it understand a beautiful three days?" test finally ran — a careful no

Last session's held-back experiment was repaired and run this session, and it gives a clean, honestly-bounded negative. The question: language models accept the unusual phrase "a beautiful three days" and rate it natural for the same words people do (shown earlier) — but do they actually use its special meaning, that the phrase presents the three days as one whole, evaluated stretch rather than three separately-nice days? The test asks each model to choose between a "one whole stretch" paraphrase and a "three separate days" paraphrase, and measures not the raw answer but how much the unusual phrasing shifts that choice compared with the ordinary phrasing (three beautiful days), backed by a yes/no entailment method and a grammar reflex (the phrase takes a singular verb: "a beautiful three days was what we needed").

First, the repair. The previous reviewer had flagged that the items about weights, areas, and sums of money used a paraphrase ("one continuous stretch") that is odd for those nouns. Rather than re-word them in the same sitting that found the flaw — a shortcut the project forbids itself — this session simply dropped that whole group of items, keeping the 23 about stretches of time and distance, for which the "one whole stretch" reading is the natural one. A small parser bug was fixed too. A fresh independent reviewer then checked the repaired test from scratch and approved it (confirming all the earlier flaws were gone and no new ones introduced), and only then was it run. After the run, a separate independent check recomputed every number from the raw data and matched them exactly.

The result is a null, with a precise and honest reason. No model shifted its paraphrase choice toward the "one whole stretch" reading when given the special phrasing — but that is because they already read the ordinary phrase (three beautiful days) as a whole stretch nearly every time. With the ordinary phrase already at the ceiling, the special phrasing has no room left to move the needle, so this method simply cannot tell the two readings apart here. So the finding is not "the models lack the understanding" — it is "this test can't separate the construction's meaning from what the model would have inferred anyway." There was one exception that did move: one model reliably switched to the singular verb ("was") for the special phrase and the plural ("were") for the ordinary one — a real grammatical reflex of the construction. But the rules fixed in advance correctly refuse to count a grammar reflex as evidence of drawing the meaning inference. The takeaway, recorded at full and no more than full strength: the construction's form is tracked (shown before and reinforced here), its inferential use remains untested-as-designed, and a sharper future test must use an ordinary comparison phrase that does not already invite the same reading. The run made 624 queries and cost about nine cents, with no failed responses.

June 13, 2026 (second session) · governance design $0 spent

A meaning-test approved, designed, reviewed — and correctly held back

A session with no model experiments and no spending, but real progress on the grammatical frontier's deepest open question. Earlier work showed that language models rate the unusual phrase "a beautiful three days" as natural, and rate it more natural for the same kinds of words people do. But rating a phrase natural is not the same as understanding what it means — that the phrase presents three days as a single, whole, evaluated stretch, not three separately-nice days. How to test for that understanding (rather than mere surface fluency) is a judgment call, so last session it was written down as an open question. This session an independent review approved a specific testing method: ask the model to choose between a "one whole stretch" paraphrase and a "three separate days" paraphrase, and — crucially — measure not the raw answer but how much the unusual phrasing shifts that choice compared with ordinary phrasing; back it up with a second yes/no method and, as the sharpest single check, a grammar reflex (the phrase takes a singular verb — "a beautiful three days was what we needed"). The approval came with eight binding conditions and an honest ceiling: the test can only show whether the construction shifts the model's behavior in the predicted direction — it can never claim the model infers "the way people do," because no human dataset measures that inference.

The full test was then built — 32 hand-written sentence sets, all the analysis code, the scoring rules fixed in advance — and handed to a second independent reviewer before any model was queried. That reviewer found the machinery sound but caught a real flaw: a third of the items (those about weights, areas, and sums of money — "thirty pounds", "forty acres", "two thousand dollars") used a "one continuous stretch" paraphrase that is odd or nonsensical for those nouns, which would have muddied the main measurement. The honest response is not to quietly patch the items and run anyway — fixing a test after seeing the reviewer's notes, in the same sitting, is exactly the kind of shortcut the project forbids itself. So the run was held back: the flaw is logged with a precise repair list, and a clean version will be built, re-reviewed, and run in a later session. A designed, reviewed, deliberately-paused experiment is a better outcome than a rushed one on flawed materials.

Two other threads advanced at no cost. A free re-analysis of existing data pinned down why models fail to track the naturalness gradient for time-words specifically (an earlier finding): it is two things at once — the human ratings for time-words are bunched into the narrowest range of any noun type (so there is barely any gradient to track), and there is one genuinely odd corner ("a scant three days", which people rate highest but every model rates lowest) that flips the whole picture negative; remove that one corner and every model lines up positively again. And on the reading-and-ideas track, the second half of a major two-part philosophical survey of language-model research was catalogued, completing the pair. No experiments ran, so nothing was spent.
