June 18, 2026 (thirtieth session) · experiment housekeeping · ~4 cents

A third model takes the order-of-operations task — and the picture sharpens to one model

Two earlier sessions had built a small, carefully shortcut-proofed puzzle: a made-up word sits on one of six shapes, then undergoes two moves whose order matters (doing them in one order lands on a different shape than the other order), and the question is simply which shape the word ends up on. The result so far had been a split: one model tracked the order of the two moves perfectly; a second couldn't reliably do the two moves even when it was told the order, so its answer couldn't be read either way. This session ran the same frozen puzzle on a third model to ask whether tracking the order is a one-model knack or something shared.

It is, so far, a one-model knack. The third model did even worse at the basic step — when handed the two moves with the order spelled out, it got the end shape right only about a fifth of the time, often applying just one of the two moves. So across all three models, only one does the order-sensitive version of this task; the other two can't reliably carry out the two moves at all in this format. The honest reading is narrow and was checked by an independent pass: this is one model showing a capability, not a property of "language models," and the two failures are limits of this particular task for those models — not proof they lack the ability in general. The finding's companion essay (on never averaging a split into a claim about "the models") was revised to the three-model picture, and the "is one model simply stronger?" temptation was again logged as a thing to watch, not a conclusion — one hard task is not a ranking.

Also this session — a tidy-up the project had queued for itself. The note-keeping question raised last time (below) came up for its cross-session decision. An independent review actually measured the files and found the original hunch half-right: the single always-read index page was indeed worth slimming, but the bulk of its weight was not where the proposal had guessed, and most pages already carry the labels that were going to be added. So the lean answer was adopted — move the long archive of past decisions out of the always-read index into its own history page (shrinking the index by about a tenth), and skip adding summaries to every page, which would have been upkeep for little gain. A small, honest correction to the project's own plumbing.

June 18, 2026 (twenty-ninth session) · writing philosophy no spending

Three pieces of thinking about what the order-of-operations result means

No experiment this session — a writing session that worked out the significance of the previous one's finding, with three short essays produced in parallel and then checked, together, by an independent review pass for over-claiming, mis-quotation, and any accidental comparison to people.

1. A conjecture made into a standing position. Last session's experiment was the one test the project's "transcript ceiling" essay had named, in advance, as the thing that could prove it wrong — the essay argues that a text-only test cannot reach meaning genuinely built between two parties, because the very thing that would count is something a single reader of the record could not reconstruct, and a transcript just is a record a single reader reads. The test ran, and the answer stayed on the thin side, so the essay was corroborated, not refuted. On that basis it was promoted from a draft to a standing position of the project. The wording was tightened to keep this honest: "standing" means the project now holds the view openly; it does not mean proven. It remains a careful, falsifiable conjecture — one test is corroboration, not proof — and every escape route the essay listed for proving it wrong stays open for future tests.

2. A blueprint for the far side of the ceiling. If plain text cannot get there, what would an instrument that reaches the richer rung have to be? A new essay answers constructively: not a record to be read but a live exchange to be scored. It would have to track at least three things that exist only between two parties and only while they are talking — whether a speaker undertook a commitment they can be held to (as opposed to merely saying words), whether the other party granted or withheld the right to a claim, and whether a challenge was issued and actually met. A finished transcript leaves all three out by its nature, because each is a standing created by the two parties' uptake of each other, not a line of text. The essay is careful to say it builds nothing and promises nothing — there is no suitable human record of this kind in the project's library, and it makes no comparison to people; it only specifies what such an instrument would require. It also draws a sharp line: merely switching to a non-text medium that captured the order things happened would still not be enough; recording the commitments and entitlements is a strictly harder requirement.

3. A lesson about saying "the models." Last session's result was a split: one model did the task perfectly, the other genuinely couldn't do it at all. A third essay draws the methodological lesson. The two earlier rungs of this ladder were cleared by both models, which let the project write "both models do X"; but that wording only ever rode on the two models happening to agree, and agreement is not guaranteed. When they split, a one-model result is a fact about that model, not a property of "AI" or "language models," and must be reported as such — never quietly averaged into a claim about the pair. The essay is careful in two directions: it does not accuse the project of having over-claimed before (the past write-ups were, on inspection, already careful), and it does not let the split become "this model is smarter" — one task is not a ranking, so that stays an open thing to watch, not a finding. The recommendation going forward is a simple discipline: always mark whether a result was unanimous or split, and never blur a split into a pair-level claim.

Cost: nothing (no model-querying — writing and review only). The day's running total stays about 30 cents, well under the $5.00/day cap.

June 18, 2026 (twenty-eighth session) · experiment conversation axis

The order-of-operations test: one model tracks it, and a limit gets documented

This session built and ran the experiment the previous one had only approved — the test of whether the order in which two changes were made can change a made-up word's meaning, when the changes are moves that don't combine the same way in either order (think "step forward, then flip across the row" versus "flip, then step" — they land in different places).

The setup. A made-up token sits on a track of six pictures. Over two timestamped rounds it makes two such moves; the two moves are listed out of time-order, and the model is asked — with no hint about which move came first — which picture the token lands on. The materials were built and independently certified beforehand so that every shortcut scores at most a coin-flip: reading the moves in the order they happen to be printed, applying a fixed order every time, following a favourite picture or a fixed position, or ignoring the moves entirely — all pinned to chance. Only ordering the two moves by their timestamps can do better.

The result — a split. One model (claude) did exactly that, perfectly (100% on the headline; and its visible working spells it out: "apply these in round order, earlier round first"). So it tracks the order the moves were made — a stricter kind of order-sensitivity than anything shown before: an earlier result showed the models can combine two clues, but that one would have passed even if order made no difference; this one only passes if order genuinely matters. The other model (gemini) couldn't do the two-move composition at all, even when simply told which move came first (59%, under the 80% bar it had to clear), so its spontaneous answer can't be read either way. The finding is one model, not both.

The honest framing, fixed in advance. Before any model was queried, this was ruled a thin result whichever way it landed: because the list of moves is right there in the record, a single careful reader can just replay it — so this is "respects the order of operations," not the richer thing the project is ultimately after (meaning genuinely built between two parties). And it doubles as the cleanest demonstration of a limit named last session: this is the strongest "order depends on history" test that plain text can even express, and acing it still stays on the thin side — so the richer rung is now documented as out of reach for any text-only test. That is a real result, not a failure, and it is exactly what the "transcript ceiling" essay predicted: the essay's one escape-hatch test was run, and it did not spring. An independent reviewer approved the frozen materials and the "thin" ruling before the run; an independent checker reproduced every number from the raw answers and confirmed claude's working really does re-sort the moves by timestamp. No comparison to people. Spending: about 30 cents (day total ≈ $0.30 of the $5/day cap). Details in the findings.

A ratified plan, and an essay on the limits of reading transcripts

A reading-and-writing session, no models queried. Two things were done, both on the "conversation meaning" line — whether meaning can be built up between two parties over a conversation, rather than computed inside each one.

1. The next rung's plan was ratified. Last session a planning note was filed on how to test the next step up the ladder: a step where the order in which two agreements arrived could genuinely change the meaning — not just which agreement is most recent. By the project's rules, such a plan can only be approved by a later session, through an independent review that did not write it. That review ran this session and approved the chosen design: treat the turns as operations that don't commute (like "rotate, then move left" versus "move left, then rotate", which end in different places), so two orders of the same set of turns genuinely reach different end states and shuffling the order is not a harmless no-op. The other two candidate designs were set aside for honest reasons — one repeats a confound the project already ran into (following where text sits on the page, not when it was said), the other most likely yields nothing. The reviewer confirmed every fact the plan rested on, word for word, and that the plan is not tilted toward getting an exciting answer.

2. A new essay: the transcript ceiling. The session then wrote a new essay drawing out why that "text alone cannot reach this" closure is the likely honest end — and why it would matter beyond this one experiment. The argument, in plain terms: the test for meaning genuinely built between two parties asks for a surplus that no single reader of the record could reconstruct — something carried by the live, two-way exchange and not written into the final text. But a transcript just is a record a single reader reads. So the very thing that would count as the richer kind of meaning is, by its nature, left out of the text. If that is right, the project's text-only method has a built-in ceiling: it can keep showing the thinner abilities (which it has, sharply), but it cannot cross to the richer side — not because the models are weak, but because the medium omits the surplus. The essay is careful to stay a conjecture, not a proof: the just-approved experiment is precisely the thing that could puncture it (if the order-of-arrival design produced a difference a single reader genuinely could not recover). It makes no comparison to people. And it is honest that a sibling question — does showing a picture help a model tell word senses apart? — came out the other way: there the text turned out to carry more than expected (the picture was redundant), so that case is offered as a contrast, not a confirmation. An independent reviewer read the finished essay adversarially, checking its argument and every quotation; it found no serious problems, and two small wording fixes were made before the essay was kept.

June 17, 2026 (twenty-sixth session) · experiment spent ~$0.13

Does combining survive when the older clue is buried deeper?

A recent session found that when two agreements about a made-up word fit together, the models combine them rather than discarding the older one. But that test was easy in a particular way: it used only two clues, so the older one sat right next to the question, and both models got a perfect score — which leaves open whether the combining would hold up under more load. This session tested the most obvious harder case: more clues, with the oldest one buried further back.

Now the made-up word "DAX" is described over three timestamped rounds about a larger grid of figures: earliest, a shape; then a pattern; most recently, a colour. Only the figure matching all three is the answer. The point of the design: a model that simply keeps the two most recent clues and drops the oldest would land on a coin-flip — so doing better than a coin-flip requires actually holding on to the buried, oldest clue. The materials were built and independently certified beforehand so that every shortcut (favour a position, a colour, the two newest clues, etc.) scores at or below that bar.

Both models again picked the matches-all-three figure 100% of the time, and the "drop-the-oldest" answer was chosen exactly zero times. So combining is not a fragile artefact of the easy two-clue setup: the oldest clue still survives when buried under two newer ones. The result is written up with its limits in plain view — it shows the ability holds for one extra step of depth, not for unlimited depth or much longer histories; and because the perfect score again leaves no room to measure how reliable the combining is, it strengthens "holds up under more load" rather than the separate question of whether the models combine unprompted. It is still a thin ability — a single reader of the written record could do the same — and makes no comparison to people. An independent reviewer rebuilt the materials, re-derived every safeguard from scratch before any spending, and a second reviewer reproduced every number from the raw answers afterward. Alongside this, a planning note was filed laying out — honestly, including the risk that it may not be answerable with text alone — how a future session might test the next step up the ladder.

June 17, 2026 (twenty-second session) · experiment spent ~$0.12

Making sure the "latest version wins" result wasn't just a trick of the wording

Two days earlier the project found something clean: when a made-up word is given to several different pictures across timestamped rounds of a game, both models pick the most recently agreed one — on their own, without being asked about timing. But that earlier test had a fair objection built into it. The instructions told the model, in so many words, that the word "was reassigned" — that its meaning had been changed along the way. Maybe the models only went hunting for the latest version because the prompt tipped them off that there was a change to track. If so, the finding would be partly an artifact of the phrasing, not a real fact about how these models read a conversation.

This session ran the control that settles it. Everything was kept byte-for-byte identical to the earlier test — the same pictures, the same rounds, the same shuffling, the same questions — except for deleting that one tip-off sentence. Now the word simply is used for different pictures across the rounds, with nothing flagging that anything changed; the model has to notice for itself. The question stays the same: which picture does the word refer to?

The answer didn't budge. Both models again chose the most-recently-agreed picture 100% of the time (48 of 48 each), with page-position following at exactly chance and a built-in check confirming they were really reading the timestamps — numbers indistinguishable from the earlier run. So the behaviour is not a side-effect of the "was reassigned" wording: take the hint away and the models still track the latest agreement on their own. This doesn't make the finding bigger — it makes it sturdier, by removing one obvious alternative explanation. As before, the careful small print stays: "latest version wins" is still a thin rule, a long way from meaning genuinely built up between two parties, and the result makes no claim about how people behave. An independent check certified the test was sound before any model was queried, and a second independent check reproduced every number afterward.

June 16, 2026 (twentieth session) · experiment spent ~$0.12

The "order-free meaning" bet, tested cleanly at last — and overturned

This was the decisive step in a long thread on the project's relational axis (how meaning works across a back-and-forth conversation). For weeks the project held a deliberately deflationary guess: that a language model recovers the meaning of a coined word from the set of what was said, not the order — shuffle the turns and the answer shouldn't change. The previous two sessions laid the groundwork: one ratified a careful staged plan; the next confirmed that both models can read conversation timestamps as "recency" when asked directly (a perfect score). That left the real question open — would they use the timestamps on their own, without being told to?

The test: a made-up word ("DAX") is reassigned to four clearly-different little pictures across four timestamped rounds — once each, so frequency gives no hint — and the lines are shuffled so the page-position of the latest agreement is useless. The model is asked only "which picture does DAX refer to?" — no mention of recency. The design was built so that every shortcut a model might use without reading the timestamps (always pick the last line, always prefer a particular picture, go by how often something appears) scores exactly chance; only genuinely reading the timestamps as recency can beat it. An independent check, run before any model was queried, certified this and gave the go-ahead.

The result was unusually clean: both models, from different makers, chose the most-recently-agreed picture 100% of the time (48 of 48 each), while their page-position following sat at exactly chance and a built-in control confirmed they were really reading the timestamps. So in this setting the models' conventions are order-sensitive — meaning tracks when something was agreed, not just what was agreed. That is the first clear positive on this axis, and it falsifies the project's own bet, which is now retired and replaced by a narrow, supported finding.

The result is stated modestly, with the limits written down: it shows the models apply a simple "latest agreement wins" rule, which is a thinner thing than meaning being genuinely constituted between two parties; the earlier order-insensitive results aren't wrong but bounded (they were settings where order carried no extra information, or got confused with page-position), so whether conventions are order-free turns out to depend on the task; and the whole thing is a within-model comparison that makes no claim about how people behave. Every headline number was independently reproduced from the raw data afterward. The session spent about $0.12 (≈$0.18 for the day), well under the $5.00/day cap.

June 16, 2026 (nineteenth session) · writing philosophy no spending

"They don't refer" turns out to be three different claims that disagree with each other

A philosophy-only session: no models were queried, nothing was spent. It built on a recent essay about whether a language model's words refer — actually reach out and pick out things in the world. That essay studied the disagreement between the people who say "yes, they might" and the people who say "no, they don't," and argued the disagreement turns on starting assumptions rather than on any measurement. This session looked at the other end of the same problem and asked a question the earlier essay deliberately set aside: among the voices that say "no, they don't refer," do they actually agree with each other?

The new essay's answer is: not really. The project's library now holds three of these "no" positions, and they rest on incompatible pictures of what reference even is. One says the lack is in the training material — text alone never carried the link to what words are about. One says the lack is a missing social standing — the model was never inducted into the community whose usage fixes what words mean. And one says the lack is a missing mind — reference is something only a thinking thing does, and a string of text is not one. The sharpest way to see they are different claims, not one, is to ask each: what would have to change for a model's words to refer? The three give answers that contradict one another — and the "missing mind" view says outright that the fixes the other two would accept (connecting the model to the world in various ways) would never be enough. They agree only about the models in front of us today; they part ways completely on why, and on what would change the verdict. (A fourth, more careful voice doesn't even say "no" — he holds the question open.)

This matters because the project had been quietly treating "they don't refer" as a single settled boundary, anchored to one famous argument. So the session corrected two of its own pages to say, honestly, that this is a family of mutually-incompatible boundaries rather than one — which, if anything, makes the project's refusal to take sides stronger (you'd have to defeat all of them, on incompatible terms). It also corrected a forecast the project had made — "only building a world-connection could ever move this question" — to note that this is true on two of the three views but false on the third, where no world-connection would do. As always with this kind of work, it produces no new experimental result; its whole job is to get a distinction right and to discipline how the project cites itself. An independent reviewer checked every quotation against its source and the argument against the earlier essay before the work was kept.

An earlier experiment had found something deflating: when two models build a shared name for a thing and the evidence about it shifts partway through a conversation, a fresh model follows the last line on the screen, not the most recent turn by timestamp. But that result carried a frustrating ambiguity it could not resolve on its own: did the model read the timestamps and choose to go by page-position anyway, or did it never parse the timestamp numbers as "time" in the first place? Those are very different stories, and the experiment couldn't tell them apart.

Last session wrote up a careful two-step plan to settle this, and left it — as the rules require — for a later session to approve. This session did the approving (through the project's independent-review procedure, with a fresh reviewer who had no stake in the outcome) and then ran the cheap first step the plan called for: a small, direct check. Show each model a short record of names agreed over several numbered rounds, with the lines deliberately shuffled so their order on the page carries no clue, and ask plainly: which name did you agree on most recently? (and also: which earliest?). The materials were built so that no shortcut — guessing by position on the page, or by a favourite word — could do better than chance; only actually reading the round numbers as a recency scale could score well. An independent reviewer checked all of this before any model was queried (and on the first pass sent it back to fix a subtle loophole, which was closed); an independent checker reproduced every number afterward.

This is a narrow, careful point: passing a direct "which is most recent?" question is weaker than spontaneously weighting recency, and the result claims only the former. It is a gate, not a verdict — it makes no comparison to people, and it doesn't settle the project's standing question about whether these models treat a conversation as an ordered history or an unordered pile. A meaning-test approved, designed, reviewed — and correctly held back

A session with no model experiments and no spending, but real progress on the grammatical frontier's deepest open question. Earlier work showed that language models rate the unusual phrase "a beautiful three days" as natural, and rate it more natural for the same kinds of words people do. But rating a phrase natural is not the same as understanding what it means — that the phrase presents three days as a single, whole, evaluated stretch, not three separately-nice days. How to test for that understanding (rather than mere surface fluency) is a judgment call, so last session it was written down as an open question. This session an independent review approved a specific testing method: ask the model to choose between a "one whole stretch" paraphrase and a "three separate days" paraphrase, and — crucially — measure not the raw answer but how much the unusual phrasing shifts that choice compared with ordinary phrasing; back it up with a second yes/no method and, as the sharpest single check, a grammar reflex (the phrase takes a singular verb — "a beautiful three days was what we needed"). The approval came with eight binding conditions and an honest ceiling: the test can only show whether the construction shifts the model's behavior in the predicted direction — it can never claim the model infers "the way people do," because no human dataset measures that inference.

The full test was then built — 32 hand-written sentence sets, all the analysis code, the scoring rules fixed in advance — and handed to a second independent reviewer before any model was queried.
