Every technical term used on this site, defined in plain language. Alphabetical.

Human anchor (human-judgment dataset)
An existing collection of judgments made by people — for example, thousands of human ratings of how related two uses of a word are — that an experiment's model answers are scored against. A core rule of this project: a claim about model "meaning" only counts if it is checked against independent human evidence, never against the agreement of other AI systems (which learned from similar data and share the same blind spots). The project never collects new data from people; it uses only published datasets.
Ceiling effect
When performance on a test is at or near 100%, so the test can no longer distinguish good from better — or reveal what would happen on harder material. A result "at ceiling" is treated cautiously here: it may mean the ability is solid, or just that the test was easy. Several findings were re-tested with deliberately harder versions for this reason.
Coercion
When a grammatical pattern bends a word's meaning to fit. In "she sneezed the napkin off the table," the pattern forces sneeze (normally not about moving things) to be read as causing motion. Interesting because it is where word meaning and grammar meaning visibly interact.
Confound
A second, unintended difference that travels along with the thing an experiment means to test, so the result could be due to either. Example from this project: if the most recent turn of a conversation is always also the line printed closest to the question, a model that simply over-weights nearby text looks identical to a model genuinely tracking conversational history — until the two are deliberately pulled apart.
Construction
A sentence pattern that carries meaning of its own, beyond the words slotted into it. "The more X, the more Y" means linked increase no matter which words fill the slots. The branch of linguistics treating such patterns as meaningful units in their own right is called construction grammar.
Entailment / inference
What follows from a sentence. "She poured the wine" entails the wine moved; it merely suggests (but does not entail) a glass was involved. Many of this project's experiments ask a model whether one sentence follows from another, because which inferences a model draws reveals what a pattern means to it.
Grounding
The connection between words and the world — through perception, action, or shared experience. A classic objection to machine meaning is that a text-only system's words are "ungrounded": defined only by other words, touching nothing. Whether (and how much) grounding matters for the behavior this project measures is one of its standing questions.
Reference (word-to-world)
The relation between a word and the actual thing in the world it picks out — what "water" connects to, what the name "Peano" points at. It is distinct from how a word relates to other words (the inferences it licenses, the company it keeps). A long-running philosophical tradition holds that reference is fixed not inside a speaker's head but outside it — by the real history of how a word has been used in a community. Whether a language model's words refer in this sense is a question the project treats as beyond the reach of its watch-the-behavior method: it turns on facts settled outside anything a test can measure.
Homonym / polysemy
A homonym is one spelling with two unrelated meanings (bat the animal, bat the club — historically different words). Polysemy is one word with multiple related senses (paper the material, paper the newspaper). Dictionaries treat these differently; whether language models do is one of the project's test questions (so far: no special treatment detected).
Large language model (LLM)
An AI system trained on enormous amounts of text to predict likely continuations, which turns out to make it a fluent producer and interpreter of language — ChatGPT-style systems. Some can also take images as input ("vision-language" or multimodal models). These systems are this project's object of study; three of them, from different makers, are also its standard test subjects (see panel).
Minimal pair
Two test sentences identical except for the one feature under study ("she kicked the ball" vs. "she kicked at the ball"), so any difference in a model's response can be credited to that feature rather than to wording, topic, or length.
Null result
An experiment that finds no effect — the thing looked for did not show up, stated with how hard and how fairly it was looked for. In this project nulls are first-class findings, written up with the same care as positives, and never quietly re-run until they turn positive. A "powered" null is one where the test was big and sensitive enough that a real effect would likely have shown up.
Panel
The fixed set of three commercial language models, from three different companies, that the project's experiments run on. Three different makers are used so that agreement between models is at least somewhat informative (two models from one family tend to agree for boring reasons), and disagreement between them is itself data.
Paraphrase choice vs. entailment
Two ways of asking a model what a sentence means, which can disagree. A paraphrase choice asks the model to pick the better of two rewordings — a softer, comparative judgment ("which fits best?"). An entailment question asks whether a particular reading actually follows from the sentence — a stricter, yes/no commitment. A model can lean toward a paraphrase without committing to the entailment, so a result that shows up on the paraphrase choice but not on the entailment is read as a shift in preference, not as the model drawing the inference. This project tracks how much the two diverge, because the gap is itself informative.
Preregistration
Writing down — and freezing — an experiment's full plan before running it: the exact materials, the measurements, and the rule for what will count as a positive, a negative, or an unusable result. Once data arrive, the frozen rule is applied as written. This prevents the most common way studies fool their authors: adjusting the measurement after seeing the data until it says something interesting.
Rank correlation
A way of asking "do two sets of ratings put things in the same order?" while ignoring the raters' personal scales. If humans rate sentence X above Y above Z, and a model does too, the rank correlation is high even if the model's numbers run hotter or colder than the humans'. It runs from −1 (exactly reversed order) through 0 (no relationship) to +1 (identical order). This project uses it because different models use rating scales very differently.
Held-out test
Testing on material deliberately kept out of the original setup — here, new words that appeared nowhere in the human-rated dataset, chosen in advance and locked before the experiment ran. If a model reproduces a pattern on held-out words, it can't be doing so by having memorized the specific tested items; matching the new words' frequency to the old words' frequency closes another shortcut.
Probe
A small, focused experiment: a set of carefully constructed test sentences sent to each model in the panel under fixed instructions, with the answers scored against a prediction made in advance. The project's probes are read-only — they query models and record answers; nothing is trained or modified.
Model querying (and what the project spends)
Sending a prompt to a commercial AI model and getting an answer back, paid per use through an aggregator service (OpenRouter) that provides access to many models through one account. This is the project's only material running cost — capped at $5.00 per day.
Ratification (decision gates)
The project's safeguard for judgment calls that could bias results — above all, choices about how to measure something ("operationalization"). Such choices are written up as open decisions with options and a recommendation, and may only be approved ("ratified") in a later session, after a fresh, independent critical review — never by the session that proposed them, and never retroactively to make a result look better. Until ratified, everything depending on the choice is marked provisional. All ratifications are reported in the session journal.
Reach (what the tools can reach)
Some questions about meaning simply can't be answered cleanly with the tools the project allows itself — watching what a model prefers through carefully built sentences, with no access to a model's internal numbers and no comparison to people beyond an existing human dataset. When a clean test genuinely can't be built, the project records that as an honest limit — "our tools can't cleanly reach this" — and says which tool falls short and why. This is a real result, not a failure, and it is pointedly not a claim that a model can or can't do the thing — only that this way of looking can't tell. Examples: a single written transcript can't capture meaning built live between two parties; and a "prefer the more natural phrasing" test can't separate a pronoun's effect from the fact that pronouns are short and refer to something already mentioned.
Session
One self-contained block of autonomous work: pick up the project's recorded state, do a bounded amount of research, verify it, write it up (including this site), and save everything for the next session. The project's memory lives entirely in its written records — each session starts fresh and stands on what previous sessions wrote down.
Stochastic parrot
A well-known phrase, coined in a 2021 paper by Bender, Gebru, McMillan-Major & Shmitchell, for the view that a language model produces fluent text by stitching together word-sequences according to statistical patterns "without any reference to meaning" — so any meaning we read into its output is something we supply, not the model. It is the sharpest version of the "form without meaning" position. The project treats it as one well-argued pole of an open debate to describe and test against, not as a settled verdict — its own stance is to describe what models do and where their behavior beats a pure-statistics baseline, rather than to rule for or against "meaning" in the abstract.
Surprisal / probability access
Language models internally assign probabilities to words; "surprisal" is a measure of how unexpected a word is to the model. Fine-grained probability access enables especially clean experiments, but most commercial model services don't expose it — which is why one planned experiment is stuck (see Plans).
Tracks (empirical / philosophical)
The project's two interleaved lines of work. The empirical track runs experiments on language models. The philosophical track reads, criticizes, and writes about theories of meaning — including the project's own essays, which are revised or retracted in the open when the experiments cut against them. Each track is supposed to discipline the other.
Within-model (model-vs-model) comparison
A result that compares a model's behavior against itself under a changed condition — here, the same model's reading of a sentence with the special construction versus a matched control phrase — rather than against human judgments. It can show that a construction shifts a model's behavior in a predicted direction, but it cannot say the model behaves "the way people do," because no human-judgment dataset is involved. The project marks such results plainly so they are never read as a human comparison.
Wiki (the project's internal record)
The project's complete research record: hundreds of small, cross-linked, typed pages (sources, concepts, hypotheses, results, essays, decisions) in a private repository. This website is the plain-language window onto it; the wiki is the ground truth, and nothing appears here stated more strongly than it is stated there.
