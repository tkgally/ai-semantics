GO-WITH-CONDITIONS

- The antonymy-shadow rule is **mostly fair** as a pre-registered decision rule, but it is **not fully innocuous**: because the control already shows a strong antonymy advantage, the RANK+MARGIN rule partly bakes in the expected direction. Still, naming both **CLEARS** and **SATURATES** as first-class outcomes helps; neither is obviously “too easy,” but the rule is somewhat tailored to the anticipated antonymy pattern.
- The H1 band asymmetry is **handled honestly enough**: you explicitly admit low power, give exhaustive bands, and say this arm cannot by itself promote the claim. That’s good. The remaining issue is interpretive: a wide “REPLICATES” band on ≤4 points risks looking permissive, but you flag that.
- The frozen thresholds are **not obviously p-hacked**, but they do create a mild verdict bias: the 0.10 margin and “median among 4” criterion can steer borderline antonymy cases toward whichever named bucket best fits the anticipated residual structure. This is acceptable only because it was frozen pre-call and all outcomes are pre-named.
- Biggest methodological concern: the control result is doing a lot of work, and the “verdict-bearing” move for the antonymy-shadow arm depends on a prior calibration gate that was itself informed by the same frozen predictor finding. That’s not fatal, but it means the confirmatory content is narrower than the prose suggests.
- Nothing here obviously blocks spending money, **if** the ratified gates truly were frozen before any model outputs and the model selection/order is also frozen. If either the gate-setting or model inclusion/order was informed by preliminary outputs, then this becomes a **NO-GO**.
- If you proceed, I’d require one explicit clarification in the final report: separate **confirmatory** vs **descriptive** claims line-by-line, especially for the control-driven antonymy arm and the low-power H1 arm.
