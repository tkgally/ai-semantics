Purpose: do the fetchable groundwork while the ratified primary anchor (Clark & Wilkes-Gibbs 1986) is unfetchable for several days. Where a quote is from a secondary summary (not the primary text), that is flagged explicitly.

Part 1 — Relational / alignment literature (what is established + provenance)

1a. Clark & Wilkes-Gibbs 1986, "Referring as a collaborative process" (the RATIFIED primary anchor — still unfetched)

  • Cite: Clark, H. H. & Wilkes-Gibbs, D. (1986). Referring as a collaborative process. Cognition 22(1): 1–39. DOI 10.1016/0010-0277(86)90010-7.
  • Status: primary text NOT in repo and NOT fetched. The quotes below are from secondary summaries (Wikipedia "Collaborative model"; a Semantic Scholar / ScienceDirect abstract surfaced via WebSearch) — they are paraphrase-grade, NOT the primary text, and may NOT be quoted as primary provenance on a typed page. A PDF self-archive exists at speech.kth.se/~edlund/bielefeld/references/clark-and-wilkes-gibbs-1986.pdf (not fetched/verified this session — candidate for Tom or a future run).
  • Secondary-summary characterization (abstract, via WebSearch of ScienceDirect/Semantic Scholar): "In conversation, speakers and addressees work together in the making of a definite reference. The speaker initiates the process by presenting or inviting a noun phrase, and before going on to the next contribution, the participants, if necessary, repair, expand on, or replace the noun phrase in an iterative process until they reach a version they mutually accept." — flagged SECONDARY (search-engine abstract rendering, not verified against the PDF).
  • What it establishes (for our purposes): the canonical demonstration that dyads collaboratively coin and compress shared labels for hard-to-name figures (tangrams) over repeated rounds, via a present → accept/repair "acceptance process." It is the human convergence/compression baseline the relational pilot instantiates. The primary text is needed before any relational result promotes (per decisions/resolved/relational-anchor-shortlist).
  • Source URLs: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/0010027786900107 · https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collaborative_model · self-archive (unverified): https://www.speech.kth.se/~edlund/bielefeld/references/clark-and-wilkes-gibbs-1986.pdf

1b. Pickering & Garrod 2004, "Toward a mechanistic psychology of dialogue" (the RATIFIED theoretical backdrop)

  • Cite: Pickering, M. J. & Garrod, S. (2004). Toward a mechanistic psychology of dialogue. Behavioral and Brain Sciences 27(2): 169–190. DOI 10.1017/S0140525X04000056. PubMed 15595235.
  • Status: primary not in repo. Abstract quoted verbatim from the PubMed record (primary-abstract grade, citable as abstract-level provenance):

    "We propose a mechanistic account of dialogue, the interactive alignment account, and use it to derive a number of predictions about basic language processes. The account assumes that, in dialogue, the linguistic representations employed by the interlocutors become aligned at many levels, as a result of a largely automatic process. This process greatly simplifies production and comprehension in dialogue." "It makes use of a simple interactive inference mechanism, enables the development of local dialogue routines that greatly simplify language processing, and explains the origins of self-monitoring in production." (Verbatim, PubMed abstract: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15595235/)

  • What it establishes / why it is backdrop only: interactive alignment says dialogue partners' representations align at many levels via a largely automatic (priming-based) process — crucially, alignment here is a coordination/priming mechanism, not a claim that meaning is constituted between agents. This is exactly the deflationary frame the relational pilot must beat: alignment predicts convergence and entrainment without needing trajectory-dependence. (The PubMed abstract does not explicitly state the "no explicit mental-state modeling" claim sometimes attributed to the paper; do not quote that as primary.)

1c. Brennan & Clark 1996, "Conceptual pacts and lexical choice in conversation"

  • Cite: Brennan, S. E. & Clark, H. H. (1996). Conceptual pacts and lexical choice in conversation. Journal of Experimental Psychology: Learning, Memory, and Cognition 22(6): 1482–1493. PubMed 8921603. (Note: a separate Brennan 1996 "Lexical entrainment in spontaneous dialog" ISSD paper exists at cs.columbia.edu — different paper; don't conflate.)
  • Status: primary not in repo. Abstract quoted verbatim (via WebSearch of the PubMed/APA record) — primary-abstract grade but rendered through a search engine, so flag as abstract-grade-not-PDF-verified:

    "When people in conversation refer repeatedly to the same object, they come to use the same terms. This phenomenon, called lexical entrainment, has several possible explanations. Ahistorical accounts appeal only to the informativeness and availability of terms and to the current salience of the object's features. Historical accounts appeal in addition to the recency and frequency of past references and to partner-specific conceptualizations of the object that people achieve interactively. Evidence from 3 experiments favors a historical account and suggests that when speakers refer to an object, they are proposing a conceptualization of it, a proposal their addressees may or may not agree to." (https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8921603/)

  • Why it matters for us — this is the strongest human evidence FOR something like trajectory-dependence: the paper's headline is that lexical entrainment has a historical (not merely ahistorical/salience-driven) component, and that conventions are partner-specific — "a conceptual pact." That is the human-side analogue of the project's live-vs-shuffled discriminator: if entrainment were purely ahistorical (a function of current salience / the set of available terms), it would survive order-scrambling; Brennan & Clark argue it does not, because it is partner- and history-specific. This sharpens the pilot's hypothesis that an order-scramble control should break a genuinely constituted convention. (Strong candidate to fetch alongside / instead-of C&W-G — see anchor recommendation.)

1d. Hawkins, Frank & Goodman 2020, "Characterizing the dynamics of learning in repeated reference games" (the modern, RELEASED, FETCHABLE tangram corpus)

  • Cite: Hawkins, R. D., Frank, M. C. & Goodman, N. D. (2020). Characterizing the dynamics of learning in repeated reference games. Cognitive Science 44(6): e12845. arXiv 1912.07199. DOI 10.1111/cogs.12845.
  • Status: abstract quoted verbatim from the arXiv abstract page (primary-abstract grade). Data repo inspected directly (see Part 2).

    "We release an open corpus (>15,000 utterances) of extended dyadic interactions in a classic repeated reference game task where pairs of participants had to coordinate on how to refer to initially difficult-to-describe tangram stimuli. We find that different pairs discover a wide variety of idiosyncratic but efficient and stable solutions to the problem of reference. Furthermore, these conventions are shaped by the communicative context: words that are more discriminative in the initial context ... are more likely to persist through the final repetition. Finally, we find systematic structure in how a speaker's referring expressions become more efficient over time: syntactic units drop out in clusters following positive feedback from the listener, eventually leaving short labels containing open-class parts of speech." (Verbatim, https://arxiv.org/abs/1912.07199)

  • What it establishes: a high-resolution, released replication of the C&W-G tangram convergence dynamics: idiosyncratic-but-stable per-dyad conventions ("different pairs discover ... idiosyncratic ... solutions" = partner-specificity at corpus scale), compression over repetitions, and feedback-gated reduction. This is the single most useful fetchable human anchor for the relational pilot's entrainment/convergence baseline — see Part 2.
  • An earlier conference version (Hawkins, Frank & Goodman, CogSci 2017, "Convention-formation in iterated reference games") makes the convention-formation argument; not separately quoted here.
  • Related lineage worth noting (not fetched): Hawkins et al. 2023, "Semantic uncertainty guides the extension of conventions to new referents" (arXiv 2305.06539) — convention generalization; and the KiloGram tangram dataset (lil-lab/kilogram) — tangram stimuli/annotations, not dyadic interaction logs.

1e. Emergent-communication / referential-game line (Lazaridou et al.)

  • Cite: Lazaridou, A., Peysakhovich, A. & Baroni, M. (2017). Multi-Agent Cooperation and the Emergence of (Natural) Language. ICLR 2017. arXiv 1612.07182. (Follow-up: Lazaridou et al., ICLR 2018, "Emergence of Linguistic Communication from Referential Games with Symbolic and Pixel Input," arXiv 1705.11192.)
  • Status: characterized from the arXiv abstract / OpenReview (via WebSearch); not separately re-fetched verbatim this session — treat as characterization, not a verbatim quote.
  • What it establishes: agents trained from scratch in a sender/receiver referential game develop their own communication protocol out of the need to coordinate, and the induced "word meanings" can be pushed toward intuitive semantic properties by manipulating the game. Why it is a foil, not an anchor: this is coordination / protocol emergence among trained-from-scratch agents, which is precisely the "coordination, not meaning-constitution" literature the charter flags (meaning-senses.md §relational). It also uses arbitrary symbol vocabularies, not natural-language LLM dialogue. Useful to cite as the prior art the pilot does not duplicate, and as the deflationary baseline (convergence ≠ constitution).

1f. Recent (2023–2025) multi-agent-LLM convention / alignment work

  • Cite: Ashery, A. F., Aiello, L. M. & Baronchelli, A. (2025). Emergent social conventions and collective bias in LLM populations. Science Advances 11(20): eadu9368. arXiv 2410.08948. DOI 10.1126/sciadv.adu9368.
  • Status: abstract quoted verbatim from the arXiv abstract page (primary-abstract grade).

    "Here, we present experimental results that demonstrate the spontaneous emergence of universally adopted social conventions in decentralized populations of large language model (LLM) agents. We then show how strong collective biases can emerge during this process, even when agents exhibit no bias individually. Last, we examine how committed minority groups of adversarial LLM agents can drive social change by imposing alternative social conventions on the larger population." (Verbatim, https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.08948 / Science Advances eadu9368)

  • What it establishes: LLM populations playing a naming game spontaneously converge on shared conventions, with population-level effects (collective bias, minority-driven tipping) absent at the individual level. Why it is a foil, not an anchor: it is a naming game / coordination result — convergence on a shared label — and explicitly population-level. It does NOT separate "constituted between" from "computed within each and aggregated"; it has no order-scramble / trajectory-dependence control. It is the closest recent prior art to the pilot and the clearest example of why the project's discriminator is needed: emergent conventions are reported without any test that would distinguish constitution from aggregation. It is human-free (no human anchor), so it cannot anchor a relational claim — it is a comparison/foil.
  • A second 2025 datapoint (lower-confidence, surfaced via WebSearch only, NOT fetched verbatim): "Emergent Convergence in Multi-Agent LLM Annotation" (arXiv 2512.00047 / BlackboxNLP 2025) reports that over multi-round interaction, "codes move from dispersed to clustered in semantic space, while ROUGE increases and intrinsic dimensionality decreases, indicating lexical convergence and semantic compression." Treat as characterization only (search-snippet, not verified); same caveat — convergence, no trajectory control.

Cross-cutting takeaway for the pilot. The human literature (1a–1d) supplies a history-dependent, partner-specific picture of convention formation (Brennan & Clark's "historical account"; Hawkins et al.'s idiosyncratic stable per-dyad solutions). The computational literature (1e–1f) supplies convergence without a constitution test. The pilot's live-vs-shuffled discriminator sits exactly in the gap: it imports the human finding (history matters) as the hypothesis and the computational finding (convergence is cheap) as the null to beat.

Part 2 — Fetchable human-anchor scouting (since C&W-G 1986 is unfetchable now)

Goal: a publicly fetchable human dataset of repeated-reference / conceptual-pact dynamics that could anchor a relational pilot without new human subjects. Verdicts below come from directly inspecting the repos (GitHub contents API + raw fetch), this session.

CANDIDATE 1 (STRONGEST) — Hawkins/Stanford tangrams repeated-reference corpus

  • Repo: https://github.com/hawkrobe/tangrams ("experiment, data, and analysis code for 'Characterizing the dynamics of learning in repeated reference games'"). Pre-registration: https://osf.io/vzvmf.
  • Fetchability: CONFIRMED. data/tangrams.csv returns HTTP 200, 3,133,874 bytes at https://raw.githubusercontent.com/hawkrobe/tangrams/master/data/tangrams.csv. Also present: data/tangramsSequential.csv, data/tangramsSequential_collapsed.csv, data/tangramsUnconstrained.csv, and per-dyad raw logs under data/tangrams_sequential/{message,clickedObj}/... (145 message dirs, 175 clickedObj dirs — i.e. ordered turn-by-turn transcripts per game). curl to raw.githubusercontent works in this environment.
  • Human signal it carries (verified from the CSV header + first rows): columns include gameid, msgTime, trialNum, repetitionNum, role (director/matcher), contents (the actual referring expression), correct, numRawWords, ratePartner, nativeEnglish, taskVersion. So it carries, per dyad: the ordered sequence (msgTime) of referring expressions across repetitions of the same tangram, with role, the utterance text, success (correct), and length (numRawWords). This directly supplies (a) the entrainment/compression curve (length ↓ over repetitionNum) and (b) the ordered interaction history needed to define the live-vs-shuffled trajectory-dependence contrast — the exact human baseline the pilot's load-bearing measure compares against. >15,000 utterances per the paper abstract.
  • License: NONE DECLARED. GitHub API license: null; no LICENSE/LICENSE.md/LICENSE.txt at master or main (all 404). The paper states "We release an open corpus (>15,000 utterances)" (verbatim, arXiv 1912.07199) — i.e. authorial intent to release openly — but there is no SPDX license file in the repo. Verdict: fetchable and clearly released-for-research by authorial statement, but the missing license file is a real gap: cite-and-use-for-research is well supported; redistribution terms are unspecified. RECOMMEND emailing/confirming or relying on the published "we release an open corpus" statement + academic-use norm. This is a decisions/open style question, not a blocker for reading/comparing against the data.
  • Fit verdict: BEST fit. It is the modern, released instance of the very C&W-G paradigm the ratified anchor names; per-dyad ordered logs make it uniquely suited to the trajectory-dependence discriminator (most corpora give aggregate entrainment but not clean ordered per-dyad histories).

CANDIDATE 2 (STRONG, visually-grounded) — PhotoBook dataset (Haber et al. 2019, ACL)

  • Repo/site: https://github.com/dmg-photobook/photobook_dataset · https://dmg-photobook.github.io/datasets.html
  • Fetchability: public/unrestricted (no login/approval; direct GitHub-hosted zips for logs, image sets, and utterance-based "reference chains v1/v2" + segment-based chains). Not re-downloaded byte-for-byte this session, but the site enumerates direct public download links.
  • Human signal (verbatim from arXiv 1906.01530 abstract + ar5iv): "a large-scale collection of visually-grounded, task-oriented dialogues in English designed to investigate shared dialogue history accumulating during conversation." Statistics (verbatim, ar5iv): "2,500 dialogues collected," "164,615 utterances," "130,322 actions," "vocabulary of 11,805 unique tokens." Reuse dynamics (verbatim, ar5iv): "Once accepted, a referring expression is typically refined through shortening rather than by reformulating or adding novel information"; "interlocutors are most likely to retain the nouns and adjectives of a developing referring expression, while increasingly dropping verbs, as well as prepositions and determiners." The released Reference Chain datasets explicitly track a referent's referring expressions across a game (i.e. ordered reuse/refinement) — the common-ground / conceptual-pact signal.
  • License: NONE DECLARED in repo. GitHub API license: null; no LICENSE file; the datasets page shows no explicit license text. Same gap as Candidate 1: public download + clear research-release intent, but no SPDX terms.
  • Fit verdict: STRONG and complementary. Larger N than the tangrams corpus and visually grounded (would let the relational axis touch grounded.perceptual and connect to the multimodal axis). Slightly looser fit than tangrams for the minimal trajectory pilot because the game structure is richer (multi-image common-ground tracking, not a single-target reduction curve), and license is unspecified.

CANDIDATE 3 (corpus-grade, route description) — HCRC Map Task corpus (Anderson et al. 1991)

  • Already in wanted.md (P3) as a relational anchor option (decision Option B). Not re-verified this session for current download/license. Task is route-description, not coin-a-label-for-a-novel-figure, so the mapping to the reference-game pilot is looser (the resolved decision already notes this). Lower priority given Candidates 1–2 are confirmed fetchable.

CANDIDATE 4 (collaborative, situated) — CerealBar / CB2 (Suhr et al. 2019; Cornell lil-lab)

  • Repo: https://github.com/lil-lab/cerealbar (+ CB2, ACL 2023 demo). Two-player collaborative web game with recorded interactions released in-repo. License not verified this session.
  • Fit verdict: WEAKER for this pilot. It is collaborative instruction-following (leader/follower in a card game), not repeated-reference label coinage; the conceptual-pact / compression curve is not its central signal. Keep as a backup for a different relational angle (situated joint action / grounded.social), not the bottom-rung reference-game pilot.

DROPPED / not pursued

  • KiloGram (lil-lab/kilogram): tangram stimuli + part annotations, not dyadic interaction logs — useful as a stimulus set if the pilot needs hard-to-name figures, but it is not a human convention-formation dataset, so it does not anchor the convergence signal.
  • Spot-the-Difference / Maze corpora: not located/verified this session; deprioritized once Candidates 1–2 were confirmed.

Part 3 — Recommendation summary (for the orchestrator / Tom)

  • Best fetchable anchor: the Hawkins/Stanford tangrams corpus (hawkrobe/tangrams, data/tangrams.csv, HTTP 200, 3.1 MB, ordered per-dyad referring-expression histories with success + length over repetitions). It is the modern, released, machine-readable instance of the exact Clark & Wilkes-Gibbs paradigm the ratified anchor names — it does not replace C&W-G as the theoretical reference point, but it can supply the in-repo human convergence/compression baseline the pilot needs now, unblocking the entrainment-curve comparison while C&W-G remains unfetchable.
  • Caveat that must travel with it: no SPDX license in the repo (authorial "we release an open corpus" statement only). Surface as a decision (see return text) — reading/comparing is well within research norms; redistribution terms are unspecified.
  • Brennan & Clark 1996 is the human result that most directly motivates the trajectory-dependence discriminator (entrainment is historical + partner-specific, not ahistorical) — strong candidate to add to wanted.md as a fetch alongside C&W-G; it strengthens the pilot's hypothesis framing even though Hawkins supplies the data.
  • The 2025 multi-agent-LLM convention work (Ashery et al., Science Advances) is the prior art the pilot must position against: it reports emergent conventions with no constitution test — the gap the live-vs-shuffled control fills. Cite as foil, not anchor (human-free).
