This page catalogs Clark & Wilkes-Gibbs 1986, the originating tangram referential-communication study — the ratified empirical relational anchor for the relational-meaning pilot (decisions/resolved/relational-anchor-shortlist, Option A; queued "fetch first" at P1 in base/wanted.md). It is the human-dyad study that the modern, corpus-scale resource/hawkins-tangrams corpus replicates, and the conceptual parent of the conceptual-pacts work catalogued at source/brennan-clark-1996-conceptual-pacts. Being a study with human dyad data, it is catalogued here as a resource (human-labeled empirical asset), framed by what human data it provides and precisely what it can and cannot ground.

What it is

A theoretical proposal plus one experiment. Clark & Wilkes-Gibbs argue that making a definite reference in conversation is not a one-shot act by the speaker but a collaborative process: speaker and addressee jointly refashion a noun phrase across an iterative presentation/acceptance cycle until they mutually accept a version, minimizing their joint effort. The model is grounded in a principle of mutual responsibility. The empirical leg is the now-canonical tangram-arranging task.

The scope is deliberately speaker's reference, not semantic reference (p. 2):

"Our concern is not with semantic reference, but with speaker's reference — not, for example, with what the phrase the clown with the red nose means, but with what the speaker does in referring."

The human data (the experiment)

Design (Method, pp. 10-11). Eight pairs of students; each pair arranged 12 tangram figures on each of six trials.

"Eight pairs of partners each arranged 12 figures on each of six trials. The 12 figures, each formed from different arrangements of seven elementary shapes, were selected from a book with 4000 such figures collected by Elffers (1976) from the ancient Chinese game of Tangram." (p. 11)

The director holds the 12 cards in a target order; the matcher holds identical figures in a random order; the director must talk the matcher into reproducing the target order, and after each trial the figures are re-randomized into new orders and the procedure repeats (p. 10):

"The director's job was to get the matcher quickly and accurately to rearrange her figures to match the target ordering. They could talk back and forth as much as they needed... After they had matched their arrangements, the director's and matcher's figures were placed in two new random orders, the director's new sequence became the new target, and the procedure was repeated. They carried out the task six times, for six trials." (p. 10)

The convergence/compression result (Results, p. 12). Repeated reference to the same figure gets dramatically shorter and faster across trials:

"Figure 2 shows that directors used an average of 41 words per figure in trial 1 but only 8 words per figure in trial 6. This decline is highly significant, F(1,35) = 44.31, p < .001. The decline was steepest from trial 1 to trial 2 and had almost disappeared by trial 6." (p. 12)

"Figure 3 shows that the director averaged 3.7 turns per figure on trial 1 but only about one per figure by trial 6. This trend was also highly significant, F(1,35) = 79.59, p < .001." (p. 12)

References also shift from indefinite descriptions to definite, standardized labels across trials (p. 12):

"On trial 1 directors always described the figure, generally with an indefinite reference (e.g., a person who's...). On trials 2 through 6, in contrast, they referred to the figure with a definite description (e.g., the ice skater)." (p. 12)

The model (what the human behavior is taken to show)

The acceptance cycle (pp. 24). Reference proceeds as iterated presentation + verdict until mutual acceptance:

"The basic process, which might be called the acceptance cycle, consists of a presentation plus its verdict... A presents x and then B evaluates it. If the verdict is not positive, then A or B must refashion that presentation. That person can offer: a repair x', an expansion y, or a replacement z... Acceptance cycles apply iteratively, with one repair, expansion, or replacement after another, until a noun phrase is mutually accepted. With that, A and B take the process to be complete." (p. 24)

The principle of mutual responsibility (p. 33). The model is said to follow from a normative principle the participants take for granted:

"Principle of mutual responsibility. The participants in a conversation try to establish, roughly by the initiation of each new contribution, the mutual belief that the listeners have understood what the speaker meant in the last utterance to a criterion sufficient for current purposes." (p. 33)

The collaborative model as stated in the abstract (p. 1):

"In the model we propose, the speaker initiates the process by presenting or inviting a noun phrase. Before going on to the next contribution, the participants, if necessary, repair, expand on, or replace the noun phrase in an iterative process until they reach a version they mutually accept. In doing so they try to minimize their joint effort." (p. 1)

Mutual responsibility as a mode-defining principle (p. 35). Non-conversational modes weaken the principle, and (per the authors) this is why monologue does not show the same compression:

"In these circumstances the participants may not have full access to one another and hence cannot adhere to the principle of mutual responsibility as it has evolved for conversation." (p. 35) — and, on the contrast with non-interactive speech, "people don't shorten their repeated references as much when speaking into a tape recorder (Krauss & Weinheimer, 1966)." (p. 35)

What it can ground

  • Human collaborative referential convergence/entrainment in dyads: the core human curve — initial reference is long and effortful; repeated reference to the same referent compresses sharply (41 → 8 words/figure; 3.7 → ~1 turns/figure over six trials) and standardizes (indefinite descriptions → shared definite labels). This is the human-side phenomenon the relational pilot's compression/reuse measures contrast LLM dyads against, and the original of the paradigm replicated at corpus scale in resource/hawkins-tangrams.
  • The interactive (not solitary) source of that compression: the authors' contrast between collaborative dialogue and tape-recorder monologue (p. 35) grounds the claim that the compression is a joint-action effect, not mere within-speaker practice — directly relevant to whether an LLM dyad's behavior is "collaborative" in this sense.
  • A documented human acceptance/grounding repertoire (presentation, acceptance, rejection, postponement, repair/expansion/replacement; pp. 23-24) — concrete, human-attested interaction moves a model-side dyad probe could code for.
  • The canonical 1986 statement of the collaborative/mutual-acceptance model and the principle of mutual responsibility, citable in the project's own voice rather than via paraphrase.

What it cannot ground

  • Order-/trajectory-sensitivity of conventions per se. This is the honest limit. The paper varies repetition (trials 1-6) and re-randomizes figure positions between trials, but it does not scramble or reorder a fixed interaction history and re-test, and it makes no claim that the order in which prior references accrued (vs. their accumulated content) changes the outcome. Its human variable is convergence-over-repetition, not the live-vs-shuffled / commutativity discriminator that is the relational pilot's own (humanly unanchored) wedge — see the same caution recorded for source/brennan-clark-1996-conceptual-pacts (frequency-over-recency). It grounds human collaborative referential convergence/entrainment, not order-sensitivity of conventions.
  • Any model-side claim. Purely human data, 1986; English; eight student dyads in one laboratory matching task.
  • Population- or corpus-scale convention claims. Eight pairs, one task; for corpus-scale entrainment use resource/hawkins-tangrams.
  • Partner-specificity / partner-reset effects. The follow-on demonstration that precedents are partner-indexed is in source/brennan-clark-1996-conceptual-pacts, not here; this 1986 paper does not switch partners.

Anchor status

Marked anchor: candidate: this is a candidate human anchor for the relational line — specifically for the convergence/entrainment (compression + reuse) leg of the relational-meaning pilot, now in-repo and verified. It does not anchor any order-sensitivity / commutativity result; those remain humanly unanchored by design. Promotion of a relational pilot result to a human-comparison claim using this anchor should cite the specific human curve (pp. 12) and respect the "what it cannot ground" limits above.
