Goldberg 1995 — Constructions: A Construction Grammar Approach to Argument Structure

What it is

The charter-core primary for the project's constructional wedge: Adele E. Goldberg, Constructions: A Construction Grammar Approach to Argument Structure, Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 1995. It is the first book-length statement of what became Cognitive Construction Grammar — the thesis that the basic unit of grammatical knowledge is the construction, a conventionalized form–meaning pairing not fully predictable from its parts, and that argument-structure patterns (the ditransitive, the caused-motion construction, the resultative, the way-construction) are themselves meaningful, carrying content independently of the verbs slotted into them. The book is the direct upstream of this project's caused-motion / way-construction / coercion probe lines (concept/coercion already attributes those examples to Goldberg 1995, chs. 7 and 9) and the source the two CxG concept pages name as "Goldberg 1995; not in-repo." Frame semantics (Fillmore — see source/fillmore-1982-frame-semantics) is the semantic backbone Goldberg builds on; the two pages are companions on the lexical↔grammatical link.

Quotes

All quotes are carried via the fetched secondaries named above; the 1995 primary was not read. Each page number is the locator the secondary attributes to Goldberg 1995.

The definition of a construction (via Boas 2021 draft, attributed "Goldberg 1995: 4")

The book's canonical definition, reproduced verbatim by the Boas 2021 CxG-and-Frame-Semantics draft:

"C is a CONSTRUCTION iffdef C is a form-meaning pair such that some aspect of Fi or some aspect of Si is not strictly predictable from C's component parts or from other previously established constructions"

(Quoted via Boas 2021 draft, which gives the locator "(Goldberg 1995: 4)"; the primary was not consulted for this fragment. The "iffdef" spelling is the secondary's rendering of Goldberg's "iff" subscript-def notation; a citing session quoting at length should re-verify against the 1995 original.)

The constructional-network "lattice" claim (via Boas "Cognitive Construction Grammar" chapter, attributed "Goldberg 1995: 5")

That the inventory of constructions is structured, not a list of independent irregularities:

"highly structured lattice of interrelated information"

and that constructions

"display prototype structures and form networks of associations"

(Both quoted via the Boas CCxG chapter, attributed "(Goldberg 1995: 5)"; primary not consulted.)

Profiled participant roles (via Boas CCxG chapter, attributed "Goldberg 1995: 44")

Boas quotes Goldberg's characterization of "profiled" arguments — entities in a verb's semantics that are

"obligatorily accessed and function as focal points within the scene, achieving a special degree of prominence"

(Quoted via the Boas CCxG chapter, attributed "(Langacker 1987, cf. Goldberg 1995: 44)"; primary not consulted. Boas's own attribution couples it with Langacker.)

Role fusion / construal (via Boas CCxG chapter, attributed "Goldberg 1995: 50")

On how a verb's participant roles fuse with a construction's argument roles:

"Whether a role can be construed as an instance of another role is determined by general categorization principles."

(Quoted via the Boas CCxG chapter, attributed "(Goldberg 1995: 50)"; primary not consulted.)

The caused-motion examples (via Boas CCxG chapter, attributed "Goldberg 1995: 152")

The textbook coercion sentences the project's caused-motion probe adopts, listed by Boas as Goldberg's examples:

"a. They laughed the poor guy out of the room. b. Frank sneezed the tissue off the table. c. Mary urged Bill into the house."

(Quoted via the Boas CCxG chapter, attributed "(Goldberg 1995: 152)"; primary not consulted. Boas additionally locates the Caused-Motion Construction diagram at "Goldberg 1995: 163" and the agent/natural-force causer constraint at "Goldberg 1995: 165," and the way-construction discussion at "Goldberg 1995: 199–218" — locators only, not quoted here.)

The thesis that constructions carry meaning (secondary-attributed, p. 1 — weaker provenance)

The book's opening thesis, as reproduced on the IIT-Kanpur excerpt page (attributed p. 1; OCR-slip "lhat"→"that" silently corrected, and so flagged):

"A central thesis of this work is that basic sentences of English are instances of constructions — form-meaning correspondences that exist independently of particular verbs."

"it is argued that constructions themselves carry meaning, independently of the words in the sentence."

(Carried via the IIT-Kanpur "Book Excerptise" page, which does not state firsthand transcription and shows an OCR slip; corroborated in substance — not verbatim — by the Boas chapters' framing of Goldberg 1995. Treat as a reliably-attributed thesis statement, not a verified verbatim p. 1 quote.)
