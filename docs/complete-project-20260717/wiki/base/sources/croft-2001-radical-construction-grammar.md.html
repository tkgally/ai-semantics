Croft 2001 — Radical Construction Grammar: Syntactic Theory in Typological Perspective

What it is

The typological, non-reductionist wing of Construction Grammar: William Croft, Radical Construction Grammar: Syntactic Theory in Typological Perspective, Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2001 (Oxford Studies in Language Typology and Linguistic Theory; 448 pp.). Where source/goldberg-1995-constructions builds Cognitive Construction Grammar around the thesis that argument-structure patterns are themselves meaningful, and source/goldberg-2006-constructions-at-work adds the usage-based account of how constructional generalizations are learned, Croft 2001 pushes the constructionist commitment to its strongest form by bringing linguistic typology to bear on it. Its signature theses, in Croft's own restatement, are: (a) constructions as wholes are the primitive/basic units of syntactic representation; (b) syntactic categories (noun, verb, subject, object, adjective) are not universal building blocks but are derived from constructions — defined by their distribution in (roles of) constructions, and therefore both language-specific and construction-specific; (c) the theory is grounded in a cross-linguistic / typological methodology — the very high degree of morphosyntactic variation within and across languages is what motivates abandoning a universal inventory of categories; and (d) it is non-reductionist about internal syntactic structure — the only internal structure a construction has is the part–whole relation between the whole and its construction elements (CEs), with no syntactic relations (constituency, dependency) posited as independent primitives.

For this project's concept/constructional-meaning wedge, Croft 2001 is the source the concept page already names ("Goldberg 1995, 2006; Croft 2001; Fillmore et al.; not in-repo") for the constructions-as-basic-units thesis. It sharpens the wedge in a specific way: where Goldberg's argument-structure constructions still presuppose categories like Verb and Object as slot labels, Croft's radical move is that those labels are themselves construction-derived, so a probe asking "does a model track the construction's constraint structure?" cannot lean on a fixed universal category inventory to define the construction — the construction comes first, the categories after. The two meaning-senses tags reflect this: constructional (form–function pairings are the basic units) and distributional (categories are defined by distributional analysis — occurrence in roles of constructions — which is the same Firth/Harris-lineage method the project's distributional tag names, here turned on syntactic categories rather than word meaning).

Quotes

All quotes below are verbatim from Croft's self-archived "Radical Construction Grammar" author manuscript (fetched, extracted), unless otherwise flagged. The 2001 book body was not read; the parenthetical book locators are Croft's own attributions inside the manuscript.

The three principles, stated together (manuscript, Introduction)

Croft's own compact statement of the theory's posited minimum of universal syntactic structure:

"First, constructions as wholes are the basic unit of syntactic analysis. Syntactic categories are not building blocks of constructions; they are instead defined by their distribution in constructions, which varies from one construction to the next. Second, the internal syntactic structure of constructions is made up of only the part-whole relation of the construction's form to its roles or elements. Third, constructions are language-specific, that is, there are no universal constructions based on their syntactic properties."

(Verbatim from the manuscript's Introduction. This is Croft's own restatement of the 2001 book's core; the book pages were not read.)

Constructions as primitive units; categories as derivative (manuscript, "Representing language form and function")

The signature reversal — constructions first, categories after:

"In Radical Construction Grammar, constructions are the primitive units of syntax. Complex constructions have roles (CEs). Syntactic categories are equivalent to construction roles, defined by their distribution in the construction and therefore derivative concepts. Since constructions are not built up from a universal or even language-specific set of building block categories, the inference of grammatical patterns in a language and grammatical universals across languages is based on other grounds: usage and function."

(Verbatim from the manuscript. "CEs" = construction elements, the manuscript's term for a construction's parts/roles.)

The two rejected assumptions — the "building block model" and the "skeleton model" (manuscript, body)

Croft names the two traditional assumptions Radical Construction Grammar rejects:

"The first is that a construction is defined by its combination of CEs… This is the building block model of individual language constructions… The second is that these same CEs—Subject, Object, Verb, Adjective, etc.—are part of a universal inventory available to all languages for building constructions. This is the skeleton model of language universals, sometimes called 'Universal Grammar'…"

and the resolution of the resulting circularity (the non-reductionist/anti-circularity move):

"In the building block model, constructions are defined by the categories, i.e. roles or CEs, that they consist of. But in the distributional method, the categories are defined by the constructions they occur in. Assuming both the building block model and the distributional method leads to circular argumentation. A consistent theory must discard one or the other… Radical Construction Grammar abandons the building block and skeleton models."

(Both verbatim from the manuscript. Ellipses mark elisions of intervening clauses; no words inside the quoted runs are altered.)

Distributional analysis defines categories (manuscript, body)

The method by which categories are derived — the bridge to this page's distributional tag:

"This method for defining categories is called distributional analysis: define syntactic categories by their occurrence (distribution) in (roles of) constructions of the language."

and the empirical finding that motivates the radical conclusion:

"However, if one does distributional analysis across multiple constructions, one finds that the syntactic categories they define are not the same."

(Both verbatim from the manuscript. The manuscript locates the "methodological opportunism" critique of single-construction category tests at "Croft 2001, chapter 1.")

The non-reductionist principle on internal structure (manuscript, "The internal structure of constructions")

That constructions have no internal syntactic relations beyond part–whole:

"Radical Construction Grammar's second major principle is that the internal morphosyntactic structure of constructions consists simply of the part-whole relation between the construction as a whole and the CEs or roles that constitute its parts. The internal morphosyntactic structure does not include syntactic relations between CEs, such as constituency or dependency."

(Verbatim from the manuscript. The manuscript attributes the cross-linguistic argument against syntactic relations to "Croft 2001, chapter 6" and "Croft 2001:185-197," and the Warlpiri indexation case to "Croft 2001:226-32"; locators only, not read from the book.)

Language-specificity, restated in the conclusion (manuscript, Conclusion)

"Constructions, and the categories defined by the roles that make them up, are language-specific entities that are created when speakers verbalize their experience in language use."

(Verbatim from the manuscript's Conclusion.)

Bearing on this project

  • concept/constructional-meaning. That page names "Goldberg 1995, 2006; Croft 2001; Fillmore et al.; not in-repo" as the source of the construction-as-basic-unit thesis. This page upgrades the Croft 2001 citation to a sourced (author-manuscript-attributed) provenance: the verbatim "constructions are the primitive units of syntax… Syntactic categories are… derivative concepts," so a concept/essay page can cite Croft's thesis rather than gesture at it — as an attribution carried by Croft's own self-archived restatement, never as a primary reading of the book.
  • The radical sharpening of the wedge. Croft's contribution over Goldberg's is the claim that the category labels a construction is described with (Verb, Object, Adjective) are themselves construction-derived and language-specific, not universal primitives. For a probe of a model's constructional knowledge this is a methodological caution: a construction's "slots" cannot be taken as fixed universal categories the model is being tested on — the construction is prior. It bears directly on the project's "live tension" (where form ends and constructional meaning begins) by relocating part of the form side itself into construction-derived, distributionally-defined categories.
  • The distributional hinge. Croft's "distributional analysis" — defining categories by occurrence in construction roles — is the same Firth/Harris-lineage distributional method the project's distributional tag names (see meaning-senses.md), applied to syntax. This is a second, independent place (alongside source/goldberg-2006-constructions-at-work's usage-based turn) where the constructional and distributional wedges meet: in RCG, what a category is is a distributional fact about constructions.
  • Typology as the evidence base. RCG's force comes from cross-linguistic variation (Adjective categories across English/Big Nambas/Japanese; voice constructions across English/Karo Batak/Maasai/etc.). This is human-language typological evidence, not a project resource — see "What it cannot ground."

What it can ground

  • The reliably-attributed wording of Croft's three principles of Radical Construction Grammar — constructions as primitive units, categories as construction-derived and language-specific, and internal structure as part–whole only — as attributions carried by Croft's own self-archived manuscript, suitable for a concept/essay page that says "Croft's Radical Construction Grammar holds that…," never for a claim resting on a primary reading of the 2001 book.
  • The non-reductionist / anti-circularity argument (building-block model + distributional method = circular; RCG discards the building-block and skeleton models), in Croft's own manuscript wording — usable to frame the methodological point that a constructional probe should not presuppose a fixed universal category inventory.
  • The historical-conceptual placement of the 2001 book as the typological wing of Construction Grammar, alongside the Goldberg pages and Fillmore's frame semantics (concept/constructional-meaning, concept/frame-and-prototype-semantics).

What it cannot ground

  • Anything requiring the 2001 book's own full text, its actual chapter arguments verbatim, or a verified book page for any of the locators Croft cites in the manuscript (81-82, 185-197, 226-32, 313, chapters 1/6/8): the book was not read here. A finding needing an exact book passage with a confirmed 2001 page must reach the primary (a library copy or a legitimately readable preview) first.
  • Any LLM/empirical claim — this is NOT a human anchor. Like the other theory primaries (the two Goldberg pages, Fillmore, Firth), it grounds a theory a model may instantiate, never a result about a model. Its empirical content is about human language typology — the cross-linguistic distribution of categories and constructions — drawn from the descriptive-grammar and typological literature the book cites; that is not a project resource and confers no anchor here. A result comparing an LLM's behavior to human category/construction judgements still needs its own resource page (a treebank, a typological database, an acceptability set), not this source.
  • The Goldberg branch's specific claims: RCG is a distinct, typology-driven variety of CxG. Do not cite a Goldberg argument-structure-construction claim from this page or a Croft language-specific-categories claim from the Goldberg pages; the constructionist family shares the form–meaning-pairing core but diverges on universality of categories.
