Falkum & Vicente 2015 — "Polysemy: Current perspectives and approaches"

What it is

The editorial introduction (a substantive survey, pp. 1–16, not a one-page preface) by Ingrid Lossius Falkum (Centre for the Study of Mind in Nature, IFIKK, University of Oslo) and Agustín Vicente (Ikerbasque – Basque Foundation for Science / University of the Basque Country) to a 2015 special issue of Lingua (vol. 157) devoted to polysemy. DOI 10.1016/j.lingua.2015.02.002; available online 11 March 2015. It is a cross-disciplinary map of the polysemy literature — linguistic, philosophical, psycholinguistic, and formal/computational — built around the distinction of polysemy from monosemy on one side and homonymy on the other, and around the internal carving of polysemy into regular/logical vs. irregular/accidental kinds.

This is a survey/reference source about human language. It makes no claim about LLMs and supplies no human-annotated dataset. The project uses it only as an a-priori interpretive frame (see What it grounds).

Open-access basis. The verbatim text below was extracted from the author self-archived full-text PDF hosted by PhilPapers/PhilArchive (https://philpapers.org/archive/FALPCP.pdf; record https://philarchive.org/rec/FALPCP), with the same full text mirrored at the University of Oslo institutional repository (https://www.hf.uio.no/ifikk/.../falkum-vicente_2015.pdf) — a green-OA / author-postprint basis, freely readable. The publisher version on ScienceDirect is paywalled and was not used. (The PhilPapers/PhilArchive and UiO HTML landing pages returned HTTP 403/401 to automated fetching, but the linked PDF itself is openly retrievable; its text was extracted locally.)

Why it bears on this project (the regular/systematic-polysemy gap)

The companion ingest source/sennet-2021-ambiguity-sep grounded the basic polysemy vs. homonymy carving the project's lexical wedge rests on, but explicitly did not develop regular / systematic / logical (Apresjan-style) polysemy — that page's own What it cannot ground flags "Regular / systematic / Apresjan-style polysemy … remains open (a dedicated Apresjan / regular-polysemy OA source would be a separate ingestion)." The essay essay/ambiguity-kind-not-level and the concept page concept/polysemy lean on a kind-of-ambiguity axis (related vs. unrelated senses; a nearer pole vs. none) without an in-repo source for the patterned, productive sub-kind of relatedness. This source fills exactly that gap: it states the Apresjan criterion for regular polysemy, gives the canonical productive patterns (animal-for-meat, container-for-contents, artist-for-works, institution/building), distinguishes regular from irregular polysemy (and both from homonymy), ties regularity to metonymy and to rule-based productivity, and — important for the project's honesty discipline — records that the regular/irregular boundary is itself not clear-cut.

Note on where regular polysemy sits on the project's scalar-vs-disjunctive axis: regular polysemy is a patterned species of the scalar/related pole (related senses, with a nearer pole), distinguished by the senses' membership in a productive cross-lexical pattern rather than by being more or less related per se. The source is careful that this pattern-membership is gradient too (degrees of irregularity; degrees to which a pattern "loosely constrains" its extended sense), which reinforces, rather than complicates, the essay's gradience framing.

Key passages (verbatim, whitespace-normalized as declared)

The internal carving — regular/logical vs. irregular/accidental (§1 Preliminaries) — the load-bearing passage for this project (independently double-confirmed):

"It is customary in the literature to distinguish between regular or logical polysemy, on the one hand, and irregular or accidental polysemy, on the other (Apresjan, 1974; Asher, 2011; Pustejovsky, 1995)."

Irregular polysemy defined, with the run example (§1; independently double-confirmed):

"Irregular polysemy, on the other hand, is described by Apresjan (1974:16) as cases where the semantic distinction between the meanings ai and aj for a word A cannot be found in any other word of the given language."

"The English verb run may be an example of this: its different senses in run a mile, run a shop, run late, run on gasoline, etc. seem idiosyncratic to this particular lexical item, and may each have arisen as a result of different lexical semantic or pragmatic processes, such as for instance specification, loosening, metaphorical extension, and so on."

Logical polysemy as a sub-class of regular polysemy, defined by the diagnostic tests (footnote 1; independently double-confirmed):

"It is also possible to distinguish regular from logical polysemy, logical polysemy being a subclass of regular polysemy, which is operationally defined as polysemy which passes the conjunction and anaphoric reference tests (Asher, 2011)."

Regular polysemy as rule-generated and productive (the formal/computational treatment), §2:

"regular polysemy of this kind is typically analysed as being generated by lexical rules, in this way accounting for the productivity and cross-linguistic availability of the patterns of sense extension, while avoiding a listing of all senses for the words in question (Asher and Lascarides, 2003; Copestake and Briscoe, 1995; Gillon, 1992; Kilgarriff, 1992; Ostler and Atkins, 1992; Pustejovsky, 1995)."

The regular/irregular boundary is itself not crisp (the honesty caveat the project should carry forward), §2:

"However, the distinction between regular and irregular polysemy is not clear-cut either. As to irregular polysemy, there appears to be degrees of irregularity, with some cases being clearly idiosyncratic, and others constrained by the way meaning chains tend to develop (Sweetser, 1990; Taylor, 2003)."

Systematic patterns are productive and cross-linguistic (reporting Srinivasan & Rabagliati's contribution to the volume), §3 — clean opening clause only:

"Srinivasan and Rabagliati investigated a large number of systematic polysemy patterns found in English"

(— the inline example list immediately following, 'animal for meat', 'container for content', etc., is column-scrambled in the extraction and is reported as paraphrase, not quoted; see Known limits.)

The reference as the paper itself prints it (bibliography):

"Apresjan, J.D., 1974. Regular polysemy. Linguistics 14 (2), 5--32."

(Reproduced verbatim as printed in this source. The project does not independently assert Apresjan's pagination; note this differs from the volume/issue formatting 12(142) sometimes cited elsewhere — what is certified here is only how this source cites it.)

Paraphrased (NOT verbatim — column-scrambled in extraction)

  • Apresjan's positive criterion for regular polysemy. The source attributes to Apresjan (1974:16) the criterion that the polysemy of a word A with senses ai and aj is regular if there is at least one other word B with senses bi and bj that are semantically distinguished from each other in exactly the same way as ai and aj. (Reconstruction consistent across the PDF and indexed mirrors, but the exact in-line word order could not be certified from the PDF's two-column extraction, so it is given as paraphrase.)
  • The canonical English example patterns. The source illustrates regular polysemy with: animal terms denoting either the animal or its meat (chicken, rabbit, turkey); container terms denoting either the container or its contents (drinking "the whole bottle/glass/mug"); artists' names denoting their works (a Picasso); and the multiple senses of school ('building', 'institution', etc.). (The sentence carrying these examples was column-interleaved in extraction; reported as paraphrase.)

What it grounds in this project (relevance)

  • concept/polysemy — adds the regular/systematic sub-kind the page did not previously cite. The concept page treats polysemy as graded relatedness with bridging cases but says nothing about patterned, productive sense extension shared across many words. This source supplies an in-repo, open-access statement of the regular (Apresjan) vs. irregular carving, of regular polysemy as rule-generated and productive, and of its tie to metonymy — sharpening the page's "related senses" notion into "related by a productive cross-lexical pattern." It does not displace the still-wanted canonical monographs (Cruse 1986, Murphy 2003, Lyons 1977, all wanted); it is a survey editorial, not a lexicology text.
  • essay/ambiguity-kind-not-level — discharges the honesty-box want. That essay's honesty box names "a future ingest of a semantics/lexicography source on the polysemy-vs-homonymy / regular-vs-irregular ambiguity distinction" as a grounding it lacks. This source is exactly that: it grounds the regular-vs-irregular axis the essay's kind-of-ambiguity framing presupposes, and it independently corroborates the essay's gradience commitment by recording that the regular/irregular line is "not clear-cut either." It grounds the framing, not the essay's empirical re-reading (which is internal-contrast-only and asserts no human comparison).

What it cannot ground

  • Any empirical claim about LLMs. A human-language survey with no labeled resource. It cannot serve as the anchors: resource for any claim or result, and it bears on the framing of the regular/systematic-polysemy distinction, not on any model behavior.
  • A graded measurement instrument. It supplies the conceptual carving (regular vs. irregular; the productive patterns), not a graded human-rated scale or annotations. The wedge's graded measurement still comes from the usage-similarity resources catalogued at resource/wic-graded-usage-similarity (DWUG / WiC), not from here.
  • A settled formal partition. The source itself states the regular/irregular boundary is "not clear-cut" and notes "degrees of irregularity"; treat the carving as a recognized, useful frame, not a sharp rule.

Map/reference, not a human anchor

This page is a reference/survey source. It grounds framing — the regular (Apresjan) vs. irregular polysemy distinction, productive pattern-based sense extension, and the metonymy connection — that the project's lexical wedge and the kind-of-ambiguity essay presuppose. It makes no empirical claim about LLMs, supplies no human-annotated resource, and cannot serve as the anchors: resource for any claim or result.
