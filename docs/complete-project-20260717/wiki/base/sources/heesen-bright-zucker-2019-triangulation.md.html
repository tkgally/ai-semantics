Source: Heesen, Bright & Zucker (2019), "Vindicating methodological triangulation"

Citation. Remco Heesen, Liam Kofi Bright & Andrew Zucker, "Vindicating methodological triangulation," Synthese 196(8): 3067–3081 (2019). Open access, Creative Commons Attribution (CC BY 4.0).

What the paper argues (abstract-level)

The paper concerns a methodological dispute in the social sciences: which method is appropriate for a given research question is often itself contested. Against that uncertainty, the authors reconstruct and defend a position they attribute to W. E. B. Du Bois — methodological triangulation — and contrast it with methodological purism.

  • The triangulation thesis. Use multiple methods at once; agreement across them confirms more strongly than any single method could. In the abstract's words: "This is to use multiple methods simultaneously in the belief that, where one is uncertain about the reliability of any given method, if multiple methods yield the same answer that answer is confirmed more strongly than it could have been by any single method."

  • Purism, the foil. "Against this, methodological purists believe that one should choose a single appropriate method and stick with it."

  • The diffidence assumption. The vindication is conditional on the scientist being uncertain about which method is best: triangulation beats purism "assuming the scientist is subject to some degree of diffidence about the relative merits of the various methods."

  • The voting-theory result. "Using tools from voting theory, we show Du Boisian methodological triangulation to be more likely to yield the correct answer than purism, assuming the scientist is subject to some degree of diffidence about the relative merits of the various methods."

  • The striking corollary. The agreement-is-confirmatory result does not require the methods to be individually reliable: "This holds even when in fact only one of the methods is appropriate for the given research question."

Verbatim abstract (CC BY 4.0; PhilSci-Archive, double-fetch-confirmed)

"Social scientists use many different methods, and there are often substantial disagreements about which method is appropriate for a given research question. In response to this uncertainty about the relative merits of different methods, W. E. B. Du Bois advocated for and applied 'methodological triangulation'. This is to use multiple methods simultaneously in the belief that, where one is uncertain about the reliability of any given method, if multiple methods yield the same answer that answer is confirmed more strongly than it could have been by any single method. Against this, methodological purists believe that one should choose a single appropriate method and stick with it. Using tools from voting theory, we show Du Boisian methodological triangulation to be more likely to yield the correct answer than purism, assuming the scientist is subject to some degree of diffidence about the relative merits of the various methods. This holds even when in fact only one of the methods is appropriate for the given research question."

What this source can ground in-repo

It is the project's external grounding for variety-of-evidence / triangulation inference — the principle that agreement across genuinely different methods confirms more strongly than a single method. It bears on essay/cross-level-convergence-ladder, where the R1 (each leg under its own instrument) → R2 (one instrument equalized across levels) step is the project's behavioral specialization of the variety-of-evidence idea. The paper's diffidence assumption and its requirement that the multiple methods be genuinely different map onto the ladder's standing caveat that shared priors are not independent witnesses.

Honest limits of the mapping. (1) The paper vindicates triangulation under diffidence — agreement is confirmatory even if only one method is in fact appropriate — which locates the confirmatory bar more permissively than the ladder's deflationary R2 floor; the relationship to the ladder is therefore a methodology analogy / confirmation of the family of inference, not a derivation of the ladder's rungs. (2) Provenance is abstract-level only (above), so the modelling assumptions behind the voting-theory result are not quoted or relied on here. (3) No human-comparison claim about LLMs is made on this page; it is a methodology source.
