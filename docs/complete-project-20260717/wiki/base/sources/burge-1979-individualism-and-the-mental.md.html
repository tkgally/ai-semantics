Burge 1979 — Individualism and the Mental

What it is

The canonical statement of social externalism (anti-individualism) about mental content. Tyler Burge, "Individualism and the Mental," Midwest Studies in Philosophy, vol. 4 (1979), pp. 73-121. Where Putnam's externalism is built on natural-kind terms and the physical environment (Twin Earth, H₂O vs. XYZ), Burge's runs on ordinary words via a thought experiment — the "arthritis" case — in which two physically and phenomenally identical individuals have different-content thoughts solely because their surrounding linguistic communities use a word differently. The conclusion generalizes externalism from reference to the contents of propositional attitudes themselves, and from natural kinds to "an artifact term, an ordinary natural kind word, a color adjective, a social role term, a term for a historical style, an abstract noun, an action verb, a physical movement verb, or any of various other sorts of words" (p. 79). It is the social-externalist companion the project's concept/referential-meaning page had listed only second-hand ("Burge's social externalism … the arthritis case … not in-repo; characterization") as a co-source of the referential.externalist sub-tag. This page supplies the verbatim primary.

A source page is NOT a human anchor. This is a 1979 philosophy essay; it grounds the concept of social externalism / anti-individualism about content, not any empirical result about any model, and it licenses no human-comparison claim. It grounds the theory a model's content-relation would be judged against, never a result. Burge predates LLMs by decades.

The target: anti-individualism, and the framing of the essay

Burge frames the whole essay against the "individualistic" tradition (Cartesian and behaviorist) that locates mental content in the isolated individual, and for the "Hegelian" social side that has had "considerably less clear-headed philosophical attention" (p. 73). His thesis (p. 74):

"I shall offer some considerations that stress social factors in descriptions of an individual's mental phenomena. These considerations call into question individualistic presuppositions of several traditional and modern treatments of mind." (p. 74)

The argument concerns propositional attitudes specified by content clauses (that-clauses) — "Thoughts, beliefs, intentions" (p. 74) — not merely the reference of a term, which is what makes it the content-externalist (not just reference-externalist) companion to Putnam.

The three-step thought experiment (the "arthritis" case)

Burge sets it out explicitly as a three-step structure (p. 77):

"We now turn to a three-step thought experiment. Suppose first that: A given person has a large number of attitudes commonly attributed with content clauses containing 'arthritis' in oblique occurrence." (p. 77)

Step 1 — the actual case. The subject has many true arthritis-beliefs (it is painful, it is in his wrists and ankles, stiffening joints are a symptom, etc.) "In addition to these unsurprising attitudes, he thinks falsely that he has developed arthritis in the thigh" (p. 77). He reports this fear; the doctor corrects him, "since arthritis is specifically an inflammation of joints. Any dictionary could have told him the same" (pp. 77-78). The error is conceptual: arthritis is by definition a disease of the joints, so "arthritis in the thigh" is not a possible state of affairs — yet the subject genuinely thinks it.

Step 2 — the counterfactual twin. "We are to conceive of a situation in which the patient proceeds from birth through the same course of physical events that he actually does" (p. 77): same physiology, same diseases, same internal physical occurrences, "the same sensory intake (physiologically described)", the same word-forms heard at the same times, the same dispositions, the same "non-intentional, phenomenal experience … the same pains, visual fields, image[s], and internal verbal rehearsals" (p. 78). Exactly one thing differs (p. 78):

"The counterfac[tua]lity in the supposition touches only the patient's social environment. In actual fact, 'arthritis', as used in his community, does not apply to ailments outside joints. … But in our imagined case, physicians, lexicographers, and informed laymen apply 'arthritis' not only to arthritis but to various other rheumatoid ailments." (p. 78)

[The bracket marks the file's "counterfacrudity" extraction artifact, here restored to "counterfactuality"; the rest is verbatim.]

Step 3 — the interpretation. In the counterfactual community the word "does not mean arthritis" (p. 79), so the twin cannot have arthritis-thoughts at all: "In the counterfactual situation, the patient lacks some — probably all — of the attitudes commonly attributed with content clauses containing 'arthritis' in oblique occurrence" (pp. 78-79). The payoff (p. 79):

"The difference in his mental contents is attributable to differences in his social environment." (p. 79)

So two individuals identical "considered in isolation from their social context" (p. 79) differ in the contents of their thoughts — content depends on the communal/linguistic environment, not on the individual alone.

Incomplete understanding (the load-bearing premise)

What makes the experiment run is that a subject can think with a notion he only partially grasps (p. 79):

"[T]he thought experiment … [gets] under way in any case where it is intuitively possible to attribute a mental state or event whose content involves a notion that the subject incompletely understands. As will become clear, this possibility is the key to the thought experiment." (p. 79)

Burge stresses that this is not deviant but normal: "One need only thumb through a dictionary for an hour or so to develop a sense of the extent to which one's beliefs are infected by incomplete understanding" (p. 79); "'Brisket', 'contract', 'recession', 'sonata', 'deer', 'elm' …" are listed as analogous cases (p. 84). The subject's arthritis-thoughts are genuinely about arthritis — anchored by the community's correct usage — despite his own faulty grasp.

Deference and the communal anchoring of content

The mechanism is deference to the community's (and its experts') usage — Burge's generalization of Putnam's division of linguistic labor from reference to attitude-content (p. 80):

"Much of our vocabulary is taken over from others who, being specialists, understand our terms better than we do. The use of scientific terms by laymen is a rich source of cases." (p. 80)

And, decisively, communal usage co-fixes content even for words the subject fully understands — the "reversed" version of the experiment (p. 85):

"[C]ommunal practice is a factor (in addition to my understanding, inference patterns, and perhaps behavior, physical activity, and other features) in fixing the contents of my attitudes, even in cases where I fully understand the content." (p. 85)

Burge's summary image (p. 84):

"Social context infects even the distinctively mental features of mentalistic attributions. No man's intentional mental phenomena are insular. Every man is a piece of the social continent, a part of the social main." (p. 84)

The scope is wide: the argument "does not depend … on the kind of word 'arthritis' is" and extends to artifact terms, color adjectives, social-role terms, abstract nouns, action verbs, and more (p. 79).

Bearing on this project

  • concept/referential-meaning — the social-externalist half of the referential.externalist sub-tag. That page names "Burge's social externalism (the arthritis case — a speaker's word can mean what the surrounding community's experts make it mean, even against the speaker's own partial grasp)" but flagged it "not in-repo; characterization" alongside Evans 1973. This page supplies the verbatim primary with locators: the three-step structure (pp. 77-79), the social-determination conclusion (p. 79), incomplete understanding (p. 79), deference to specialists (p. 80), and communal practice fixing content even under full understanding (pp. 84-85). The link is supports (this source makes the concept page's externalist characterization more secure). Burge is in some respects stronger than Putnam for the project: he covers ordinary words, not only natural kinds, and externalizes the content of attitudes, not only the reference of a term — so the community-and-deference dependency is even more general.
  • The community-membership antecedent the in-repo essays turn on (bears directly; no typed link added here, the interpretation lives on those pages). essay/reference-as-premise-bound locates the whole in-repo dispute over LLM reference in one antecedent — whether a system trained on a community's pooled corpus counts as "part of the linguistic community" whose use fixes reference/content — and argues that antecedent is settled off-board, so no behavioral probe reaches it. Burge makes that antecedent even more load-bearing than Putnam does, because on his account it is the community (and deference to it) that fixes attitude content, not just word-reference. The textual evidence cuts both ways and is not decided here (the orchestrator decides):
  • Toward "could count": Burge's bar for having a content-bearing thought is strikingly low on the individual side — the subject may grasp the notion incompletely (p. 79), even misapply it (the "arthritis in the thigh" belief is false and conceptually confused), and still think with the communal notion, because the community's correct usage anchors his thought. If incomplete grasp plus immersion in a community's usage suffices, a system saturated in that community's pooled usage has, on the face of it, exactly the kind of communal anchoring Burge makes constitutive — and more exposure to the usage than any single deferring layperson.
  • Toward "could not count": Burge's account requires the bearer to have attitudes in the first place — "Thoughts, beliefs, intentions" specified by content clauses judged true or false (p. 74) — and to stand in a relation of deference: the layman's word is anchored because he defers to "others who, being specialists, understand our terms better than we do" (p. 80), i.e. there is an expert behind him and a willingness to be corrected (the "swan" discussion turns on whether the subject "would fairly naturally concede his mistake," p. 84). It is an open question whether a text model's pooled output instantiates deference (a normative relation to a corrigible-by-experts community) or merely reproduces the community's strings. Burge's bearer is a subject with attitudes embedded in a corrigible social practice; whether that description fits a corpus-trained system is precisely the off-board classificatory question — not settled by Burge's text, which says nothing about corpus-trained membership.
  • essay/stereotype-without-the-expert (directly relevant; no link added here). That essay argues the model occupies neither role in Putnam's division of linguistic labor — neither the deferring layperson (no expert behind it) nor the anchoring expert (no recognitional method). Burge's account is deference-centric in exactly the way that essay's "neither role" point engages: Burge's layman bears content because he defers to a corrigible community of experts. The essay's open question — whether "holds the community's pooled output but occupies no role / defers to no one" suffices for content — is sharpened, not settled, by Burge: he makes deference constitutive, which is precisely the relation the essay says the model lacks an occupiable seat in. The orchestrator should weigh whether to add a typed relation from that essay; this source page only supplies the primary.

What it can ground

  • The verbatim primary for social externalism / anti-individualism about mental content: the three-step arthritis thought experiment (pp. 77-79), the conclusion that mental content "is attributable to differences in [the] social environment" (p. 79), the incomplete understanding premise (p. 79), deference to specialists who "understand our terms better than we do" (p. 80), and the claim that communal practice fixes content "even in cases where I fully understand the content" (p. 85) — for any page needing the founding social-externalist formulation rather than a paraphrase.
  • The conceptual point that the contents of propositional attitudes are fixed partly by the linguistic community, generalizing externalism beyond natural-kind reference (Putnam) to ordinary words and to attitude-content itself.
  • The deference / corrigibility structure (a subject thinking with a notion he incompletely grasps, anchored by experts he defers to) that the in-repo essays' community-membership antecedent and "neither role" arguments engage.

What it cannot ground

  • Any claim about LLMs, embeddings, or neural models: the essay predates all of it by decades; it grounds the theory a model's content-relation is judged against, never a result about a model. It is not a human anchor and licenses no human-comparison claim.
  • A verdict on whether a corpus-trained system counts as a deferring community member / content-bearer. Burge's text bears on the question (both ways, above) but does not decide it — it is silent on corpus-trained membership, and whether pooled output instantiates deference (vs. reproducing strings) and whether a model has attitudes are exactly the open classificatory questions. This page lays out the textual evidence; it ratifies no answer.
  • A settled verdict that externalism is correct. It is one strongly-argued position; the internalist pole (Chomsky's i-language, Fodor's narrow content; applied to LMs, source/baggio-murphy-2024-internalist-rejoinder) contests it. Cite it as the social-externalist primary, not as established fact.
  • The reference-anchor gap. As concept/referential-meaning stresses, there is no in-repo resource that anchors reference/content the way the AANN stimuli anchor form. Ingesting Burge closes another part of the "conceptual grounding is provisional pending ingestion" half of that gap (the social-externalist sub-tag), not the missing-resource half.
