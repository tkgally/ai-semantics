Campbell & Fiske 1959 — Convergent and Discriminant Validation by the Multitrait-Multimethod Matrix

What it is

Donald T. Campbell (Northwestern) and Donald W. Fiske (Chicago)'s 1959 Psychological Bulletin article (56(2): 81–105), "Convergent and Discriminant Validation by the Multitrait-Multimethod Matrix." It is the primary source for the multitrait–multimethod (MTMM) matrix and for the paired vocabulary of convergent and discriminant validation. Its core moves: a measure is not a pure read-out of a trait but a trait-method unit (trait content fused with method-specific features); to establish a trait measure one must show both that independent methods of measuring the same trait agree (convergent validation) and that the trait is distinguishable from other traits (discriminant validation); and the way to see both at once is to lay out, in a single matrix, all the intercorrelations produced when several traits are each measured by several methods. It is a measurement-methodology / philosophy-of-science source — not a meaning-theory source and not a human-labeled empirical resource.

It was fetched for one specific in-repo purpose: to discharge the Campbell & Fiske leg of revision trigger (e) of the essay essay/construct-validity-without-a-criterion. The MTMM vocabulary has, until now, entered the project only through secondary channels — Messick's citation of it and Borsboom's one-line critic's verdict (the line "a favorable matrix configuration is not constitutive of validity," recorded on source/borsboom-2004-concept-of-validity, p. 1068). This page ingests the convergent/discriminant + MTMM framework at primary strength, from Campbell & Fiske's own text, so the essay can cite the matrix logic on its own terms rather than through a critic or a citer. It is the companion primary to source/cronbach-meehl-1955-construct-validity: Campbell & Fiske explicitly build on Cronbach & Meehl's construct-validity framework and quote it (p. 83), and the two together are the canonical mid-century statement of construct validity.

This is a methodology anchor, NOT a human-comparison anchor. It licenses no claim about any LLM. It supplies the convergent/discriminant + MTMM vocabulary and the validity logic the project's probe-design discipline can be read as instantiating; it cannot ground any empirical finding about a model, and (being a 1959 measurement paper) it says nothing about language models, lexical gradience, or Construction Grammar.

Key passages (verbatim, with journal-page locators)

p. 81 — aspect 1: validation is typically convergent (the convergent-validation requirement):

"Validation is typically convergent, a confirmation by independent measurement procedures."

p. 81 — aspect 2: discriminant validation is also required (the discriminant-validation requirement):

"For the justification' of novel trait measures, for the validation of test interpretation, or for the establishment of construct validity, discriminant validation as well as convergent validation is required."

(The stray apostrophe after "justification" — "justification'" — is in the source text and is reproduced verbatim [sic].)

"Tests can be invalidated by too high correlations with other tests from which they were intended to differ."

p. 81 — aspect 3: the test as a trait-method unit (the famous line):

"Each test or task employed for measurement purposes is a trait-method unit, a union of a particular trait content with measurement procedures not specific to that content."

"The systematic variance among test scores can be due to responses to the measurement features as well as responses to the trait content."

(In the printed abstract column "trait-method" is split across a line as "trait-/method"; it is reproduced here as the hyphenated compound "trait-method unit," which is the term's intact form used elsewhere in the paper, e.g. the section heading "The test as a trait-method unit," p. 84. The two clauses of the first sentence were each verified verbatim across that line-break.)

p. 81 — aspect 4: why a matrix, and what the MTMM matrix is (the definition):

"Such a matrix presents all of the intercorrelations resulting when each of several traits is measured by each of several methods."

p. 82 — naming the regions of the matrix (validity diagonal / monotrait-heteromethod; the heterotrait triangles):

"A heteromethod block is made up of a validity diagonal (which could also be designated as monotrait-heteromethod values) and the two heterotrait-heteromethod triangles lying on each side of it."

(The companion definitions on the same page: the "reliability diagonals … could also be designated as the monotrait-monomethod values," and "Adjacent to each reliability diagonal is the heterotrait-monomethod triangle.")

p. 82 — the first of the four matrix criteria = convergent validity:

"the entries in the validity diagonal should be significantly different from zero and sufficiently large to encourage further examination of validity. This requirement is evidence of convergent validity."

p. 83 — the third criterion (a measure agrees more with the same trait by another method than with different traits by the same method) and the verdict that the last three criteria are discriminant:

"A third common-sense desideratum is that a variable correlate higher with an independent effort to measure the same trait than with measures designed to get at different traits which happen to employ the same method."

"The last three criteria provide evidence for discriminant validity."

p. 83 — reliability vs. validity placed on one continuum of method-similarity (the load-bearing definition):

"Reliability is the agreement between two efforts to measure the same trait through maximally similar methods. Validity is represented in the agreement between two attempts to measure the same trait through maximally different methods."

p. 84 — discriminant validation as the reason a construct must be distinguishable from its neighbours:

"One cannot define without implying distinctions, and the verification of these distinctions is an important part of the validational process."

p. 84 — method variance is itself a source of invalidity (the trait-method-unit consequence):

"To the extent that irrelevant method variance contributes to the scores obtained, these scores are invalid."

p. 85 — how the MTMM matrix exposes method variance (the method-variance / methods-factor statement):

"In the multitrait-multimethod matrix, the presence of method variance is indicated by the difference in level of correlation between the parallel values of the monomethod block and the heteromethod blocks, assuming comparable reliabilities among all tests."

p. 85 — trait vs. method is not absolute but relative to intent (a much-cited qualification):

"The distinction between trait and method is of course relative to the test constructor's intent."

p. 103 — convergence is a low bar, discrimination is hard (and can never be fully established):

"While agreement between several methods is desirable, convergence between two is a satisfactory minimal requirement. Discriminative validation is not so easily achieved."

"Just as it is impossible to prove the null hypothesis, or that some object does not exist, so one can never establish that a trait, as measured, is differentiated from all other traits."

p. 104 — SUMMARY: the matrix prescription, and the empirical punchline that method factors dominate:

"Measures of the same trait should correlate higher with each other than they do with measures of different traits involving separate methods."

"Method or apparatus factors make very large contributions to psychological measurements."

(Verification note. The PDF extraction wraps lines and hyphenates words across line ends — e.g. "valida-/tional," "pro-/cedures," "trait-/method." Every quote above was matched against the extracted text under whitespace normalization, and for spans crossing a line-break against both de-hyphenation choices — drop-the-hyphen for genuinely-split words like "procedures," keep-the-hyphen for true compounds like "trait-method." What it can ground

  • The convergent/discriminant + MTMM vocabulary at primary strength — discharging the Campbell & Fiske leg of essay/construct-validity-without-a-criterion revision trigger (e). The essay can now state, from Campbell & Fiske's own text, that convergent validation is "a confirmation by independent measurement procedures" (p. 81) and that discriminant validation — showing a trait is "differentiated from" the constructs it must be distinguished from (p. 103) — is "required" alongside it for construct validity (p. 81), rather than relying on Messick's citation or Borsboom's one-line verdict.
  • The trait-method-unit point as the structural reason a behavioral probe is not a pure read-out of the construct it targets. Campbell & Fiske's "Each test or task … is a trait-method unit" (p. 81) and "irrelevant method variance … these scores are invalid" (p. 84) are the canonical statement that how you measure contaminates what you measure. This is the methodology backing for the project's repeated worry that a probe's behavior reflects the probe format (prompt template, scoring rule, answer channel) as much as the targeted construct — the "design-out-not-model-out," confound-at-construction discipline. Campbell & Fiske's prescription (vary the method, read the construct off what survives the method change) is the MTMM form of that worry; the project's "method variance" is the prompt/format artifact it tries to design out.
  • A primary basis for the project's preference for cross-method convergence over single-probe results. Campbell & Fiske make convergence "a confirmation by independent measurement procedures" the common denominator of validity "in contradistinction to reliability" (p. 83), and warn that "convergence between two is a satisfactory minimal requirement" while "discriminative validation is not so easily achieved" (p. 103). This grounds, at primary strength, why the project treats agreement across different probe designs as more telling than a single design's result — and why it does not over-read a single probe's null.
  • The companion-primary to Cronbach & Meehl 1955. Campbell & Fiske explicitly build on and quote source/cronbach-meehl-1955-construct-validity (their p. 83 quotes Cronbach & Meehl 1955 p. 295 on phenotypically diverse criteria), and SUMMARIZE that their convergence/discrimination notions are "compared with previously published formulations, such as construct validity" (p. 104). Together the two pages are the canonical mid-century construct-validity pair the essay reasons through.

What it cannot ground

  • Any claim about any model. It predates language models by decades, reports no measurement of any LLM, and is not a human-labeled empirical asset (no treebank, sense inventory, acceptability norm, or annotation). It cannot be an anchors: resource. Its role is methodology framing only.
  • A human-comparison anchor. It supplies a method for validating a measure (and a vocabulary for method-contamination), not a human yardstick. Citing it discharges no empirical claim's human-anchor obligation; it discharges the primary-source gap the essay's trigger (e) flagged for the Campbell & Fiske / MTMM leg.
  • A licence to claim a probe "is valid" by exhibiting a favorable matrix. Borsboom's standing objection — recorded on source/borsboom-2004-concept-of-validity (p. 1068) — is precisely that "a favorable matrix configuration is not constitutive of validity." Campbell & Fiske themselves say discriminant validation "is not so easily achieved" and that one "can never establish that a trait, as measured, is differentiated from all other traits" (p. 103). The MTMM matrix is a tool for evidence, not a constitution of validity; do not cite this page as warrant for declaring any probe valid.
  • Anything about lexical gradience or Construction Grammar. The paper is general measurement methodology (its examples are personality and ability tests, rater halo effects, response sets, apparatus factors in animal-differences research); it says nothing about graded word sense, form–meaning pairing, or acceptability methodology.

Known limits

  • 1959 measurement methodology. Its statistical apparatus is the eyeballed correlation matrix; later work (confirmatory factor analysis, CFA-based MTMM models, the CT-C(M−1) family) formalized and partly superseded the visual-inspection criteria. The logic of convergent/discriminant validation and the trait-method-unit insight transfers; the analysis technique is dated. Cite it as the origin and canonical statement of the MTMM framework, not as the current state of MTMM analysis.
  • A contested place in validity theory. The MTMM matrix sits inside the construct-validity tradition that source/borsboom-2004-concept-of-validity attacks (validity as a property fixed by causation, not by a correlation configuration) and that source/messick-1995-validity-of-psychological-assessment folds into a unified construct-validity judgment. Cite Campbell & Fiske for the convergent/discriminant + method-variance vocabulary; the question of whether a matrix constitutes validity is contested and is recorded on those pages, not here.
