Goldberg 2006 — Constructions at Work: The Nature of Generalization in Language

What it is

The usage-based extension of Cognitive Construction Grammar: Adele E. Goldberg, Constructions at Work: The Nature of Generalization in Language, Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2006 (Oxford Linguistics; 280 pp.; Google-Books id LHrcqeZmUN4C). Where source/goldberg-1995-constructions established that argument-structure patterns are themselves meaningful form–meaning pairings, the 2006 book turns to the nature of generalization: how learners (children and adults) acquire constructional generalizations from finite input, how those generalizations are constrained so learners avoid overgeneralizing, and how a function-and-processing account explains cross-linguistic and language-internal regularities without language-specific stipulations. Its two signature mechanisms are statistical preemption (indirect negative evidence: repeatedly hearing a competing pattern where another might have been expected lets the learner infer the other is not conventional) and a pattern's degree of openness / type frequency (productivity). The book is explicitly usage-based: actual-use facts (frequencies, fully compositional individual patterns) are stored alongside broader generalizations — the bridge from the constructional wedge toward the project's distributional tag.

Quotes

All quotes below are verbatim from Goldberg's 2009 précis PDF (fetched, extracted), unless otherwise flagged. Locators are the précis's section/structure; the one book-page locator is Goldberg's own attribution to the 2006 book.

The constructionist core — knowledge of language as form–function pairings (précis, opening)

"These approaches emphasize that speakers' knowledge of language consists of systematic collections of form-function pairings that are learned on the basis of the language they hear around them. This simple idea is, to my mind, what sets constructionist approaches apart from traditional generative grammar."

(Verbatim from the 2009 précis, opening paragraph; this is Goldberg's own framing of the book's thesis.)

The definition of a construction — the 2006 reformulation (précis, quoting "Goldberg, 2006:5")

The 1995 "iff" definition is broadened in 2006 to admit fully predictable but sufficiently frequent patterns. Goldberg reproduces the book's definition in the précis with the book-page locator:

"Any linguistic pattern is recognized as a construction as long as some aspect of its form or function is not strictly predictable from its component parts or from other constructions recognized to exist. In addition, patterns are stored as constructions even if they are fully predictable as long as they occur with sufficient frequency (see Chapter 3 for discussion)."

(Quoted from the 2009 précis, which attributes it "(Goldberg, 2006:5)." The book page itself was not independently read; this is the définition as Goldberg restates it in her own précis, carrying her own book locator. Note the 2006 broadening over the 1995 definition on source/goldberg-1995-constructions: the "+ frequency" clause is the usage-based addition.)

Usage-based grammar (précis, §"Item specific knowledge and generalizations")

"constructionist approaches are generally USAGE-BASED: facts about the actual use of linguistic expressions such as frequencies and individual patterns that are fully compositional are recorded alongside more traditional linguistic generalizations"

and, on the rule/list trade-off:

"Available evidence suggests that both generalizations ('rules') and item-specific knowledge ('lists') are recorded"

(Both verbatim from the 2009 précis. "USAGE-BASED" small-caps is the précis's own emphasis; the second fragment cites "CW 2006, chapter 3.")

Statistical preemption (précis, §"How generalizations are constrained")

The book's signature constraint on overgeneralization:

"Two factors are argued to play a central role: statistical preemption or repeatedly witnessing a word in a competing pattern, and a pattern's degree of openness: the variability of the items that occur in a given pattern."

"more specific knowledge always preempts general knowledge in production, as long as either would satisfy the functional demands of the context equally well."

and that preemption (unlike raw token frequency) is semantically conditioned:

"Thus frequency plays a role in the process of statistical preemption exactly because the preemption is statistical. Upon repeated exposures to one construction in lieu of another construction with closely related meaning, the learner can infer that the second construction is not conventional."

(All verbatim from the 2009 précis. The précis credits the statistical-preemption idea to "Goldberg (1995)" and develops it; the worked contrast is She explained him the problem (??) preempted by She explained the problem to him.)

Surface generalizations and the conclusion (précis)

That generalizations are read off surface structure, not an underlying level:

"Generalizations are best described by analyzing surface structure instead of positing an underlying level of representation."

"generalizations are constrained by the indirect negative evidence children receive involving statistical preemption of non-occurring patterns, together with conservative induction based on the degree of openness."

(Both verbatim from the 2009 précis, §"Conclusion." The book argues, per the précis, that "many cross-linguistic generalizations can be accounted for by appealing to pragmatic, cognitive, and processing facts that are independently required, without stipulations that are specific to language.")

Bearing on this project

  • concept/constructional-meaning. That page names "Goldberg 1995, 2006 … not in-repo" as the source of the form–meaning-pairing thesis. The 1995 page upgraded the 1995 half; this page upgrades the 2006 half to a sourced (author-précis-attributed) provenance — including the broadened 2006 definition (the "+ sufficient frequency" usage-based clause), which the 1995 "iff" definition lacks. A page citing "Goldberg's 2006 definition" can now point here, as an attribution carried by Goldberg's own précis, never as a primary reading of the book.
  • The bridge to distributional. 2006 is the usage-based turn: frequencies and compositional individual patterns are stored, and statistical preemption is a frequency-sensitive, meaning-conditioned learning mechanism. This is the natural hinge between the project's constructional wedge and its distributional tag (see meaning-senses.md) — a construction's productivity and its blocking are partly distributional facts. It also bears on the project's overgeneralization/coercion probe lines: the sneeze-caused-motion datum reappears here (the précis uses it to argue preemption, not frequency, prevents overgeneralization).
  • constructional + inferential + distributional tags. The construction means an inference the parts do not license (1995); 2006 adds that which generalizations survive is fixed by usage statistics (preemption, type frequency). That triad is exactly the theory the project's constructional probes test a model against.
