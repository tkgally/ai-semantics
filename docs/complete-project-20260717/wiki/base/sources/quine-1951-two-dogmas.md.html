Quine 1951 — Two Dogmas of Empiricism

What it is

W. V. O. Quine's 1951 paper "Two Dogmas of Empiricism," originally published in The Philosophical Review 60(1) (1951): 20–43. It is the headwater text of confirmation holism (a.k.a. semantic holism in its Quinean form) and the locus classicus of the attack on the analytic/synthetic distinction. Quine names two dogmas of modern empiricism and argues both are ill-founded: (1) the belief in a fundamental cleavage between analytic and synthetic truths, and (2) reductionism — the belief that each meaningful statement is equivalent to some logical construct upon terms referring to immediate experience. The positive doctrine that replaces them (§VI) is that statements meet experience not individually but as a corporate body: "The unit of empirical significance is the whole of science," and "total science is like a field of force whose boundary conditions are experience," so that any statement can be held true come what may and none is immune to revision. It is a primary philosophy-of-meaning text — not a human-labeled empirical resource, and so not a human anchor: it grounds the theory the project's concept pages characterize, never a result about any model.

Before this page, concept/semantic-holism cited Quine 1951 (and Word and Object, 1960) only as "(not in-repo; characterization)" — calling Quine's confirmation holism "the headwater" and leaning on it for the claim that "the analytic/synthetic distinction … cannot be drawn." This page is the in-repo primary that concept page can now point at for the two-dogmas statement, the corporate-body/field-of-force holism, and the circle-of-interdefined-terms attack on analyticity.

Key passages (verbatim, with section locators)

1. The two dogmas stated, and reductionism defined (§I, opening)

The essay's first paragraph names both dogmas and gives reductionism its canonical formulation. (Reproduced verbatim including the transcription's stray space before the commas in "analytic ," and "synthetic ," — flagged.)

"Modern empiricism has been conditioned in large part by two dogmas. One is a belief in some fundamental cleavage between truths which are analytic , or grounded in meanings independently of matters of fact and truths which are synthetic , or grounded in fact. The other dogma is reductionism : the belief that each meaningful statement is equivalent to some logical construct upon terms which refer to immediate experience. Both dogmas, I shall argue, are ill founded." (§I, opening paragraph)

This is the load-bearing statement of dogma (1) — the analytic/synthetic cleavage — and dogma (2) — reductionism. Quine adds two paragraphs later that abandoning them blurs "the supposed boundary between speculative metaphysics and natural science" and brings "a shift toward pragmatism."

2. The analyticity / synonymy / definition circle (§I and §IV)

The attack on the intelligibility of analyticity is that it is explained only by other terms of the same family — meaning, synonymy, definition — none independently clear. At the close of §I, on the second class of analytic statements (those reducible to logical truth by substituting synonyms, like "No bachelor is married"):

"We still lack a proper characterization of this second class of analytic statements, and therewith of analyticity generally, inasmuch as we have had in the above description to lean on a notion of 'synonymy' which is no less in need of clarification than analyticity itself." (§I, closing)

§IV then makes the circularity explicit — the proposed definientia for analyticity loop back to analyticity:

"Analyticity at first seemed most naturally definable by appeal to a realm of meanings. On refinement, the appeal to meanings gave way to an appeal to synonymy or definition. But definition turned out to be a will-o'-the-wisp, and synonymy turned out to be best understood only by dint of a prior appeal to analyticity itself. So we are back at the problem of analyticity." (§IV)

This is the precise line concept/semantic-holism leans on for "the analytic/synthetic distinction … cannot be drawn": the distinction between what a word means and what we merely believe using it rests on a circle of interdefined terms, none of which is independently given.

3. Reductionism in its surviving, statement-by-statement form (§V)

§V tracks reductionism from its "radical" sense-datum form down to the residual dogma that each statement, taken alone, has its own range of confirming experience:

"The dogma of reductionism survives in the supposition that each statement, taken in isolation from its fellows, can admit of confirmation or infirmation at all." (§V)

This is the form of dogma (2) that §VI's holism directly denies.

4. Confirmation holism: the corporate body (§V–§VI)

The famous counter-thesis — statements meet experience only collectively:

"My countersuggestion, issuing essentially from Carnap's doctrine of the physical world in the Aufbau , is that our statements about the external world face the tribunal of sense experience not individually but only as a corporate body." (§V, closing into §VI)

And the unit thesis that opens §VI proper:

"But what I am now urging is that even in taking the statement as unit we have drawn our grid too finely. The unit of empirical significance is the whole of science." (§VI)

5. The web/field-of-force metaphor (§VI)

Total science as a field of force touching experience only at its edges:

"The totality of our so-called knowledge or beliefs, from the most casual matters of geography and history to the profoundest laws of atomic physics or even of pure mathematics and logic, is a man-made fabric which impinges on experience only along the edges. Or, to change the figure, total science is like a field of force whose boundary conditions are experience. A conflict with experience at the periphery occasions readjustments in the interior of the field." (§VI)

6. Any statement holdable, none immune to revision (§VI)

The two consequences that make the holism bite — revisability runs all the way through:

"Any statement can be held true come what may, if we make drastic enough adjustments elsewhere in the system." (§VI)

And conversely:

"Conversely, by the same token, no statement is immune to revision. Revision even of the logical law of the excluded middle has been proposed as a means of simplifying quantum mechanics …" (§VI)

7. The two dogmas are at root identical (§VI)

Quine ties dogma (1) and dogma (2) together — reductionism's "each statement has its own confirmation" and analyticity's "some statements are confirmed come what may" are two faces of one mistake:

"The two dogmas are, indeed, at root identical." (§VI)

Bearing on this project

  • concept/semantic-holism. That page's spectrum-of-positions section names "Quine's confirmation holism ('Two Dogmas of Empiricism', 1951; Word and Object, 1960; not in-repo; characterization)" as "the headwater: statements face the tribunal of experience not individually but only as a corporate body, so there is no fact of the matter about the meaning of one sentence in isolation, and the analytic/synthetic distinction … cannot be drawn." This page supplies the verbatim primary for exactly that — the corporate-body passage (§V–§VI), "the unit of empirical significance is the whole of science" (§VI), and the analyticity-circle (§§I, IV). The 1951 paper grounds the Quine half of that characterization; Word and Object (1960) remains not-in-repo.
  • concept/compositionality — via the holism→compositionality threat. The semantic-holism page records (head 4 of the holism objection) that "Holism is in tension with the compositional picture," handing the argument to the compositionality sibling; Fodor & Lepore's well-known charge is that holism threatens compositionality, and Quine is the headwater of the holism they target. This page is the in-repo primary for that headwater. Caveat: Quine 1951 does not itself argue against compositionality or even discuss it — the holism→compositionality threat is Fodor & Lepore's later transposition (not in-repo). Cite Quine here for the holism that the threat-argument takes as its premise, not for any claim about compositional structure, which the paper does not make.
  • The inferential and distributional meaning-senses. The holism thread bears on inferential (meaning as place in a web of inferential/confirmational relations rather than statement-by-statement) and distributional (a token's content as its position in the whole web — concept/semantic-holism notes distributional models are "holist by construction"). These are the two tags carried. (An earlier draft also tagged referential on the ground that the analyticity attack denies a clean meaning/fact separation; that was dropped on review — Quine's target is the analytic/synthetic distinction and confirmation holism, not the expression-to-world relation referential denotes in meaning-senses.md, so the tag over-reached.)

What it can ground

  • The verbatim two-dogmas statement and the definition of reductionism (§I); the analyticity/synonymy/definition circle (§§I, IV); the surviving statement-by-statement reductionism (§V); the corporate-body / whole-of-science holism, the field-of-force metaphor, "held true come what may," "no statement is immune to revision," and "the two dogmas are at root identical" (§§V–VI) — for any page needing Quine's own words rather than a paraphrase of confirmation holism or the analytic/synthetic critique.

What it cannot ground

  • Any claim about LLMs, embeddings, or neural models. The essay predates all of it; it grounds the theory a model may or may not instantiate, never a result about a model. It is not an anchors: resource — it is not a human-labeled empirical asset (no treebank, sense inventory, acceptability norm, or annotation). A source page supplies theory/provenance, not a human yardstick; citing it does not discharge any empirical claim's human-anchor obligation, and it grounds no claim about any model.
  • The holism→compositionality threat as Quine's own argument. That transposition is Fodor & Lepore's (not in-repo); Quine 1951 supplies only the holism premise, not the anti-compositionality conclusion.
  • The 1951 wording of §§1, 3, 4, 6 at fine grain, or the endnotes. The host read is a 1951/1961 composite whose §§1 and 6 were abridged and §§3–4 expanded in reprint; the load-bearing famous lines are stable across editions, but finer-grained quotation in those sections needs re-verification against the PR original (see Known limits).

Known limits

  • 1951/1961 composite text. The fetched ditext reproduction is explicitly a hybrid of the 1951 Philosophical Review original and the 1953/1961 From a Logical Point of View revision (its body prose is the reprinted text; endnotes are tagged "a" = 1951, "b" = 1961). §§1 and 6 were abridged and §§3–4 expanded between editions. The passages quoted above are the canonical, edition-stable lines, but the 1951 PR original is the citation target and was not directly read; any session quoting §§1/3/4/6 at finer grain, or the endnotes, must re-verify against a 1951 PR scan.
  • No PR pagination. This host preserves no PR page numbers (20–43); locators are section-level only. The single bare "17b" marker in the text is a 1961-version endnote anchor, not a page number.
  • Transcription artifacts. The mirror inserts stray spaces before some punctuation (reproduced and flagged in passage 1, "analytic ," / "synthetic ,"). A session quoting at length should re-verify against a cleaner scholarly edition.
  • Copyright. The work is likely still in copyright (© The Philosophical Review). This page quotes only short fair-use passages; do not reproduce the essay wholesale.
  • Scope. §§II (Definition), III (Interchangeability), and IV (Semantical Rules) — Quine's detailed dismantling of definition, interchangeability salva veritate, and Carnap's semantical rules — are summarized only via the §IV circularity verdict here. This page captures the two-dogmas statement, the analyticity-circle headline, and the §V–§VI holism core, not the full technical critique of synonymy.
