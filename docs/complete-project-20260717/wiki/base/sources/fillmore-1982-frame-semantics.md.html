Fillmore 1982 — Frame Semantics

What it is

The paper that names and programmatically states frame semantics: Charles J. Fillmore, "Frame Semantics," in Linguistics in the Morning Calm (Selected Papers from SICOL-1981), Linguistic Society of Korea (ed.), Seoul: Hanshin, 1982, pp. 111-137. Frame semantics holds that a word's meaning can only be understood against a structured background of knowledge — a frame, a system of related concepts such that to understand any one concept you must understand the whole structure it belongs to. A word "evokes" its frame; the frame is the interpretive scene the word is understood against. This is the theory that ties the lexical and grammatical levels together — words, idioms, and grammatical constructions are all form-and-frame pairings — and it is the direct ancestor of both Construction Grammar (Fillmore, Kay, Goldberg) and the FrameNet project. For this project it is a charter-core primary on the lexical↔grammatical link, and a natural companion to the distributional founding statements (source/harris-1954-distributional-structure, source/firth-1957-synopsis): where Harris and Firth locate word meaning in linguistic company, Fillmore locates it in an evoked frame of structured world-knowledge.

This is a partly-thin, secondary-and-preview-sourced page. No fully readable open-access full text of the 1982 primary was reached (routes tried below). The body of the paper and its original Morning Calm pagination (pp. 111-137) were not read here. Two opening sentences were read firsthand only via a registration-walled flipbook preview of the 2006 reprint (different pagination), and one further definitional sentence is carried via a named secondary. Every quote below is flagged with exactly how it was obtained, and no page number from the 1982 original is asserted as read.

Quotes

Opening of the paper (read firsthand via the 2006 reprint preview; original 1982 pagination NOT seen)

The paper's first sentence, read directly from the Yumpu reprint preview (reprint page labelled "1" / p. 373 of the Basic Readings reprint; the 1982 original page is not visible in the preview):

"With the term 'frame semantics' I have in mind a research program in empirical semantics and a descriptive framework for presenting the results of such research."

And, from the same preview's introduction:

"In the view I am presenting, words represent categorizations of experience…"

(Both read firsthand, but from the 2006 reprint preview, not the 1982 Hanshin original; the preview exposed only the introduction, so no original-pagination locator is claimed and the body of the paper was not read.)

The definition of "frame" (quoted via metaphorhacker.net 2014; primary NOT consulted, no locator)

The sentence most often quoted from the paper, as reproduced by the metaphorhacker.net secondary attributing it to Fillmore 1982:

"By the term 'frame' I have in mind any system of concepts related in such a way that to understand any one of them you have to understand the whole structure in which it fits…"

(Quoted via metaphorhacker.net 2014; the Fillmore primary was not consulted for this fragment, and the secondary gives no page number. The opening clause also appears in the Yumpu reprint preview, which is consistent with this wording, but a complete reading and an original-page locator are still lacking.)

Bearing on this project

  • concept/frame-and-prototype-semantics. That concept page is the in-repo home for Fillmore's frame notion; this source gives it a real, fetched provenance chain for the "frame" definition and the "research program in empirical semantics" framing — but a partly-secondary one, so the concept page should not present these as primary-read with a 1982 locator. The distinction matters for the no-fabrication discipline: the frame definition is reliably attributed and its opening clause preview-verified, not fully primary-read.
  • concept/constructional-meaning. Frame semantics is the theory under which lexical items, idioms, and grammatical constructions are alike treated as form-paired-with-evoked-frame; it is the direct upstream of the Construction Grammar that the constructional meaning-sense names. This page lets a constructional probe cite the frame-semantic root of "form–meaning pairing at any level of the grammar" rather than gesturing at it.
  • constructional and inferential tags. Frame semantics fits constructional (form paired with an evoked frame) and inferential (a frame is exactly a structure of inferential relations among concepts — to grasp one node is to grasp the inferences the whole structure licenses; see meaning-senses.md). It is a useful counterweight to the purely distributional founding sources: Fillmore's "company" is a structured knowledge frame, not a co-occurrence neighborhood.

What it can ground

  • The reliably-attributed wording of Fillmore's definition of a frame ("any system of concepts related in such a way that to understand any one of them you have to understand the whole structure in which it fits"), and the "frame semantics … research program in empirical semantics" self-description — as an attribution carried by a fetched secondary plus a fetched reprint preview, suitable for a concept/essay page that says "Fillmore's frame-semantic notion," never for a claim resting on a full primary reading.
  • The historical-conceptual point that frame semantics ties the lexical and grammatical levels through a single device (form paired with an evoked frame), placing it upstream of Construction Grammar and FrameNet.

What it cannot ground

  • Anything requiring the 1982 primary's full text, its argument structure beyond the opening, or its original Morning Calm pagination (pp. 111-137): the body was not read, and no original page number here is read-verified. A page needing an exact passage with a 1982 locator must reach the primary first.
  • Any LLM/empirical claim: like the distributional founding sources, this grounds a theory a model may instantiate, never a result about a model.
  • Any claim that frame semantics is reference-/truth-conditional in the Fregean sense: Fillmore's notion is knowledge-frame-based and inferential, and the relation to reference is not settled by what was read here.

Known limits

  • Secondary-and-preview, not primary-read. status: secondary-only records that no full OA primary was reached. The two opening sentences were read firsthand but from the 2006 Basic Readings reprint preview (registration-walled body, reprint pagination starting p. 373), and the frame definition is via metaphorhacker.net without a locator. A later session reaching a legitimate full text (controlled-lending scan, the Geeraerts reprint in full, or a library copy of the 1982 Hanshin volume) should re-read the body, attach original-pagination locators, and only then consider promoting the status.
