Pickering & Garrod 2004 — Toward a Mechanistic Psychology of Dialogue

What it is

The canonical statement of the interactive alignment framework: a mechanistic (processing-level) account of how two interlocutors come to understand each other in dialogue. The account claims that successful dialogue rests on interlocutors developing aligned representations at many linguistic levels (situation model, lexical, syntactic, phonological, …), and that this alignment is driven primarily by a primitive, automatic, resource-free priming mechanism rather than by explicit modeling of each other's mental states. This is the project's RATIFIED theoretical backdrop for the relational axis (decision relational-anchor-shortlist, Option A, 2026-05-29; queued at P2 in wanted.md).

This is a THEORY source, NOT a human anchor. It supplies the framework the project borrows to describe what alignment-across-levels would predict and — importantly — what such an account does and does not claim. It is a psycholinguistic theory of human dialogue processing; it contains illustrative human data (e.g. the Garrod & Anderson 1987 maze-game transcript reproduced in its Table 1) but is not itself a human-labeled empirical asset the project can ground an empirical LLM claim against. Any application of its constructs to LLMs is the project's interpretive use, not a Pickering & Garrod claim — the paper is about human interlocutors throughout and predates the relevant LLM literature.

Abstract (verbatim, p. 169)

"Traditional mechanistic accounts of language processing derive almost entirely from the study of monologue. Yet, the most natural and basic form of language use is dialogue. As a result, these accounts may only offer limited theories of the mechanisms that underlie language processing in general. We propose a mechanistic account of dialogue, the interactive alignment account, and use it to derive a number of predictions about basic language processes. The account assumes that, in dialogue, the linguistic representations employed by the interlocutors become aligned at many levels, as a result of a largely automatic process. This process greatly simplifies production and comprehension in dialogue."

Key passages (verbatim, with locators)

Alignment defined — and distinguished from coordination (§2, p. 171):

"Specifically, alignment occurs at a particular level when interlocutors have the same representation at that level. Dialogue is a coordinated behavior (just like ballroom dancing). However, the linguistic representations that underlie coordinated dialogue come to be aligned, as we claim below."

The six-point program — alignment of situation models via priming at multiple levels (§2, p. 171):

"We now argue six points: (1) Alignment of situation models (Zwaan & Radvansky 1998) forms the basis of successful dialogue; (2) the way that alignment of situation models is achieved is by a primitive and resource-free priming mechanism; (3) the same priming mechanism produces alignment at other levels of representation, such as the lexical and syntactic; (4) interconnections between the levels mean that alignment at one level leads to alignment at other levels …"

Aligned situation models as the basis of successful dialogue (§2.1, p. 172):

"More specifically, we assume that in successful dialogue, interlocutors develop aligned situation models."

Successful dialogue = aligned representations (§3, p. 174-175):

"The interactive alignment model assumes that successful dialogue involves the development of aligned representations by the interlocutors. This occurs by priming mechanisms at each level of linguistic representation, by percolation between the levels so that alignment at one level enhances alignment at other levels, and by repair mechanisms when alignment goes awry."

The mechanism is direct, automatic priming — no "decision box" (§3.2, p. 176):

"Critically, these channels are direct and automatic (as implied by the term \"priming\"). The activation of a representation in one interlocutor leads to the activation of the matching representation in the other interlocutor directly. There is no intervening \"decision box\" where the listener makes a decision about how to respond to the \"signal.\""

What it does NOT require — explicit modeling of the other's beliefs (§4.1, p. 178):

"Unlike common ground, implicit common ground does not derive from interlocutors explicitly modeling each other's beliefs. Implicit common ground is therefore built up automatically and is used in straightforward processes of repair."

Full common ground / mental-state modeling is the costly exception, not the basis (§4.2, p. 178):

"Establishment of full common ground is, we argue, a specialized and non-automatic process that is used primarily in times of difficulty (when radical misalignment becomes apparent)."

Meaning by consensus, not by dictionary meanings (§2, p. 171; the paper's gesture at constitution):

"Furthermore, the meaning of what is being communicated depends on the interlocutors' agreement or consensus rather than on dictionary meanings (Brennan & Clark 1996) and is subject to negotiation (Linnell 1998, p. 74)."

What the framework claims — and does NOT claim — about meaning

This distinction is the reason the page is load-bearing for the project, so it is stated carefully:

  • What it IS. A processing/alignment account. Its explananda are the mechanisms by which two language-processing systems come to share representations and thereby understand each other efficiently. Its central causal claim is mechanistic: priming (automatic, resource-free, bidirectional, parity-of-production-and-comprehension) drives alignment across levels, with interactive repair and full common ground as costlier backstops.
  • What it does NOT do. It does not offer a theory of meaning-constitution — i.e. it does not claim that alignment is what constitutes the content of an utterance, nor that shared representations are meaning. Alignment is presented as how interlocutors come to understand each other given representations they already compute; the situation model is "a multi-dimensional representation of the situation under discussion" (§2.1, p. 171), assumed, not derived from the alignment process. The one near-constitution remark — "meaning … depends on the interlocutors' agreement or consensus rather than on dictionary meanings" (§2, p. 171) — is attributed to Brennan & Clark 1996 and Linnell 1998 and is not developed into a constitution thesis; the paper's own contribution is the mechanism, not an account of what content is.
  • The negative is sharp on mental-state modeling. A frequent misreading is that alignment requires interlocutors to represent each other's minds. The paper explicitly denies this for the basic process: implicit common ground "does not derive from interlocutors explicitly modeling each other's beliefs" (§4.1, p. 178). Mental-state modeling (full common ground) is reserved for breakdown.

So: alignment ⇒ successful mutual understanding (the paper's claim); alignment ⇏ meaning is constituted by alignment (NOT the paper's claim). The project must not import the framework as a constitution theory.

What this bears on in-repo

  • Supplies the citable theoretical backdrop the relational axis was IOU'd. concept/relational-meaning and the relational tag in meaning-senses.md assert "the multi-agent literature is about coordination, not meaning-constitution." Pickering & Garrod is the human-dialogue counterpart of that distinction made precise: they explicitly separate coordination (joint activity) from alignment (shared representation) (§2, p. 171), and their alignment account is, on its own terms, a processing theory — it shows how rich coordination/alignment phenomena arise without a constitution claim. This strengthens, on the human-theory side, the project's foil reading of the LLM convention work in source/ashery-2025-llm-conventions: coordination/alignment, even pervasive, is the floor, not constitution.
  • Frames the live-vs-shuffled discriminator. The framework's core prediction is that alignment is driven by priming — an automatic, accumulation-style mechanism. Whether a given amount of shared interaction history acts commutatively (sensitive only to content) or non-commutatively (sensitive to order/trajectory) is exactly the wedge in conjecture/commutative-convention and essay/aggregation-not-constitution. Note honestly: a pure priming-by-activation reading would predict substantial order/recency sensitivity in humans, which sits in tension with the frequency-over-recency finding catalogued from source/brennan-clark-1996-conceptual-pacts; the two human-side sources do not obviously agree on how history enters, and the project should not assume they do.
  • The repair/implicit-common-ground machinery is a source of candidate observables. §4 (interactive repair using implicit vs. full common ground) describes concrete dialogue behaviors that a future model-side dyad probe could code for — parallel to the pact/hedging observables drawn from Brennan & Clark — when the relational axis next needs operationalizations (cf. open-question/relational-meaning-pilot).

What it can ground

  • The interactive-alignment framework itself: definition of alignment (vs. coordination), the priming-drives-alignment mechanism, percolation across levels, parity of production/comprehension, and the implicit-vs-full common-ground distinction — all with §/page locators above.
  • The theoretical claim that successful human dialogue can be explained by aligned representations built automatically, without routine mental-state modeling (§4.1, p. 178).
  • The framing that an alignment/processing account is not a meaning-constitution account — useful when the project argues that LLM-dyad coordination results do not by themselves bear on constitution.

What it cannot ground

  • Any empirical LLM claim. It is a theory of human dialogue processing; it is not a human-labeled resource and carries no LLM data. It cannot serve as the anchors: resource for an empirical claim about LLM meaning.
  • A meaning-constitution claim of any kind — the paper explicitly stays at the processing/alignment level; reading it as "alignment constitutes meaning" would misuse it (see the dedicated section above).
  • Order/trajectory (commutativity) results, human or model. The paper proposes priming as the mechanism but reports no manipulation that isolates order-of-history from amount-of-history; its illustrative data (maze game) are not designed for that contrast.
  • Quantitative human baselines for the project's reference-game measures — it reproduces others' transcripts illustratively, not as a controlled corpus (for that, see resource/hawkins-tangrams).

Known limits

  • Only the target article (pp. 169-190) was read and quoted; the open peer commentary and authors' response (pp. 190-225, present in the same PDF) were not mined and may qualify or contest points above.
  • Version of Record via the Edinburgh institutional repository; quotes verified against a PyMuPDF extraction of that PDF, with hyphenation-at-line-break artifacts rejoined as noted under Provenance. Page locators use the journal's printed pagination.
  • A 2004 psycholinguistic theory: its evidence base, terminology (e.g. "situation model," "lemma stratum"), and the priming literature it rests on predate two decades of subsequent work and the entire LLM literature; the project's relevance is by analogy, declared as interpretive use, not by the authors' intent.
