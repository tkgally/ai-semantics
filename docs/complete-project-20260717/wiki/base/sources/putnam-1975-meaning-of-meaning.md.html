Putnam 1975 — The Meaning of "Meaning"

What it is

The canonical statement of semantic externalism. Hilary Putnam, "The Meaning of 'Meaning'," in Keith Gunderson (ed.), Language, Mind, and Knowledge (Minnesota Studies in the Philosophy of Science, vol. 7, University of Minnesota Press, 1975), pp. 131-193. The essay attacks the "traditional" theory of meaning by showing that its two load-bearing assumptions cannot be jointly held, and replaces the picture of meaning-as-private-mental-state with a picture in which extension is fixed partly by the speaker's physical environment, partly by a social division of labor, and partly by an indexical/causal relation to locally-sampled stuff. It is the primary the project's concept/referential-meaning had been characterizing second-hand ("not in-repo; characterization") as the source of the referential.externalist sub-tag (Twin Earth, division of linguistic labor). This page supplies the verbatim primary for that sub-tag.

A source page is NOT a human anchor. This is a 1975 philosophy essay; it grounds the concept the externalist sub-tag names, not any empirical result about any model, and it licenses no human-comparison claim.

The target: the two "unchallenged assumptions" and the claim they cannot be jointly held

Putnam frames the whole essay as a wedge between two assumptions the traditional theory of meaning held together. He states them (printed pp. 135-136):

"So theory of meaning came to rest on two unchallenged assumptions: (I) That knowing the meaning of a term is just a matter of being in a certain psychological state (in the sense of 'psychological state' in which states of memory and psychological dispositions are 'psychological states' […]). (II) [OCR: 'TI1at'] That the meaning of a term (in the sense of 'intension') determines its extension (in the sense that sameness of intension entails sameness of extension)." (pp. 135-136)

and the thesis that drives the essay (printed p. 136):

"I shall argue that these two assumptions are not jointly satisfied by any notion, let alone any notion of meaning. The traditional concept of meaning is a concept which rests on a false theory." (p. 136)

This is the rejection of the traditional "intension determines extension" assumption the project's concept page needs: Putnam does not simply deny (II); he shows (I) and (II) cannot both hold, then keeps the externalist horn (extension is not fixed by the individual's psychological state) at the cost of (II) and of meanings-as-mental-entities.

Twin Earth — "meanings just ain't in the head"

The thought experiment (printed pp. 138-139): a planet "very much like Earth" where "the liquid called 'water' is not H 2 0 but a different liquid whose chemical formula" he abbreviates XYZ, "indistinguishable from water at normal temperatures and pressures." A speaker on Earth in 1750 and his molecule-for-molecule-identical Doppelgänger on Twin Earth are in the same psychological state, yet "water" has a different extension (H₂O vs. XYZ) in each mouth. Putnam runs the same move on "elm"/"beech" (switched on Twin Earth) to make the point with words whose stereotype the speaker cannot even tell apart (printed p. 144):

"It is absurd to think his psychological state is one bit different from mine: yet he 'means' beech [OCR: '}'hen'] when he says 'elm' and I 'mean' elm when I say elm. Cut the pie any way you like, 'meanings' just ain't in the head!" (p. 144)

The famous line is verified verbatim on printed page 144, in the elm/beech (not the water) discussion. Putnam's own gloss earlier in the essay: "Extension is not determined by psychological state" (p. 137-138 region; the section opens "Are meanings in the head? That psychological state does not determine extension will now be shown with the aid of a little science fiction." p. 138).

The division of linguistic labor (the sociolinguistic hypothesis)

Putnam introduces it as "a fact about language that seems, surprisingly, never to have been pointed out: that there is division of linguistic labor" (p. 144), illustrated with gold (and elsewhere aluminum, elm) — most speakers acquire the word without acquiring the method of telling whether something falls in its extension (printed p. 144):

"But they engender a division of linguistic labor: everyone to whom gold is important for any reason has to acquire the word 'gold'; but he does not have to acquire the method of recognizing if something is or is not gold. He can rely on a special subclass of speakers." (p. 144)

He raises it to a cross-linguistic conjecture, the HYPOTHESIS OF THE UNIVERSALITY OF THE DIVISION OF LINGUISTIC LABOR (stated as a set-off block, printed pp. 145-146): every linguistic community has "some terms whose associated 'criteria' are known only to a subset of the speakers who acquire the terms, and whose use by the other speakers depends upon a structured cooperation between them and the speakers in the relevant subsets" (pp. 145-146). The payoff for reference-fixing (printed p. 146):

"Whenever a term is subject to the division of linguistic labor, the 'average' speaker who acquires it does not acquire anything that fixes its extension. In particular, his individual psychological state certainly does not fix its extension; it is only the sociolinguistic state of the collective linguistic body to which the speaker belongs that fixes the extension." (p. 146)

His image: words have been thought of "too much on the model" of a hammer (a one-person tool) rather than a steamship (a cooperative tool) (p. 146).

The stereotype, and the rejection of "intension determines extension"

Against the traditional idea that grasping a term is grasping a description that determines its extension, Putnam puts the stereotype — a "standardized description of features of the kind that are typical, or 'normal,' or at any rate stereotypical" (printed p. 147):

"Or one can give him a description. In the latter case the description one gives typically consists of one or more markers together with a stereotype [footnote 7] — a standardized description of features of the kind that are typical, or 'normal,' or at any rate stereotypical." (p. 147)

The stereotype explicitly does not determine extension: "Not all criteria used by the linguistic community as a collective body are included in the stereotype, and in some cases the stereotype may be quite weak" — the speaker's stereotype of an elm "is just that of a common deciduous tree" and falls "far short of constituting a way of recognizing elms" (p. 147). So a competent speaker can possess the full stereotype and still not fix the extension; extension is fixed by the world plus the expert sub-community, not by the description the speaker carries. This is the constructive replacement for assumption (II).

The indexical / causal component (the sameL relation)

The mechanism by which a natural-kind term reaches its extension: an unnoticed indexical component, tying the word to the stuff locally sampled via a same-kind relation (printed p. 152):

"Our theory can be summarized as saying that words like 'water' have an unnoticed indexical component: 'water' is stuff that bears [OCR: 'bears.'] a certain similarity relation to the water around here. Water at another time or in another place or even in another possible world has to bear the relation sameL to our 'water' in order to be water." (p. 152)

This is taught ostensively — "One can give him a so-called ostensive definition — 'this (liquid) is water'" (p. 147) — and Putnam aligns it with Kripke's rigid designation: the two doctrines are "but two ways of making the same point" (p. 152). Because the indexical/causal relation runs from the word to the actual local substance (H₂O here, XYZ on Twin Earth), two internally-identical speakers in different environments refer differently — which is exactly why "meaning (intension) determines extension" fails and why extension is fixed partly outside the head.

The concept page argues an LLM "has only narrow access by construction" — "no causal-historical chain … no membership in a community of experts, no environment it is embedded in independently of the corpus." Putnam's three externalist reference-fixers map directly onto what the LLM lacks: (i) the environment that distinguishes the Twin-Earth pair (the model has no Earth/Twin-Earth to be embedded in), (ii) the expert sub-community that fixes "gold"/"elm" for the layperson (the model is the average speaker with no expert to defer to), and (iii) the indexical/causal sameL relation to locally-sampled stuff (the corpus contains no sampled stuff). This is the precise content behind the concept page's claim that the externalist "no" is "the strongest in-principle case that an LLM does not refer," and behind its observation that a Twin-Earth diagnostic "has no purchase" on a model with no environment to vary. No typed link added here (the interpretation lives on the concept page); this source page only supplies the primary the interpretation quotes.
  • source/baggio-murphy-2024-internalist-rejoinder and the M&L exchange (context, no link). That rejoinder accepts the externalist analysis "only for 'a narrow class of natural language expressions' (proper names, natural-kind terms)" — i.e. precisely Putnam's natural-kind cases. Putnam's own text bounds the phenomenon similarly ("some words do not exhibit any division of linguistic labor: 'chair,' for example," p. 144), which is the textual basis for the "narrow class" framing those later debates inherit.

What it can ground

  • The verbatim primary for semantic externalism: the two-assumptions wedge (pp. 135-136), Twin Earth / "meanings just ain't in the head" (pp. 138-139, 144), the division of linguistic labor and its universality hypothesis (pp. 144-146), the stereotype as a non-extension-determining description (p. 147), and the indexical/sameL causal component of natural-kind reference (p. 152) — for any page that needs the founding externalist formulation rather than a paraphrase.
  • The conceptual point that extension is fixed partly outside the individual — by environment, by an expert sub-community, and by an indexical relation to local samples — which is the externalist "no" the LLM-reference debate invokes.
  • The textual basis for the "narrow class" scope later interlocutors attribute to externalism (natural-kind terms and names, not "chair").

What it cannot ground

  • Any claim about LLMs, embeddings, or neural models: the essay predates all of it by decades. It grounds the theory a model's reference-relation is judged against, never a result about a model. It is not a human anchor and licenses no human-comparison claim.
  • A settled verdict that externalism is correct: it is one strongly-argued position; the internalist pole (Chomsky, Fodor's narrow content; and, applied to LMs, source/baggio-murphy-2024-internalist-rejoinder) contests it. Cite it as the externalist primary, not as established fact.
  • The reference-anchor gap. As the concept page stresses, there is no in-repo resource that anchors reference the way the AANN stimuli anchor form; this source supplies the philosophical primary for the externalist sub-tag, which is a different thing from a reference-bearing empirical anchor. Ingesting Putnam closes the "conceptual grounding is provisional pending ingestion" half of that gap, not the missing-resource half.
