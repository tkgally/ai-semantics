Essay: kind, not level

REVISION (2026-06-23, session 87) — the central reading DISSOLVED at R2 for the lexical case; revision trigger (b) FIRED. The matched-ambiguity-kind probe this essay motivated has now run: result/within-lexical-scalar-vs-disjunctive-v1. Its verdict is SURVIVAL, 3/3 models — on a genuinely-disjunctive balanced homonym (no tie-break) the models commit (near-zero UNCLEAR) exactly as they do on a scalar bridging midpoint, level held fixed. So the essay's central bet — that a disjunctive lexical item would draw decline, showing the lexical specialness to be the softness of the scalar stimulus — is wrong for the lexical case. Per the convergence ladder's symmetric standard this is a first-class result, not a failure: it is the more interesting positive the essay itself named under trigger (b) — the lexical commit-without-hedging is about the word-sense layer, not the scalar-vs-disjunctive kind axis. The kind-reading is retracted for the lexical case (kept visible below, per the project's retraction discipline). One honest residual the result discloses bounds how strong "layer" can be: a balanced homonym is still resolvable-by-committing-to-a-reading, where a relational same-round dual-binding is literally unresolvable — so "word-sense is special" vs "a balanced homonym still affords a defensible resolution" is not fully separated. The essay's conceptual contribution (the scalar/disjunctive contrast as the better-shaped question) survives; only its empirical resolution flipped — and it flipped against the essay. See the revised trigger (b) below. The original draft is preserved unchanged beneath this banner.

Status: draft → revised (originally 2026-06-22). A philosophical-track essay arguing in the project's own voice, written the session after the cross-level dissolution it re-reads. Its original contribution is a re-reading, not a new measurement: it argues that the dissolution in result/cross-level-shared-instrument-v1 is most naturally read not as "three rhyming facts indexed by level" but as a single candidate regularity indexed by kind of indeterminacy — the same models commit on a scalar ambiguity (a graded similarity with a nearer pole to lean to) and decline on a disjunctive one (two complete readings or bindings, no nearer pole). On this reading the lexical leg is not a special layer; it is the leg that happened to be handed the softer kind of ambiguity. The essay is careful that this kind-reading is itself underdetermined by the dissolution's design (kind is confounded with level there), so it is filed as a conjecture-shaped reading at the bottom of the convergence ladder, not asserted as a finding — and it routes to the matched-ambiguity-kind decision opened this session. No new empirical claim; no human comparison. Read the revision triggers before citing.

The occasion

The previous session ran the shared-instrument cross-level probe and got a clean negative. result/cross-level-shared-instrument-v1 held one frozen commitment instrument fixed and applied it identically at three semantic levels — lexical sense, constructional reading, relational referent — and found that the discrete-on-the-moment posture (commit on the genuinely-ambiguous item, almost never take the explicit "UNCLEAR" option) holds only at the lexical level; at the constructional and relational levels the same models take "UNCLEAR" elevated on the ambiguous item. The bet that the "graded-aggregate / discrete-moment" shape was one cross-level property (conjecture/cross-level-gradience-aggregate-not-moment) lost; the deflationary default — three rhyming, instrument-specific facts, not one property — stood. On the convergence ladder the shape was unified at R1 (agreement under each leg's native instrument) and dissolved at R2 (the equalized instrument), the worked example that essay's trigger (b) anticipated.

That is the right verdict on the question that was asked, and this essay does not reopen it. The level-indexed unification is dead. But the dissolution result did not leave a clean three-way coincidence; it left a shape in the residue, and the result page itself names the shape as its load-bearing caveat. This essay is about that residue: what the dissolution surfaced even as it killed the thing it was testing.

The residue the dissolution left

The result's most honest paragraph is not the headline; it is the caveat. The three levels' "ambiguous" classes, it discloses, are not the same kind of indeterminacy:

"a lexical bridging pair is a human-rated usage-similarity midpoint (DURel ≈ 2–3): a graded similarity the model can still lean on and resolve to same/different; a constructional ambiguous item is a genuine structural two-reading (both readings fully available); a relational ambiguous-midrecord item is a genuine underdetermination (a same-round dual binding with no tie-break — both/neither)." (result/cross-level-shared-instrument-v1)

And it draws the consequence in the same place, in a sentence the essay takes as its pivot:

"the discrete-vs-graded split tracks, at least in part, what kind of indeterminacy each level presents: the models commit through a graded similarity but decline a genuine both/neither."

The page flags this as a confound — "Whether the lexical discreteness reflects a deep model fact or merely that a usage-similarity midpoint is a softer kind of ambiguity than a structural one is not separable in this design" — and concludes, correctly for its scope, that "Either way the unified 'one cross-level property' conjecture loses". The result was right to lead with the deflationary reading: for the question it asked (is the shape one property across levels?), the confound does not rescue the bet, and the most natural explanation of the split — different kinds of indeterminacy at different levels — is itself the "three instrument-specific facts" reading.

The move this essay makes is to take that same confound and turn it the other way up. A confound is two variables that move together; the result varied level and kind together and could not separate them. The result page resolves the tie toward the null because its job was the level-hypothesis and the null is the conservative call. But the other leg of the confound is not nothing. If the split tracks kind rather than level, then the dissolution has not shown three rhyming coincidences; it has shown a single, sharper regularity wearing the level hypothesis's clothes — and the lexical leg's "specialness" is an artifact of which kind of ambiguity that leg's stimulus happened to instantiate.

The distinction: scalar vs. disjunctive indeterminacy

The two kinds of ambiguity the result's caveat separates are worth stating as a clean conceptual contrast, because the contrast is the essay's load-bearing object and it is older and more general than this one probe.

  • A scalar ambiguity is a point on a similarity continuum. The lexical bridging item is one by construction: a DWUG mid-scale pair is a human-rated usage-similarity midpoint, two uses of a word whose senses are related — "scalar, not binary," in the lexicographer's picture (concept/polysemy). Crucially, a scalar ambiguity has a nearer pole: the two readings are not symmetric and complete, they are ends of a graded axis, and an item near the middle can still be resolved by leaning to the nearer end. There is always a "more same than different" or "more different than same" answer available, even at the midpoint, because the midpoint is a degree, not a tie.

  • A disjunctive ambiguity is a forced choice between two complete, symmetric readings with no nearer pole. The constructional ambiguous item is one — "both readings fully available" (telescope-attachment, quantifier scope): two grammatical parses, each entire, neither a degree of the other. The relational ambiguous-midrecord item is the limit case — a coined term bound to two figures in the same round with no recency or frequency tie-break, which the result glosses as "both/neither." A disjunctive ambiguity offers no leaning: there is no fact of the matter that makes one reading nearer, so a forced single pick is not resolving the item, it is guessing it.

The behavioral prediction this distinction makes is exactly the dissolution's data, read across the kind axis rather than the level axis. A model commits on a scalar ambiguity because the scalar item has a resolvable nearer pole — committing is a defensible response, the same defensible commitment the lexical bridging probe documented as "a graded SCALE with ungraded COMMITMENT" (essay/graded-scale-ungraded-commitment). A model declines on a disjunctive ambiguity because the disjunctive item has no nearer pole — declining ("UNCLEAR") is the correct response to a genuine both/neither, and the result shows the models take it, at ceiling, on the relational dual-binding (100% UNCLEAR, 3/3 models) and elevated on the constructional structural ambiguities. On this reading the models are not inconsistent across levels; they are consistent across kinds — committing where a nearer pole exists, declining where none does — and the level-indexed picture only looked like three facts because each level's stimulus happened to sit at a different place on the kind axis.

Why this is a re-reading, not a rescue of the dead conjecture

It would be a serious error — the operationalization-tuning failure (PROTOCOL.md §B) in essay clothing — to present the kind-reading as a way to un-dissolve the cross-level conjecture. It is not. The conjecture bet on a level property and that bet is lost, full stop; the kind-reading does not revive it. Three guards keep this essay honest about that:

  1. Kind is confounded with level in the very design that surfaced it. The dissolution varied the two together; it cannot tell a kind-regularity from a level-coincidence any better than it could tell a deep lexical fact from a soft stimulus. The result page says exactly this, and the essay does not pretend otherwise. The kind-reading is therefore not licensed by the dissolution — it is a rival reading of the same underdetermined data, with precisely the evidential standing the dissolution gave the level-reading: a direction-of-shape suggestion, not a demonstrated mechanism.

  2. On the project's own evidence standard, the kind-reading sits at R0/R1, not at a finding. The convergence ladder is explicit that "a cross-level unification may be asserted only at the rung the evidence has reached," and that below R2 "it is not a claim; it stays a rhyme." The kind-reading is, today, a shared phrasing ("commit on scalar, decline on disjunctive") laid over the same three legs — R0 — promoted to R1 only by the observation that the legs do point the same way once re-indexed by kind. It has not survived an instrument-that-equalizes-kind, because no such probe has run. By the ladder's own rule, asserting "kind, not level" as a mechanism now would repeat the exact overclaim the ladder forbids. So this essay asserts the kind-reading at R1 and no higher: it is the more parsimonious description of the residue, and a conjecture-shaped question, not a finding.

  3. The deflationary default still holds the burden. The OQ's standing warning binds here too: a pattern that re-spans the levels "is also the pattern most likely to be an artifact of how the project phrases its findings rather than of the models" (open-question/gradience-population-not-moment). "Kind, not level" is a new phrase, and a new phrase is exactly what the deflationary default distrusts. The essay's claim is only that, between two phrasings of the same underdetermined data, the kind-phrasing is the one that makes a sharper, testable prediction — and so is the one worth promoting to a test, not the one worth promoting to a belief.

So the kind-reading does nothing to the dissolution's verdict. What it does is convert the dissolution's caveat from a dead end ("the mechanism is not isolated, oh well") into a live hypothesis with a clean discriminating experiment — which is the difference between a null that closes a line and a null that opens one.

The discriminating test, and where it already half-exists

The kind-reading makes a prediction the level-reading does not, and the prediction is what lifts it off R1. Equalize the kind of ambiguity across levels — in particular, hand the lexical leg a genuinely disjunctive ambiguity instead of a scalar one — and watch the lexical leg.

  • If the lexical leg's commit-without-hedging is a fact about the layer (the level-residue reading, or a "deep model fact" about word-sense), it should persist even when the lexical item is disjunctively ambiguous: the model commits on word senses whatever their kind.
  • If it is a fact about the kind (this essay's reading), it should flip: a balanced homonym in a context with no tie-break — bank between "river" and "finance" with nothing to lean to — is a lexical disjunction, and the model should decline it the way it declines the constructional and relational disjunctions. The lexical specialness would dissolve into the kind axis.

The cleanest form of this test does not even need to go cross-level, and that is itself significant: the scalar-vs-disjunctive axis lives inside the lexical level. A polysemy bridging midpoint (related senses, scalar) and a homonym (unrelated senses, disjunctive) are both "lexical," so a within-lexical contrast — DWUG bridging items vs. balanced-homonym items, the same frozen instrument — tests "kind, not level" while holding the level fixed, sidestepping the cross-level comparability problem that dogged the original probe. This is the matched-ambiguity-kind follow-up, surfaced as an operationalization decision opened the session this essay was written (session 85) and ratified session 86 (2026-06-22, ADOPT DEFAULT) — see decisions/resolved/matched-ambiguity-kind-cross-level: the ratified yardstick is Option B (keep the DWUG scalar-bridging lexical leg; add a balanced-homonym disjunctive lexical class as a within-lexical contrast, level held fixed), internal-contrast-only for the disjunctive arm, plus a binding nuisance-matching freeze (register/length/frequency/syntactic-frame between the two lexical classes) — so the within-lexical probe below is now buildable (the run still gated by a fresh pre-run critic + budget). This ratification fixes the yardstick; the kind-reading itself stays at R1 until such a probe runs. The essay does not decide that operationalization; it states why the test is worth running: it is the R2 rung for the kind-reading, the one move that would tell a kind-regularity from a soft-stimulus artifact, exactly as the original shared-instrument probe was the R2 rung for the level-reading. And — by the ladder's symmetric standard — its null is first-class: if the lexical leg commits even on a balanced homonym, the kind-reading dissolves at R2 and the lexical specialness is shown to be about the layer after all, which would be a more interesting positive about word-sense than this essay bets on.

What the re-reading buys, and what it costs

What it buys is a sharper map of the project's own residue. The three level-home essays each stand untouched — graded-scale-ungraded-commitment on the lexical fork, preference-without-commitment on the constructional preference/commitment split, aggregation-not-constitution on the relational set-not-trajectory finding — and the dissolution touched none of them either; it failed only their join. The kind-reading offers a candidate new join, one the dissolution's own caveat points at: not "these three levels share a property" but "these models treat scalar indeterminacy and disjunctive indeterminacy differently, and that difference is what each level's result was partly measuring." If that join survives its R2 test it would be a genuinely more parsimonious account than three coincidences; if it dissolves, the project learns that the lexical commitment is about word-sense specifically, which is the more surprising answer. Either way the question is now shaped — a fork with a clean experiment — where the dissolution left only a caveat.

What it costs is vigilance against its own seductiveness. "Kind, not level" is a tidier, more quotable phrase than "three rhyming facts," and tidiness is precisely the property the deflationary default warns is most likely to be the project's prose rather than the models'. The essay's discipline is to hold the kind-reading at R1 — a description and a test-motivator — and to let the matched-ambiguity probe, run under the same freeze-before-results discipline as the original, be the only thing that can move it up. The kind axis is a better hypothesis than the level axis; it is not yet a better finding.

Why it matters to the meanings of "meaning"

The scalar-vs-disjunctive distinction is not a probe artifact; it is a real seam in how distributional competence could carry indeterminacy, and the seam bears on a standing project question. concept/distributional-meaning holds that distributional structure is "by itself … silent on reference and on truth," and the kind axis sharpens what kind of silence. A model trained on next-token statistics has rich access to graded similarity — the scalar pole is exactly what co-occurrence structure encodes well, so commitment on a scalar ambiguity (lean to the nearer distributional neighbor) is what distributional competence should do effortlessly. A genuine disjunction with no tie-break is the harder case: there is no distributional nearer-neighbor to break the tie, so the model's options are to guess or to decline, and the result shows it declines. On this reading the models commit and decline as if reading off where distributional structure supplies a gradient and where it does not — which is a calibrated statement about the shape of distributional competence, not about meaning proper, and not a human comparison. It says: the competence is gradient-shaped, so it resolves gradients and abstains on genuine disjunctions — exactly the profile concept/distributional-meaning predicts for a similarity engine, now visible as a behavioral signature rather than a theoretical expectation. The under-claim is deliberate: the essay asserts the scalar/disjunctive contrast is real and worth testing, not that the models' behavior is caused by distributional structure — that causal claim is exactly the R-ceiling the ladder names as out of reach for a behavioral project.

Flagged, not asserted; the present scope is the three ambiguity legs.
  • (e) A representational method becomes available. The kind-reading is behavioral: it claims the models behave as if tracking a nearer pole on scalar items and abstaining on disjunctive ones. If a representation-level probe ever runs, "scalar vs. disjunctive" would need restating at the representational grain, where a model's behavior can be discrete while its representation is graded, or vice versa. Nothing in-repo licenses that today; the panel is behavioral, n=3 commercial.

What this essay is not

  • Not a rescue of the dissolved conjecture. The level-indexed cross-level property is dead (conjecture/cross-level-gradience-aggregate-not-moment lost its bet); this essay does not revive it. It proposes a different axis (kind) as a rival reading of the same residue, at R1, with its own test.
  • Not a new empirical claim. Its original contribution is the re-reading — that the dissolution's commit/decline split tracks scalar-vs-disjunctive kind and that the lexical specialness is plausibly the stimulus, not the layer — together with the observation that this is testable and the routing to that test. Every empirical sentence is cited at the result page's stated strength; the result's own caveat carries the kind-confound verbatim.
  • Not a human comparison. The motivating result is internal-contrast-only (constructional + relational legs) with the lexical leg capped to usage-similarity, never certified sense-co-presence; this essay imports no human result and asserts none. The referential tag is carried because the scalar/disjunctive contrast is partly a contrast between related senses (polysemy, scalar) and unrelated senses (homonymy, disjunctive), not because any human reference comparison is made.
  • Not a claim that distributional structure causes the behavior. The "shape of distributional competence" reading is a calibrated description of where the behavior supplies a gradient and where it abstains; the causal claim (that next-token training produces the split) is the R-ceiling the ladder names out of reach for a behavioral project.
  • Not a restatement of the three level-home essays. Each stands at its own level and its own strength; this essay borrows their legs as instances of a kind contrast and points at a candidate new join, without re-arguing any of them.
