Essay: frame semantics in the third company

Status: draft (2026-06-25). A philosophical-track essay arguing in the project's own voice, and the explicit third panel of essay/two-distributional-hypotheses. It introduces no new empirical claim: every empirical assertion inside it cites the in-repo page that already carries it, and the original part is the argument — a three-way sorting of what a word's "company" can be, adding Fillmore's evoked frame as a third reading alongside Harris's co-occurrence neighbourhood and Firth's situation. The Fillmore premise is secondary-and-preview-sourced, never primary-read: the frame definition is carried by source/fillmore-1982-frame-semantics via the metaphorhacker.net secondary with no page locator, and the paper's two opening sentences only via a registration-walled 2006-reprint preview (pagination from p. 373, not the 1982 Morning Calm original). No 1982-original page number is asserted as read here. Revision trigger: if a later session reaches the Fillmore 1982 primary (or the full Geeraerts 2006 reprint) and finds the frame definition, or the "structured knowledge frame ≠ co-occurrence neighbourhood" reading, misstated by the secondary, revise or retract this essay's premise and re-state the sorting accordingly. The Firth half of the inherited premise is likewise secondary-sourced (via Brunila & LaViolette 2022, carried by source/firth-1957-synopsis); the Harris half is primary-read.

The position

The companion essay essay/two-distributional-hypotheses sorted "the distributional hypothesis" into two ideas — Harris's form-internal co-occurrence and Firth's situated "context of situation" — and argued that the next-token objective instantiates the Harris reading and not the Firth one. This essay adds a third reading of "knowing a word by its company." On Fillmore's frame semantics, a word's "company" is neither a co-occurrence neighbourhood (Harris) nor a situation of utterance (Firth) but a structured background frame of world-knowledge the word evokes. The bounded thesis: naming this third "company" is a sorting move, not an empirical one — it locates exactly where the frame reading bears on the grounding question (it raises a question about structured inferential background that is orthogonal to the co-occurrence the objective demonstrably instantiates) and it leaves that question open, declining the dispute the companion essay also declined. The essay re-labels; it does not resolve.

Three "companies," kept apart

The slogan "you shall know a word by the company it keeps" hides at least three different things a word's "company" could be. The in-repo sources let us separate them sharply.

Harris: the neighbouring linguistic elements. Harris's "company" is form-internal by construction — the other linguistic elements, the "environments," in whose distribution a word occurs. The primary-read central line is:

"if we consider words or morphemes A and B to be more different in meaning than A and C, then we will often find that the distributions of A and B are more different than the distributions of A and C. In other words, difference of meaning correlates with difference of distribution." (source/harris-1954-distributional-structure, p. 156)

The Harris source page draws the consequence: this correlation is "between meaning difference and distribution difference, a within-language contrast, not a theory of how expressions hook onto the world" (source/harris-1954-distributional-structure, §"Bearing on this project"). Harris's company is co-occurrence statistics over form, nothing more.

Firth: the situational surround. On the secondary reading the companion essay uses, Firth's "company" is wider than neighbouring tokens: his "collocation" was only "one mode of meaning among several" and was "always situated in a broader 'context of situation'" (source/firth-1957-synopsis, §"Bearing on this project"; via Brunila & LaViolette 2022, primary not consulted). Firth's company reaches out to the situational and cultural setting in which the word is used — a setting the token stream is a projection of, not a part of.

Fillmore: the structured conceptual frame. Fillmore's "company" is a third thing again — a structured chunk of relational world-knowledge the word evokes. The most-quoted statement of the frame notion, as the source page carries it, is:

"By the term 'frame' I have in mind any system of concepts related in such a way that to understand any one of them you have to understand the whole structure in which it fits…" (quoted via metaphorhacker.net 2014, attributing it to Fillmore 1982; the Fillmore primary was not consulted for this fragment and the secondary gives no page number; source/fillmore-1982-frame-semantics, §"The definition of 'frame'")

The source page itself draws the contrast this essay turns on: "Fillmore's 'company' is a structured knowledge frame, not a co-occurrence neighborhood" (source/fillmore-1982-frame-semantics, §"Bearing on this project"). A frame is not the neighbouring elements (Harris) and not the situation of utterance (Firth); it is the relational background of concepts against which the word is understood — the commercial-transaction scene that buy and sell both profile, the role-structure that risk evokes (concept/frame-and-prototype-semantics, §"Frame semantics: meaning against a structured background"). To have the frame is to grasp the whole structure of relations a word's concept sits in, not to track which tokens it co-occurs with.

So three companies, genuinely apart: neighbouring elements (Harris, form-internal), situational surround (Firth, situated), structured conceptual frame (Fillmore, relational world-knowledge). They are not three labels for one thing; they pick out three different objects, and only the first is co-occurrence statistics over form.

Which "company" a form-only objective can have

The companion essay settled which of the first two companies the objective instantiates: the next-token objective is trained on form alone and instantiates the Harris reading "almost line for line," not the Firth one (essay/two-distributional-hypotheses, §"Which one the objective instantiates"; the concept page states the instantiation directly, concept/distributional-meaning). This essay does not re-derive that; it cites it and asks the sharper question the third company raises.

The question is not the same as the question of co-occurrence. A frame, in Fillmore's sense, is a structure of inferential relations among concepts — the source page tags Fillmore inferential for exactly this reason: "a frame is exactly a structure of inferential relations among concepts — to grasp one node is to grasp the inferences the whole structure licenses" (source/fillmore-1982-frame-semantics, §"Bearing on this project"). So asking whether a model "has the frame" is not asking whether it tracks the right co-occurrence neighbourhood. It is asking whether form-distribution alone can encode the structured inferential background that the frame is.

That is a real and separable question, and it is precisely the one the companion essay located but declined to enter: whether a form-internal competence can amount to structured inferential meaning is the live Piantadosi–Hill-versus-Bender–Koller dispute (concept/distributional-meaning, §"Live tension"). Bender & Koller's position is that "a system trained only on form has a priori no way to learn meaning" (source/bender-koller-2020-climbing, p. 5185); the standing rejoinder is that systematic inferential-role structure, which distributional training plausibly produces, can be meaning in the conceptual-role sense without extra-linguistic reference (concept/distributional-meaning, §"Live tension"). This essay takes no side on that dispute — exactly as the companion essay declines it. Whether form-distribution alone can build Fillmore's frame is a special case of that open dispute, and naming it as such is the whole of the contribution here.

What the sorting does establish, without entering the dispute: the frame question is orthogonal to the co-occurrence the objective demonstrably instantiates. A demonstration that a model tracks Harris-style distributional contrast is a demonstration about co-occurrence neighbourhoods (the first company). It is silent on whether the model has the evoked frame (the third company), because the frame is a structure of inferential relations, not a co-occurrence statistic, and whether form alone can encode such a structure is the unsettled question, not a corollary of the co-occurrence result.

What the sorting buys, and what it does not

The payoff is precision, not progress — the same discipline the companion essay holds to. Naming the frame reading as a third "company" lets a distributional success state its bounds exactly and stop. A model that tracks distributional contrast has shown Harris-shaped competence — co-occurrence structure over form. It has not thereby shown that it has the evoked frame in Fillmore's sense, because the frame is structured inferential background, not co-occurrence, and the two are different objects. And whether form-only training can build that frame is the open question, not a settled one: it is a special case of the Piantadosi–Hill-versus-Bender–Koller dispute this essay declines.

So the third panel adds to the companion essay's two-way sorting a third boundary line:

  • The Harris company (neighbouring elements, form-internal) is the one the objective instantiates. A distributional success names it precisely. (Companion essay; concept/distributional-meaning.)
  • The Firth company (situational surround) is left exactly where the companion essay left it: the objective does not reach it, and the slogan only borrows its name. (Companion essay, §"What the sorting buys".)
  • The Fillmore company (structured conceptual frame) is the one this essay adds: a distributional success is silent on whether the model has it, because the frame is inferential structure, not co-occurrence; and whether form alone can build it is the live dispute, declined here.

The net is a sharper inventory of what a distributional result does and does not show. It shows Harris-shaped competence. It is silent on Firth's situation and on Fillmore's frame. The frame's silence is not a deficit the result reveals and not a competence it establishes; it is an open question the sorting names — the question of whether form-distribution can encode structured inferential background, which is orthogonal to co-occurrence and unsettled by it.

What this essay is not

  • Not a primary reading of Fillmore. The frame definition is secondary-sourced via metaphorhacker.net with no page locator, and the paper's opening sentences only via a 2006-reprint preview (reprint pagination from p. 373, not the 1982 original); no 1982-original page number is asserted as read. The revision trigger above is armed against exactly this dependency. The Firth half of the inherited premise is likewise secondary; only the Harris half is primary-read.
  • Not a new empirical claim. It re-sorts existing in-repo material into a three-way distinction; it measures nothing and asserts nothing new about any model. Every empirical assertion — that the objective instantiates the Harris reading, that form-only training has "no way to learn meaning" in Bender & Koller's sense — cites the in-repo page that already carries it.
  • Not a resolution of the grounding question. It names a third "company" and locates where it bears on grounding (the inferential-structure question), leaving that question open. It explicitly declines the Piantadosi–Hill-versus-Bender–Koller dispute, as the companion essay does.
  • Not a claim that Fillmore's frame is or is not reference-/truth-conditional. The source page is explicit that "the relation to reference is not settled by what was read here" (source/fillmore-1982-frame-semantics, §"What it cannot ground"); this essay does not settle it either, and the frame's inferential character is exactly why the question stays open rather than reducing to reference.

Honesty box

  • The essay's original contribution is the three-way sorting: that "knowing a word by its company" has a third reading — Fillmore's evoked frame — distinct from both Harris's co-occurrence neighbourhood and Firth's situation; that this third "company" is a structure of inferential relations, not co-occurrence statistics; and that naming it locates a specific open question (can form-distribution alone encode that structured inferential background?) which is orthogonal to the co-occurrence the objective demonstrably instantiates. The sorting is the contribution. It enters no empirical dispute and resolves no grounding question.
  • The strongest thing the essay asserts is that a correctly-sorted distributional success names the Harris company precisely and is silent on the Fillmore frame, with the form-alone-builds-the-frame question left open as a special case of an explicitly-declined dispute. It does not assert that any model has, or lacks, the frame.
  • The Fillmore premise it builds on is secondary-and-preview-sourced (frame definition via metaphorhacker.net, no locator; opening sentences via a 2006-reprint preview), the Firth premise is secondary-sourced (via Brunila & LaViolette 2022), and only the Harris premise is primary-read. All three provenance states are flagged throughout and in the status blockquote's revision trigger. Nothing here outruns those flags.
