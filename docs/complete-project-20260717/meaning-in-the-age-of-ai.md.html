Introduction: A Sentence You Already Understood

After the storm, the children ran outside.

You understood that sentence. You did it instantly, without effort, and almost certainly without noticing that anything had happened at all. You know that a storm is a violent spell of weather, that it has now ended, that "the children" are some particular young people the writer has in mind, that "ran" describes them moving quickly on their legs, and that "outside" means out of whatever building they had been sheltering in. You can go further. You can infer that they had been kept indoors, that they were probably glad to be released, that the ground may be wet. None of this was stated. All of it came along, free, riding on a handful of ordinary words.

Now try to say how you did it. Not what the sentence means — that part was easy — but what it is for a string of marks to mean anything at all, and what exactly you were doing when you grasped it. Here the ease vanishes. We are fluent users of meaning and almost completely unable to explain it. We are in the position of someone who can ride a bicycle perfectly but cannot say what keeps it upright, except that the bicycle is language and the thing we cannot explain is the very center of being human.

This essay is about that gap. It is about what meaning is, why the question turns out to be one of the deepest and most stubborn in all of philosophy, and what the great traditions of thought have proposed as answers. And it is about a new and strange development that has thrown the whole question into fresh light: the arrival of machines — large language models, the technology behind systems like ChatGPT and its kin — that produce fluent, apt, often insightful language, while having none of the things we usually assume a meaner must have. No eyes, no hands, no childhood, no body, no life among other people, no world except a vast ocean of text. Do these systems mean anything by the words they so convincingly arrange? And — a harder and more interesting question — how on earth would we tell?

I should say at the outset what kind of book this is. It is an essay, not a textbook and not a manifesto. It will define every technical term in plain words, lean on concrete examples, and try never to mention a thinker or a theory without saying, in everyday language, what the idea actually is. It draws on a small, ongoing research project — a notebook, really — that has been trying to investigate machine meaning not by arguing about it but by probing it: designing careful little experiments and measuring what today's models actually do. I will use that project's framing to organize the ideas, and I will use a few of its experiments, sparingly, to show how such questions can be put to the test rather than merely debated. But the experiments are illustrations, not the point. The point is the ideas. And a warning that will echo through every chapter: the honest answers here are modest ones. Anyone who tells you confidently that the machines obviously understand, or that they obviously understand nothing, has stopped paying attention to a question that is genuinely, productively hard.

Let us begin where the difficulty does.

The Puzzle of Meaning

Here is a small experiment you can run with a dictionary. Pick a word — say, meaning itself — and look it up. You will be given other words: significance, sense, import. Look those up. You will be given still other words. Keep going. You will never, at any point, be handed something that is not a word. You will travel forever from symbol to symbol and never arrive at the things the symbols are about. The dictionary is a closed loop. It defines language entirely in terms of more language, like a map whose only feature is other maps.

This little merry-go-round — we will meet a sharper, more famous version of it later — is the first hint that meaning is harder than it looks. If words got their meaning purely from other words, the whole system would be a magnificent structure floating in midair, anchored to nothing. Somehow, for us, it is anchored. The word wasp connects not only to insect and sting but to actual wasps, the ones that ruin picnics. Where does that connection live, and what is it made of?

The naive theory — the one most of us would blurt out if asked — is that a word is a label for a thing. Wasp is the label we stick on wasps; Venus is the label for that bright planet; run is the label for that thing legs do. This theory is not crazy. It captures something real. But it falls apart the moment you press on it, and the ways it falls apart are a quick tour of why meaning is deep.

Start with the fact that one word can wear two unrelated meanings. A bat is a small flying mammal; a bat is also a wooden club for hitting a ball. These two meanings have nothing to do with each other — they merely happen to be spelled and pronounced the same, an accident of linguistic history. A bank is the sloping edge of a river and also a place that keeps your money. If words were simply labels for things, this double life would be baffling. The label theory has no room for one label honestly naming two unrelated things.

Now go the other way: two different words, or phrases, for a single thing. The ancients knew a bright object that appears in the morning sky and called it the morning star, and a bright object in the evening sky they called the evening star. It was a genuine astronomical discovery that these are the same body — the planet Venus. Here is the puzzle the philosopher Gottlob Frege drew out of this in the nineteenth century. If the meaning of a name were simply the thing it points to, then "the morning star is the evening star" would mean exactly the same as "the morning star is the morning star" — both just say Venus is Venus. But the first is a real discovery you could be surprised by, and the second is an empty truism. So the meaning of a phrase cannot be just the object it picks out. There must be a second layer: not only what a phrase refers to, but the way it presents that thing — the mode of presentation, the angle of approach. We will give these two layers their proper names soon. For now, just register that meaning already has at least two floors, and the label theory built only one.

It gets worse for the label theory. Plenty of perfectly meaningful words label nothing at all. There are no unicorns, yet unicorn means something precise — you can sort pictures into unicorns and non-unicorns. And some words refer to nothing because they are not the kind of word that points at objects in the first place. What object is justice a label for? What thing does and name, or very, or of? You understand all of these completely, and not one of them works by labeling a thing in the world. Whatever meaning is, it cannot in general be a relation between a word and an object, because for vast stretches of language there is no object.

And consider how loose, how shaded, how alive word meanings are. Think of all the things run. A person runs; a river runs; a nose runs; an engine runs; a play runs for six months; a stocking runs; you run a business, run a risk, run for office, run out of milk. These are not unrelated accidents like the two bats. They shade into one another. They share a faint family feeling — something to do with continuous movement, flow, operation — that you can sense without being able to pin down. Where does one sense of run stop and the next begin? Often there is no fact of the matter. You can construct a sentence — the new software runs — that sits genuinely between two senses, belonging fully to neither. Meaning, up close, is not a tidy set of boxes. It is a landscape of gradients and overlaps.

So the simple picture — words are labels, sentences are strings of labels — is hopeless. Meaning is layered (sense and reference), it floats free of objects for huge parts of the vocabulary, it lets one form carry unrelated senses and lets many forms share one referent, and it is graded and shifting rather than crisp. Any real theory has to cope with all of that.

For most of human history this was a problem for philosophers and lexicographers, of great depth but no particular urgency. What has made it urgent — what has turned an old puzzle into a live and almost experimental question — is that we have now built machines that traffic in meaning at industrial scale, and we do not know what, if anything, they are doing. To even pose that question clearly, we need the great theories of meaning laid out in plain view. There are, very roughly, four big ideas about where meaning comes from, and they are best understood as four different answers to a single question: what is it that a word's meaning ultimately consists in? Patterns inside language and its use. A grip on the world. The inferences a word licenses. And an anchoring in perception, action, and a shared life. The next four chapters take these in turn.

The Company a Word Keeps

The first great idea locates meaning inside language and its use — not in a relation reaching out to the world, but in the patterns words form among themselves and in the human activities they are woven into. It has two strands, developed by different people for different reasons, that turn out to be cousins. Both will matter enormously when we come to the machines, because both describe something a machine made of text could, in principle, actually have.

The first strand is an idea from linguistics, often associated with the British linguist J. R. Firth, who put it in a phrase that has never been bettered: a word is known by the company it keeps. The thought is this. Forget, for a moment, about what dog points to in the world. Look instead at the words that tend to cluster around dog in actual usage: bark, leash, tail, bone, fetch, loyal, bite, kennel. That cloud of companions is a remarkably rich fingerprint. It distinguishes dog from cathedral (whose companions are spire, nave, bishop, Gothic) and even from the closely related cat (more purr, aloof, litter; less fetch, loyal). The claim — the distributional hypothesis, developed rigorously by the linguist Zellig Harris and others — is that a word's meaning is captured, or at the very least closely tracked, by the statistical pattern of contexts it appears in. Meaning is distribution: tell me the company a word keeps across millions of sentences, and you have told me, in large part, what it means.

You can feel the force of this with a trick. Suppose I write: the blicket flapped twice, then settled onto its nest and tucked its head beneath one wing. You have never seen the word blicket, and I have not told you what it means. Yet you now know a great deal: a blicket is a bird, or at least a winged, nesting creature. You learned this purely from the company the word kept — from flapped, nest, wing. This is not a parlor trick; it is, as far as anyone can tell, close to how children actually expand their vocabularies, and it is exactly, precisely, how a modern language model learns. The distributional idea is the deep theory behind the technology we are circling toward. Its great strength is that it explains so much with so little, and that it is mechanical — you can compute it, with no appeal to minds or worlds. Its great weakness is the dictionary merry-go-round in new clothes: knowing the company water keeps (it hangs around drink, wet, river, thirst) is not obviously the same as knowing that water is the wet stuff you can actually drink, still less that it is H₂O. The distribution sits entirely inside language. Whether that inside ever reaches the outside is the question the whole rest of this essay orbits.

The second strand comes from philosophy, from the later work of Ludwig Wittgenstein, and it is more radical. Wittgenstein's slogan, roughly, was: don't ask what a word names; ask how it is used. Meaning, on this view, is not a thing — not an object, not a mental picture, not a definition hiding behind the word. Meaning is a role in an activity. He pictured language as a vast collection of overlapping practices, which he called language-games: giving orders, describing, joking, thanking, cursing, greeting, counting, praying, telling a story. To know what a word means is to know how to play your part with it in these games, the way knowing what a chess bishop "means" is just knowing how it moves and what it does in the game. There is no further fact, no inner essence, behind the use. The use is all there is.

This sounds slippery until you see the example Wittgenstein used to break the back of the old picture. What, he asked, is the meaning of the word game itself? Surely, you think, there must be some feature common to all games — some definition. Try to find it. Board games, card games, ball games, the Olympic games, children's ring games, a child throwing a ball against a wall, solitaire. Some involve winning and losing; solitaire and the child with the ball do not. Some involve competition; not all. Some require skill, some luck, some neither. Amusement? Not always. There is no single thread running through all of them. What there is instead is a web of overlapping, crisscrossing similarities — this game like that one in one respect, that one like a third in another — the way members of a family share a look without any one feature being common to all. Wittgenstein called this family resemblance, and it is a permanent correction to the idea that a word's meaning is a tidy definition. Many of our most ordinary words are held together not by a shared core but by a tangle of resemblances, and they work perfectly well that way.

Notice how close these two strands are, for all that one comes from a linguist counting words and the other from a philosopher dissolving puzzles. Both say that meaning lives in patterns — patterns of company, patterns of use — rather than in a private mental object or a label pasted on a thing. Both treat the dream of crisp definitions with suspicion and the messy gradience of real language as the normal case, not a flaw. And both, crucially, describe a kind of meaning that you could have without ever leaving language — a meaning made entirely of relationships among words and the things people do with them. Hold that thought. A system built from nothing but the statistics of text is, almost by definition, a creature of exactly this kind of meaning, if it has any meaning at all. Which is precisely why the rival tradition — the one that insists meaning must reach outside language, to the world — is the one that bites hardest against the machines.

Reaching for the World

Against the idea that meaning is patterns inside language stands a powerful and stubborn intuition: that meaning is, at bottom, about the world. When you say "there is a wasp on your collar," your words mean something because there are wasps, and collars, and one of the former is on one of the latter, and what you said is true or false depending on how things actually stand. Language is not a closed game; it is how we get a grip on reality, warn each other, describe what is there. Strip out the world and you have not stripped out a detail. You have stripped out the point.

This tradition gives us the two-floor structure of meaning we glimpsed with the morning star. The bottom floor is reference: what a word or phrase picks out in the world. The reference of Venus is that particular planet; the reference of the tallest mountain on Earth is Everest; the reference of dog, roughly, is the dogs. The second floor is sense — Frege's term was Sinn — the mode of presentation, the particular way a thing is given to us. "The morning star" and "the evening star" have the same reference (Venus) but different senses (the bright thing seen at dawn versus the bright thing seen at dusk). It is because they differ in sense that discovering they share a reference was real news. This sense/reference distinction is one of the permanent achievements of the philosophy of language, and it will return when we ask what a machine could be tracking: it is comparatively easy to imagine a text-trained system picking up something sense-like (the morning-star contexts do differ from the evening-star contexts, statistically), and much harder to see how it could grip reference — the actual planet — which never appears in any text, only words about it.

For whole sentences, the world-facing tradition offers an elegant proposal: to know what a (declarative) sentence means is to know what the world would have to be like for it to be true — its truth-conditions. "Snow is white" is true if and only if snow is white; to understand it is to grasp that condition, to know which way the world must be for the sentence to hold. This sounds almost too simple to be a theory, but in the hands of logicians and philosophers — Alfred Tarski, who made "is true" precise for formal languages; Donald Davidson, who proposed truth-conditions as the backbone of a theory of meaning for ordinary language; Richard Montague, who showed that fragments of English could be handled with the same mathematical machinery used for logic — it became a powerful and rigorous research program, often called formal or truth-conditional semantics. Its governing image is that meaning is a function from words to the world: a systematic mapping that takes expressions and delivers the conditions, out in reality, that would make them true.

Now comes the deepest twist in this tradition, and one of the most ingenious arguments in modern philosophy: the discovery that meaning might depend on the world in a way that reaches outside the speaker's own head. This is externalism, and its founding thought experiment, due to the philosopher Hilary Putnam, is worth telling slowly because it is so clarifying.

Imagine a planet — call it Twin Earth — that is exactly like ours in every detail but one. There is a Twin Earth version of you, molecule for molecule identical, with all the same thoughts and feelings. On Twin Earth there are oceans and rain and a clear, drinkable liquid that everyone calls "water," that fills the rivers and falls from the sky and quenches thirst, exactly as here. The single difference is invisible: this liquid is not H₂O. Its chemistry is some other formula — call it XYZ — but it looks, tastes, and behaves just like water in every way an ordinary person could notice. Now: when your Twin says "water," and you say "water," do you mean the same thing? Putnam's argument is that you do not. You mean H₂O; your Twin means XYZ; the word in each mouth is locked onto a different substance — and the difference lies not in your heads, which are identical, but in the actual stuff in your two environments. Meaning, Putnam concluded in a famous phrase, "just ain't in the head." Part of what your words mean is fixed by the world they grew up pointing at, whether or not you can tell the difference.

Putnam added a second, more homely observation he called the division of linguistic labor. You probably use the word elm correctly — you can talk about elms, mean elms, say true things about them — even though, if pressed, you could not reliably tell an elm from a beech. How can you mean elms if you cannot identify them? Because you are part of a community that contains experts who can, and your word borrows its precision from the collective. Meaning is partly a social possession, held by the language community and the world together, not minted fresh in each private skull. (The philosopher Tyler Burge pushed this further: even your own grasp of a concept can be partly fixed by how your community uses the word, so that what you mean is not entirely up to you. And Gareth Evans argued that a name like Aristotle reaches its bearer not through any description you carry but through a long causal chain of usage running back to the man himself — a kind of historical handing-down.)

Externalism matters here because it draws the sharpest possible line for the machine question. If part of what a word means is fixed by a causal and social connection to the actual world — to the real H₂O, the real elms, the community of users embedded in an environment — then a system that has only ever seen text is, in principle, cut off from exactly the thing that fixes reference. It has no environment to be embedded in, no causal chain back to any baptism, no membership in the community of users. There is, as it were, no Twin Earth you could move it to, because it was never on any Earth to begin with. (There is a rival, internalist camp — Noam Chomsky and Jerry Fodor are the usual reference points — which holds that the content properly studied by a science of the mind is the "narrow" content inside the head, and that word-to-world reference may not be a topic for that science at all. On their gentler verdict, a text system might have something — narrow content of the only kind they think is real — without that something amounting to reference in the externalist's demanding sense. The two camps, notice, are answering different questions: the externalist's "no" and the internalist's "yes, but thin" are not really contradicting each other.)

Beneath reference and externalism lies the most visceral version of the world-facing intuition: that meaning has to be grounded in perception, action, and the body — that symbols must, somewhere, touch down on something that is not another symbol. This is where the dictionary merry-go-round of Chapter 1 returns in its sharpest form. The cognitive scientist Stevan Harnad called it the symbol grounding problem, and his image was exactly our dictionary, made cruel: imagine trying to learn Chinese armed only with a Chinese-to-Chinese dictionary. Every word is defined using other words, each of which sends you to other words, in an endless merry-go-round that never once touches down on a thing. You could ride it forever and never learn that one of these symbols means cat, because nothing in the loop ever connects a symbol to an actual cat, seen or heard or stroked. Harnad's point was that a purely symbolic system — one that only ever shuffles tokens according to other tokens — has this problem built in. To break out of the loop, some symbols have to be grounded: hooked, by perception and action, to the sensory world. Cat has to connect, somewhere down the line, to the look and feel and yowl of cats, or it is just a counter in a game with no outside.

A whole family of theories in cognitive science, gathered under the banner of embodied cognition, takes this further and proposes that meaning is not merely hooked to perception but partly made of it. The psychologist Lawrence Barsalou argued that a concept like chair is not an abstract definition filed in some amodal mental database, but is closer to a stored capacity to re-enact perceiving and using chairs — a partial simulation of sitting, of the look of a seat and a back. To think the concept is, in part, to run a faint rehearsal of the relevant experience. On these views, a creature with no senses and no body — no experience to simulate, nothing to ground the symbols in — is missing not a footnote but the foundation. Its words, however fluently arranged, would be ungrounded all the way down: the dictionary merry-go-round, spinning forever.

This is the tradition that presses hardest against the idea of a meaning machine made of text. It says: you can have all the company-keeping and use-patterns you like, but if your symbols never touch the world — through reference, through a causal-social embedding, through perception and the body — then you have an elaborate, self-referential game and not meaning at all. Whether that verdict is right, or too quick, is something we will be in a much better position to judge after the remaining two ideas, and after we meet the machines themselves.

What Words Let You Conclude

There is a third place one might locate meaning, neither purely in the company words keep nor in a grip on the world, but in the inferences a word licenses — what it lets you conclude, and what it lets you be concluded from. This is the idea of meaning as inferential role, and once you see it, it is everywhere.

Consider the word and. What is its meaning? Not a thing it labels — there is no object and points to. What there is, instead, is a pattern of inference: from "the door is locked and the lights are off" you may conclude "the door is locked," and you may conclude "the lights are off," and from those two together you may rebuild the original. To know what and means simply is to be master of those moves. Or take bachelor. To understand it is to be prepared to infer, from "Tom is a bachelor," that Tom is unmarried, and that Tom is a man, and to refuse the inference that Tom has a wife. The meaning is the bundle of licensed inferences. A word means what it lets you reason to and from.

The appeal of this idea is that it offers meaning without requiring a relation to the world — which is exactly what you would want for the abstract words that defeated the label theory. Justice, wit, democracy, prime number: none of these labels a touchable thing, yet each is richly meaningful, and the inferentialist can say why. Their meaning is their inferential role — the dense network of what follows from what. The cognitive scientists Steven Piantadosi and Felix Hill, writing directly about language models, put the case in its strongest pro-machine form. Meaning, they argue, can arise from conceptual role — from the relationships a representation has to other representations inside a system — and reference to the outside world is not required for it. There are, they note, many words that are perfectly meaningful to us and have no referent at all; and means logical conjunction not because it points at anything but because of how it composes with true and false and the other logical words — because it has the right role. If that is what meaning is, then a system whose internal states stand in the right web of relations might genuinely share the foundation of how our concepts get their meaning, even with no senses and no world. This is the most serious case that a text machine could mean something, and we will weigh it.

But there is a fork in the inferentialist road, and it matters intensely for the machine question, so it is worth marking clearly. The version just described is internalist: meaning is constituted by relations among states inside one system. There is a second, more demanding version, developed by the philosopher Robert Brandom (building on Wilfrid Sellars), on which the inferential roles that constitute meaning are not private affairs inside a head but social and normative — moves in what Brandom called "the game of giving and asking for reasons." On this picture, to mean something is to undertake commitments and acquire entitlements that other people keep track of: if I assert something, I commit myself to its consequences and lay myself open to your challenge, "what's your reason?" Content, for Brandom, is a kind of social standing — a status in a shared practice of holding each other to account — and not a causal-functional property you could have alone in the dark. Meaning, in this strong sense, lives between people, in the normative back-and-forth, not inside any one of them.

The two inferentialisms point in opposite directions when we turn to the machines. The internalist version is, in principle, satisfiable inside a single model: if meaning is just the right web of internal relations, a system rich enough in such relations could have it without ever talking to anyone. The Brandomian version is not satisfiable that way at all: it would require the system to be a genuine participant in a social practice of commitment and challenge, a player in the game of reasons — to mean things with others, answerable to them. This is not a quibble. It is a disagreement about where meaning even resides — within one reasoner, or between several — and, as we will see, it suggests two completely different experiments. If the machines mean anything inferentially, is that something each one has on its own, or something that could only arise between them? Keep the fork in mind; we will come back and stand at it with actual measurements.

How Meaning Is Built, and How It Hangs Together

The three ideas so far — company and use, world and grounding, inference — are rival answers to what meaning consists in. Cutting across all of them are two structural facts about meaning that any theory has to respect, and that give us some of the sharpest tools for testing a machine. The first is about how meanings combine into bigger meanings. The second is about how tightly each meaning depends on all the others.

Start with combination. You have never heard the sentence the purple accountant apologized to a nervous walrus, yet you understood it the instant you read it. How? Because you know the words and you know the way they are put together, and from those two things the meaning of the whole assembles itself automatically. This is the principle of compositionality, usually credited to Frege: the meaning of a complex expression is a function of the meanings of its parts and of the way they are combined. Fix the parts, fix the arrangement, and the meaning of the whole is determined — nothing extra is needed, nothing comes from nowhere.

Compositionality is not an idle observation; it is what makes human language the unbounded thing it is. Two features force it on us. One is productivity: from a finite stock of words and a finite set of combining rules, we generate and understand an infinity of sentences, including endless ones never before uttered. There is no way to learn an infinite language sentence by sentence; the only way is to learn the parts and the rules and let them compose. The other is systematicity: the ability to understand "the dog chased the cat" is not a separate skill from the ability to understand "the cat chased the dog." Anyone who has one has the other, automatically, because the two are built from the same parts under the same rules. That tight coupling of abilities is exactly what compositionality predicts and what a system that merely memorized whole sentences could not explain. (This will turn into a precise test for machines: a system that has truly composed a meaning, rather than matched a remembered pattern, should handle the recombination just as well — and we will see a striking case where today's models do.)

The second structural idea pushes back on the tidy picture left over from the label theory: that a word's meaning is a definition, a checklist of necessary and sufficient conditions. Two traditions in cognitive science dismantled that. The first is frame semantics, due to the linguist Charles Fillmore. His insight was that many words can only be understood against a whole structured scene of background knowledge — a frame. You cannot grasp buy in isolation; buy evokes an entire commercial-transaction scene with fixed roles — a buyer, a seller, goods, money — and so do sell, pay, cost, spend, and charge. Those are not six unrelated meanings but six camera angles on one underlying event. To know any of them is to have the whole frame and to know which participants it puts in the foreground. Meaning, on this view, is not a label but a way of accessing and profiling a structured chunk of knowledge about how the world hangs together.

The second is prototype theory, from the psychologist Eleanor Rosch, and it is the death blow to the definition picture. Rosch showed, experimentally, that our categories have graded internal structure. Ask people for a bird and they say robin, not penguin; a robin is felt to be a "better," more central example of bird than a penguin or an ostrich or a kiwi, and people verify "a robin is a bird" faster than "a penguin is a bird." Categories, it turns out, are organized around central prototypes, with membership a matter of resemblance to the center and boundaries that are genuinely fuzzy rather than sharp. Is a rug a piece of furniture? A telephone? There is often no fact of the matter — only more or less central cases shading off into the doubtful. The linguist George Lakoff extended this from categories of things to the senses of a single word: a word's many senses radiate from a central one by metaphor and extension, like the senses of run we met earlier, forming a structured network rather than a list. This is the gradience we saw in Chapter 1, now given a theory — and it connects straight back to Wittgenstein's family resemblance. Meaning is graded, prototype-centered, frame-relative. Crisp definitions are the exception, not the rule.

The last structural idea is the most vertiginous, and it concerns how interdependent meanings are. How much of a language do you need in order to fix the meaning of one word in it? At one extreme is atomism: each word's meaning is self-contained; you could in principle know what dog means while knowing nothing else. At the other is holism: the meaning of any word is fixed by its place in the entire web of the language, so that nothing means anything except against the background of everything. In between sits molecularism: meaning is fixed by some bounded, privileged chunk of an expression's connections — more than the lone atom, less than the whole web. The philosopher W. V. O. Quine pushed hard toward holism with the observation that our beliefs face the test of experience not one at a time but as a connected body; you cannot revise one without rippling the rest. The trouble, as the philosophers Jerry Fodor and Ernest Lepore pressed, is that strong holism has an alarming consequence: if a word's meaning is its place in your entire web, then since no two people's webs are identical, no two people ever mean exactly the same thing by any word — and, more unsettling still, you do not mean the same thing today as yesterday, because you have learned things in between and the web has shifted. Taken to the limit, shared meaning, and so communication itself, starts to look impossible. (Holists reply that exact sameness was the wrong demand — close-enough similarity of role is plenty for us to understand each other — and the debate runs on.)

Why drag in this abstract quarrel? Because it lands directly on the machines, which are holistic by construction. In a modern language model, a word has no self-standing atom of meaning; it is represented by its relationships to every other word, a position defined entirely against the whole. So the holism objection — and the question of whether mere similarity of webs is enough for two systems, or a system and a person, to share meaning — is not a philosopher's abstraction when we turn to them. It is a fact about how they are built, with the philosophical worry attached.

We now have the map. Four ideas about what meaning is — the company words keep, a grip on the world, the inferences words license, an anchoring in perception and a shared life — and two structural facts about how meanings combine and how they hang together. Each idea, notice, makes a different prediction about a creature of pure text. The company-and-use idea says such a creature could have a real kind of meaning. The world-and-grounding idea says it could not, in principle, because it never touches the world. The inferential idea splits: the internalist branch says yes, the social branch says only between agents. And the structural facts hand us tools — does it compose? does it track graded senses? — sharp enough to actually measure. It is time to meet the creature.

Enter the Machines

What is a large language model, really, underneath the conversational polish? Strip away the chat interface and the helpful persona, and you find something startlingly simple to describe and startlingly hard to believe could do what it does. A large language model is a system trained to do one thing: predict the next chunk of text. Show it a stretch of writing with the continuation hidden, and it learns to guess what comes next — which word, which punctuation, which turn of phrase is likely to follow. Do this over an almost unimaginable quantity of human writing — books, articles, conversations, code, the accumulated text of the species — and adjust the system relentlessly toward better and better guesses, and something remarkable happens. To predict text well, it turns out, you have to absorb an enormous amount of the structure latent in that text: grammar, idiom, fact, style, the rhythms of argument, the shape of a joke. The model is, at its core, a magnificently elaborate engine for "the company words keep" — Chapter 2's distributional idea, scaled to the entire written output of humanity and run on machines the size of warehouses.

Now hold this plain description against everything in Chapters 3 through 5, and feel the friction. The model has no eyes and no hands. It has never seen a sunset or stubbed a toe or been thirsty. It has no environment to be embedded in, no causal chain back to any real elm or any real Venus, no membership in a community of speakers it shares a world with, no body whose sensorimotor life could ground a single symbol, no goals of its own in the basic case, and — this is the crux — no access to anything except text. It is a near-pure instance of the one tradition (company and use) with everything the other traditions insist you also need (reference, world, grounding, body, social practice) conspicuously, almost experimentally, removed. If you had set out to build a test case to pry the theories of meaning apart, you could hardly have designed a cleaner one. That is why the arrival of these systems has electrified the old question. They are not a distraction from the philosophy of meaning. They are, accidentally, its laboratory.

The sharpest way to feel the worry was put by the linguists Emily Bender and Alexander Koller in a thought experiment that has become the field's reference point: the hyper-intelligent octopus. Picture two people on two desert islands who have learned to talk to each other by telegraph along an undersea cable. Now picture a fabulously clever octopus that taps the cable and listens. The octopus has never seen an island, a coconut, a rope, or a human being; it knows nothing of what the chatter is about. But it is a brilliant statistician, and over time it learns the patterns of the conversation so well that it can predict, with uncanny accuracy, what one islander will say in reply to the other. One day it cuts in and starts answering in place of one islander — and, for a while, the other notices nothing wrong. The patterns are perfect. Then the trouble comes. One islander, pursued by an angry bear, frantically signals for help: quick, how do I build a weapon from these sticks and this rope? And the octopus, who has only ever seen the forms of words and never the bears or sticks or fear they were about, has nothing useful to send back. It can produce plausible-sounding text; it cannot help, because it never had access to the meanings — the world and the intents — that the forms were riding on. Bender and Koller's conclusion is uncompromising, and I will give it in their exact words, because it is the cleanest statement of the skeptical case: "a system trained only on form has a priori no way to learn meaning." Form, the patterns in the text, is all the octopus — and all the model — ever sees; and form, they argue, is simply not enough.

It is worth being precise about what kind of claim this is, because the precision will matter enormously later. Bender and Koller's argument is in principle, and it is about the training signal, not about behavior. They are not predicting that the model will fail this or that test. They are saying that the thing that fixes meaning — the relation between forms and the world-and-intents they are used to convey — is simply absent from text, so that no amount of skill at the text could constitute having it. This has a startling consequence we will return to: if the argument is right, then no behavioral test whatsoever could refute it. A model that aced every probe we could devise would still, on this view, be producing form, and form was never the thing in question. We will see that this cuts both ways, and that it is the key to staying honest.

The skeptical case has a slogan, too, from an influential 2021 paper co-authored by Bender and colleagues: the language model as a stochastic parrot — a system that stitches together sequences of linguistic forms according to probabilistic patterns, "stochastic" meaning randomly-driven, with no comprehension of meaning behind the stitching. The phrase has become shorthand for the strong skeptical position: there is nothing there but sophisticated pattern-completion; to speak of the model "meaning" or "understanding" anything is a category mistake, a projection.

So one end of the spectrum is staked out: eliminativism, the view that there is simply no meaning here at all. But it is a spectrum, and it is worth laying the whole thing out, because most of the public conversation collapses it into a shouting match between the two extremes. Moving inward from "no meaning at all," the next position is deflationism: yes, there is real structure in what the model does — genuine, systematic, surprising structure — but it is just distributional, nothing over and above the company words keep; you do not need to posit understanding or reference to describe it, only very rich statistics. Further along is the gradualist middle, articulated by the philosopher Hannes Lyre, who argues that grounding and meaning are not all-or-nothing but come in degrees along several dimensions, and who reaches a deliberately hedged verdict: today's models, he suggests, are "neither stochastic parrots nor semantic zombies," but already "understand the language they generate, at least in an elementary sense." And at the far end sits inflationism: the view, whose strongest version is the internalist inferential-role theory of the last chapter, that these systems have genuine — if strange and non-human — meaning.

Here is the temptation, and the trap. Almost everyone who weighs in treats this as a yes-or-no question to be won: do they understand, or don't they? Pick a side. But step back and look at the two ends of the spectrum together, and a deep problem appears. The strongest "no" — Bender and Koller's — is, as we just saw, an in-principle claim about the training signal that no behavior can touch. And the strongest "yes" can always be deflated: any impressive feat can be re-described as "richer statistics than you thought," and no experiment can rule that re-description out. The yes/no question, in other words, is underdetermined by any evidence we could actually gather. The two camps are not so much disagreeing about the facts as talking past each other about what would even count as a fact.

This is the realization that reorganizes everything, and it is the stance of the research project this essay draws on: stop trying to litigate the yes/no, and start trying to describe. Treat whatever the models are doing as a genuine phenomenon worth characterizing carefully — not "is it meaning, yes or no?" but "what, precisely, is the structure here? What does the model track, and what does it miss? Which of the things our theories say meaning requires does it seem to have, and which does it conspicuously lack?" This is not a dodge or a refusal to take a stand. It is the only rational response to a question that the available evidence genuinely cannot settle. Describe, don't litigate. And to describe rather than litigate, you need two things the shouting match never supplies: test cases narrow enough to actually measure, and a method disciplined enough to be believed. Those are the next two chapters.

Why Words and Grammar Are the Sharp Edge

"Does the machine understand?" is too big a question to measure. It is like asking "is she healthy?" — meaningful, but you cannot put a single instrument to it. What a doctor does instead is measure specific, well-defined things — blood pressure, temperature, a particular enzyme — that bear on health without being it. The equivalent move for meaning is to stop probing "understanding" in the abstract and find narrow, precise, well-studied phenomena where the difference between tracking meaning and merely tracking surface patterns becomes sharp enough to see. Two such phenomena, one about words and one about grammar, turn out to be unusually good for this. They are the sharp edge of the whole inquiry.

The first is word sense, and specifically the way a single word's uses shade from closely related to utterly unrelated. We met the raw fact in Chapter 1. There are two cases to distinguish. Homonymy is the accidental kind: one spelling, two unrelated meanings, like bat the flying mammal and bat the wooden club, or bank the riverside and bank the place for money. These do not shade into each other at all; they are simply two words wearing the same coat, and a competent speaker keeps them cleanly apart. Polysemy is the deep and interesting kind: one word, several related senses that genuinely shade into one another — paper as a material, a newspaper, an academic article; run in its dozens of linked uses. Here there are in-between cases, bridging contexts where two senses are both alive at once and no sharp line can be drawn. This gradience — the fact that sense-relatedness is a smooth scale, not a set of boxes — is the lexicographer's daily reality and, for our purposes, a gift. Because it is graded, it can be measured. You can take a pile of sentence pairs that use the same word, ask careful human judges how related the two uses are on a scale, and get a graded human signal of real meaning. And then you can ask a machine for the same graded judgments and see whether it tracks the human gradient — or whether it is only responding to something shallower, like how similar the surrounding topics are. That contrast, between tracking sense and tracking mere topic-overlap, is exactly the kind of clean, measurable difference the vague "understanding" question never offered.

The second sharp phenomenon is more surprising, because it lives in grammar, where most people assume meaning is not. Consider this sentence: she sneezed the napkin off the table. Read it again. You understood instantly that the napkin flew off the table, and that the sneeze is what propelled it. But look at where that meaning came from. The verb is sneeze — and sneeze is not a verb of pushing or propelling. You sneeze; sneezing is just a thing that happens in your face. Nothing in the word sneeze says anything about moving a napkin. Yet the sentence unmistakably means the napkin was caused to move. So where did the causing-to-move come from? Not from any word. It came from the pattern — the grammatical construction "X VERB Y off/into/across Z," which carries a meaning of its own: caused motion. The construction takes the humble, motionless verb sneeze and coerces it into a verb of propulsion. The grammar is doing semantic work that none of the words can do alone.

This is the central claim of Construction Grammar, developed by the linguist Adele Goldberg and others: that grammar is not an empty scaffolding into which word-meanings are slotted, but is itself stuffed with meaning. A construction is a pairing of a form with a meaning — and these pairings exist at every level, from individual words up to abstract sentence patterns. Another textbook example: Mia whistled her way down the hall. The verb whistle has nothing to do with travel, yet you know Mia went down the hall — the "way" construction (VERB one's way somewhere) supplies the journey, and again coerces the verb. The word for this — the construction overriding a verb that does not, on its own, carry the relevant meaning — is coercion, and it is the single most useful phenomenon in the whole investigation, for a reason that is worth making explicit.

Coercion is so useful because it separates the construction's contribution from the words' contribution as cleanly as anything in language. When you read that the napkin moved, that inference cannot have come from sneeze, because sneeze is incapable of supplying it. So if a system reliably draws the inference — if it consistently understands that the napkin moved and the sneeze did it, even for odd, made-up combinations it cannot have memorized — then the construction must be doing genuine semantic work in that system. You have found a place where meaning-from-the-pattern can be cleanly told apart from meaning-from-the-words, which is exactly the kind of clean separation the abstract "understanding" question never permitted.

And here is the unifying idea that ties the two sharp phenomena — words and grammar — into one. Construction Grammar's radical proposal is that words and grammatical patterns are not different kinds of thing at all, but two ends of a single continuum. A word is just a maximally specific construction (a form-meaning pairing with a fully fixed form); a sentence pattern like caused-motion is a maximally abstract one (a form-meaning pairing with open slots). In between lie idioms, partly-fixed phrases, and everything else. It is all one inventory of form-meaning pairings, shading from the concrete to the schematic. Which means the word question and the grammar question are not two investigations but one, asked at two grains: does the system track a genuine meaning, or only a surface shadow of it? For words, the surface shadow is topic-overlap. For constructions, it is frequent-phrase memorization. In both cases the real question is whether something survives once you strip the shadow away. That single, sharp, repeatable question — can the model beat the distributional shadow? — is what makes these phenomena the place to actually look. The next chapter is about how you look without fooling yourself.

How to Actually Find Out

Suppose you take the project's advice and stop arguing about whether the machines mean, in order to measure what they do. How, concretely, do you measure meaning in a system you cannot open up and read out? You cannot interview it about its inner life; whatever it says about itself is just more generated text. What you can do is behavioral probing: design careful sentences and questions, present them to the model under controlled conditions, and watch what it does — which continuations it favors, which inferences it draws, how it rates one thing against another. It is the same logic a psychologist uses on a creature that cannot be opened up either: you cannot see the mind, but you can design the situation so that its behavior reveals structure. The art is entirely in the design, and three disciplines separate a real measurement from a flattering illusion.

The first discipline is about what you compare the machine to, and it is the one most easily gotten wrong. The tempting shortcut, when you want to know whether one AI's output is good, is to ask other AIs to judge it. Resist this completely. Today's models are trained on overlapping oceans of the same human text and share deep biases and blind spots; when several of them agree, that agreement is cheap, because they are not independent witnesses — they are siblings raised on the same books. Convergence among AIs tells you that some pattern is easy to learn from text, not that it is true or meaningful. (This is sometimes called the AI-in-the-mirror trap: hold up one model to another and you mostly see a reflection.) The only cure is to anchor every claim to independent human evidence — to datasets of human judgments produced by people, for other purposes, before your experiment ever ran. Not fresh human experiments (the project this essay draws on never tests live human subjects); rather, the great stock of existing human-labeled resources: collections where linguists or annotators have rated how related two uses of a word are, or marked whether two sentences use a word in the same sense, or recorded how real people played a naming game. The human data is the yardstick. The machine is measured against it. AI agreement is never allowed to stand in for the yardstick, no matter how convenient.

The second discipline is the conceptual heart of the whole method, and it has a memorable name in the project: beating the distributional shadow. Recall that a fluent model gets "the company words keep" for free — that is what it was built from. So whenever a model appears to track meaning, there is always a deflationary explanation standing right behind it: maybe it is not tracking meaning at all, only the surface statistics that shadow meaning. When the model seems to know that two uses of paper are related, maybe it is just noticing that the two sentences are about similar topics. When it seems to understand that the napkin moved, maybe it has just seen a million similar sentences and is completing a pattern. The distributional shadow is the null hypothesis — the boring explanation — and it is always there. The entire craft of the method is to design tests where the boring explanation is controlled out, so that if a meaning-signal survives, it has earned the name. Concretely: when probing word sense, you also measure how similar the two surrounding sentences are — the topic overlap — and then ask whether the model's sense-judgment still tracks the human gradient after you statistically subtract the topic overlap. If it does, the model is tracking something beyond mere context-similarity. When probing a construction, you lean on coercion (where, as we saw, the inference cannot come from the words) and on deliberately atypical, low-frequency combinations the model is unlikely to have memorized. If the inference survives even there, memorization cannot be the whole story. You never eliminate the distributional explanation entirely — we will see why that is impossible in principle — but you can force any genuine meaning-signal to clear a real bar, again and again, raising the cost of the deflationary story until it strains.

The third discipline is about temperament more than technique: honesty about negative results. The deep hazard of this kind of work is that a sufficiently motivated researcher can almost always tune a setup until it produces a positive — change the wording, pick the friendly examples, run it until it works. That way lies self-deception dressed as discovery. The countermeasure is to fix the design in advance, freeze it, run it once, and report the result you get — including, especially, the null. A clean negative result — "we tried exactly this, here is precisely how, and we found nothing" — is a real finding, often a more trustworthy one than a positive, and the project treats its nulls as first-class citizens rather than failures to be buried. A program that only ever reports successes is not measuring the world; it is decorating a foregone conclusion.

Two more honesty constraints round out the method, and they keep the conclusions appropriately small. First, scale: the experiments here are small — a handful of models, modest numbers of test items, often a single run. They tell you the direction of an effect, not its precise size, and a result from three models is an interesting orientation, not a law of nature. Second, and most important, the in-principle ceiling we met with the octopus. Because the strongest skeptical argument is about the training signal rather than about behavior, no behavioral result can ever prove that the machine "means." A model that beats every shadow you throw at it is still, to a determined skeptic, producing very good form. This is not a reason for despair; it is a reason for precision. It means the honest output of this work is never a verdict ("the machine understands") but always a careful description with its bearing made explicit: this gradient was tracked, that shadow was beaten, this other thing was not found — and here is exactly what each result does and does not license you to conclude. The method, in short, is built to give the deflationary story every chance to win, to anchor everything to human evidence rather than to the AI mirror, and to write down the losses as faithfully as the wins. That is what lets the results, modest as they are, be believed. So: what do they show?

What We Seem to Be Learning

Everything in this chapter should be read with the cautions of the last one firmly attached. These are small, cheap experiments — a few dozen of them, run for single-digit dollars apiece — using three of today's leading models from three different companies (a Claude model, a GPT model, and a Gemini model, chosen to be genuinely independent), mostly on text alone, usually in a single run with modest samples. They are not the truth about machine meaning. They are first, careful data points, offered as illustrations of how the question can be probed rather than merely argued. With that said, a shape does begin to emerge, and it is more interesting than either cheerleading or dismissal would have predicted.

The clearest positive result is at the word grain, and it beats the distributional shadow. Take a large collection of sentence pairs — each pair using the same word in two contexts — that human annotators have rated, on a graded scale, for how related the two uses are. (Such collections exist; they were built by linguists studying how word meanings drift over time.) Now ask each model to make the very same graded judgment. All three line up with the human ratings strongly — at a level at or above the level at which the human annotators agreed with one another, which is a high bar. More tellingly, this alignment survives the shadow test: when you statistically subtract the model's own sense of how topically similar the two sentences are, the agreement with the human meaning-gradient remains. So the models are tracking word meaning, not merely topic overlap. This is a real, if modest, positive — a case where a meaning-signal cleared the bar that the boring explanation set. The honest bounds: it is a behavioral correlation, not a look inside the model's representations; it is English; it is a single run. But within those bounds, it is a genuine "yes, and not just the shadow."

At the grammar grain, the picture is sharper and more revealing, because it splits into an easy direction and a hard one. When a construction adds a meaning onto a verb — the caused-motion pattern making sneeze the napkin off the table imply the napkin moved, or the "way" construction making whistle one's way down the hall imply travel — the models handle it reliably, drawing the inference almost every time. And this is not a loose reflex. Tested harder, they withhold the inference when the same words are rearranged so that the construction is no longer present ("she sneezed, and the napkin happened to end up on the floor" does not get read as the sneeze causing it), and they back off when the sentence explicitly cancels the meaning ("…but the napkin never moved"). So it really is the construction doing the work, not a blanket habit of saying yes — the meaning is keyed to the pattern. That is the easy direction, and the models are good at it.

The hard direction is when a construction must cancel a meaning the verb normally carries. The test case is the contrast between Maria kicked the ball (she made contact) and Maria kicked at the ball (she may well have missed — the little word at cancels the implication of contact). Here the models do markedly worse, and a carefully matched control confirms the gap is really about direction — adding versus cancelling — and not just about one test being harder. One of the three models even flips its answer depending on how the question is phrased, withholding the contact-inference one way and asserting it another. The tentative generalization, stated modestly because it rests on a small body of single runs: these systems more readily add a construction's inference than suppress a verb's default one. They are better at piling meaning on than at taking it away.

And one construction stands out as impressively robust — the project's strongest case of a grammatical meaning being genuinely tracked rather than faked. It is the "comparative correlative," the the more X, the more Y pattern (the harder you push, the more it resists). The models not only handle it; they compose with it. Chain two steps together where both involve a decrease, and the correct combined answer is an increase — two negatives making a positive ("the less he sleeps, the less rested he feels, so the less rested he feels, the more he relies on coffee," tracked correctly through the reversal) — and the models get it right, where a shallow "the-more-means-increase" reflex would fail. Better still, when the pattern is placed under a negation or a hedge ("it is not the case that the more X, the more Y"; "it remains unproven whether…"), two of the three models correctly stop drawing the covariation. That is exactly the systematicity that Chapter 5 said genuine composition should show — the meaning built up from the parts and their arrangement, surviving recombination and reversal — and it is the cleanest evidence in the whole project that a construction's meaning is being computed, not pattern-matched. (Even here the caution holds: the tests run near the ceiling of difficulty, and ceiling performance on a relatively easy instrument is weaker evidence than it looks for any deep-processing story. How you ask still matters.)

Now the nulls — the negative results, which the method treats as just as informative, and which map onto the two traditions that press hardest against machine meaning: grounding, and meaning-between-agents.

On grounding — the body, the senses, the world — the project ran the most natural first test and got a clean, instructive null. Take clear homonyms, the bat animal versus bat the club case, and show the models actual little photographs depicting each use, asking whether seeing the picture sharpens the model's grasp of which sense is which. It does not. And the reason it does not is itself the finding: the models already separate the two senses perfectly from the text alone. The picture had nothing to add, because the words had already done the whole job. This is what the project calls a redundancy null — perceptual input adds no signal where the text distribution already saturates the task. It is honest about its own limits, and the limit is the interesting part: this was the easy case, where text was always going to be enough, so the test was least able to detect a contribution from perception exactly where it looked cleanest. It says nothing about the hard, blurry cases — the fine shades of polysemy, the abstract senses where text might genuinely not suffice — which is precisely where seeing might still matter and precisely where no one has yet looked. The grounding question is not closed by this. It is narrowed, and the prior is nudged, and the genuinely informative experiment — on the cases where text runs out — is left standing as the next thing to do. That is what an honest null looks like: not a door slammed, but a region of the map carefully marked "nothing here, and here is exactly why, and here is where to look next."

The most distinctive experiment, and the one that turns the most romantic hypothesis into something testable, is about meaning between agents — Chapter 4's Brandomian fork, the idea that meaning might be constituted in the interaction rather than computed inside one head. Here the project built a little game. Two copies of the same model are paired up and shown hard-to-describe abstract shapes — the sort of thing people, famously, get strikingly good at naming together over a few rounds, coining private nicknames ("the ice-skater," "the rabbit") and then compressing them as a shared shorthand settles in. The AI pairs do coordinate: they coin names, reuse them, and a fresh third copy of the model can later recover what a coined name refers to from the record of the exchange. The crucial question is what the coined meaning depends on. If meaning were genuinely constituted between the agents — built in the lived, ordered back-and-forth — then the order of the conversation should matter: scramble the sequence of turns while keeping their content identical, and the meaning should degrade. If, instead, each agent merely computed the convention privately from the available information and they happened to match, then only the content should matter, not its order. So the experiment holds the content fixed and destroys the order, and measures.

The result, across all three models, is a clean null: the order makes no reliable difference. Giving the fresh agent the content of the prior turns helps it a lot; giving that content in its true order rather than scrambled helps essentially not at all. The convention is recovered from the set of what was said, not from the lived trajectory of saying it. In the project's phrase, this is coordination, not constitution: the agents converge, but there is no sign that the meaning lives between them rather than being computed within each and aggregated. A telling side-detail sharpens it: real human pairs, in the studies this was modeled on, shorten their descriptions as they converge — from around eight words down to around four over a handful of rounds, the compression that marks a shared shorthand settling in. The AI pairs converged on success but kept their descriptions at roughly constant length; they reached agreement without that human signature of a convention becoming jointly owned. This is a bounded negative — a small pilot, with a sharper version of the test deliberately deferred — so it characterizes the floor rather than closing the question. But it is no longer rhetoric. The hypothesis that machine meaning might live in the space between agents has been given a real first measurement, and the first measurement says: not here, not yet, not in this form.

Step back and the shape comes into view. Where the method can actually see, the machines' "meaning" looks model-internal (each model has it on its own; nothing appears in the space between them), graded and use-like (sense tracked as a smooth gradient, just as the use-and-prototype traditions predict, rather than as crisp definitions), and compositional at the grammar grain (it builds and reverses construction meanings, sometimes impressively). It beats the distributional shadow on specific, well-designed tests — which is more than the strict "stochastic parrot" picture allows. And yet it never escapes that shadow either: every positive remains re-describable, by a determined deflationist, as richer statistics, and the in-principle gap from the octopus stands untouched. Meanwhile the three places where meaning might also live, according to the traditions that demand more than text — out in the world it refers to, down in perception and the body, and between agents in a shared practice — come back, so far, either silent (we have no way even to test reference for a text model) or negative (the grounding null, the relational null). Each of those is bounded; not one is closed. That distribution of results — a real but modest internal structure, and a row of honest blanks and nulls on the "beyond text" frontiers — is not a thesis imposed in advance. It is what fell out of the measurements.

Closing: The Honest Place to Stand

We began with a sentence you understood without trying, and a gap you could not cross: knowing what words mean while being unable to say what meaning is. We have not closed that gap. No one has. The great traditions remain genuine rivals — meaning as the company words keep, as a grip on the world, as the inferences words license, as an anchoring in perception and a shared life — and the philosophy of meaning is not a solved science with the machines as an afterthought. If anything, the machines have made the rivalry more vivid, because they are a creature that has the first kind of meaning in abundance and conspicuously lacks the equipment the other kinds demand.

But something has changed, and it is worth naming precisely, because it is easy to miss between the headlines. We have learned how to turn a shouting match into an inquiry. The question "do the machines understand?" is, taken whole, a bad question — not because it is unimportant but because it is underdetermined: the strongest "no" is an in-principle claim that no behavior can touch, and the strongest "yes" is one that no behavior can force. Asked that way, it can only be argued, never settled, and the arguing has the hollow quality of two people insisting at each other across a gap that no evidence spans. What the patient, deflating, measure-don't-litigate approach offers instead is a way to make real progress on the parts that are tractable: which specific gradients a model tracks, which shadows it beats, which inferences it composes, which of the things our theories require it has and which it lacks — each answered with human evidence as the yardstick, each bounded by exactly what the experiment can and cannot show, and the failures written down beside the successes. That is not a smaller question dressed up as the big one. It is the big one, finally cut into pieces you can actually pick up.

So where does this leave the question of machine meaning? In a place that will satisfy neither the enthusiast nor the cynic, which is usually a sign of having stopped flinching. The honest verdict, on the evidence so far, is that these systems have something — a graded, use-like, compositional internal structure that beats the distributional shadow on careful tests and is not well described as mere parroting — and that this something is, on present measurement, model-internal rather than referential, ungrounded rather than embodied, and computed-within rather than constituted-between. Whether that something deserves the word "meaning" is a question the available evidence cannot, in principle, decide — and the discipline is to say so plainly rather than to round up to "understanding" or down to "autocomplete." Resist both the hype and the dismissal; they are the same error, a confident answer to an underdetermined question, wearing opposite costumes. The calibrated stance is less comfortable and more true: describe what is there, mark what is not, keep the nulls, and hold every claim to the size of its evidence.

There is a final dividend, and it is the one I find most moving. The effort to measure whether a machine means anything has forced us to be far more precise about what it is for a person to mean something. To design these experiments at all, you have to pull apart things that ordinary life keeps fused: sense from reference, use from truth-conditions, the company a word keeps from the world it grips, the inferences inside one head from the practice between many, the easy direction of meaning from the hard one. The machine, in other words, has turned out to be a strange new mirror — not because it reflects us back faithfully, but because, in being so unlike us while sounding so like us, it shows up by contrast the hidden machinery of our own understanding, the parts that were invisible precisely because they never failed. It may be that these systems mean very little, in the end, or mean it in some thin and alien way we are only beginning to chart. But held up to that mirror, the oldest question about words — what does this mean, and how could it? — has become, for the first time, something we can probe rather than only ponder. Carefully. Modestly. One shadow at a time.
