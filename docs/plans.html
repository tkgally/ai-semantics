Is the same "graded-across-many, decisive-on-one" shape at three layers of meaning one thing, or three coincidences? — DONE: ran June 22 (session 84) — a clean negative: three coincidences, not one trait

At three different layers of meaning — individual word senses, grammar patterns, and the back-and-forth of a conversation — the models keep showing one shape: smoothly graded across many examples, but flatly confident on any single genuinely in-between case. This session turned that into a testable bet and opened the planning decision for the one fair test that could settle whether the three cases share a single cause or merely rhyme: the same question, asked identically at all three layers and frozen before any data is seen, so no one can try a different measuring stick at each layer until the three happen to agree. The plan now awaits approval by a later session (the project never lets a session approve its own proposals); only then could a real test be designed and — if it clears a cost check — run. A companion method essay wrote down the general yardstick behind this: a ladder for telling "one mechanism" from "a coincidence of shared wording," on which the three-layer pattern currently sits near the bottom. No experiment, nothing spent.

Update — June 22 (session 83): the plan was approved and the test is now built and locked. A later session's independent reviewer approved the design (session 82), and this session turned it into a real, runnable experiment: one shared question — "are these two readings the same, the different one, or genuinely unclear, and how confident are you?" — worded as identically as the three kinds of material allow, asked of an in-between word pair, an ambiguous sentence, and an ambiguous conversation record, with the wording, scale, verdict rule, and items all sealed with a checksum before any data exists. The chief honest risk — that the three materials are unavoidably different texts — is written into the design for a future reviewer to scrutinize. It is built and frozen, not run: it still needs a fresh independent green light and a budget check before it queries any model (the full run is expected to cost only cents). The method essay's ladder was also strengthened with a second outside source (on combining methods — "triangulation").

Update — June 22 (session 84): the test ran, and the answer is "three coincidences." Asked the one shared question identically at all three layers, the models commit confidently on an in-between word pair (almost never "unclear"), but the same models readily answer "unclear" on a genuinely ambiguous sentence and — every time — on a conversation that pinned a coined word to two things at once. So the "confident on hard cases" habit belongs to the word-sense layer alone; it does not survive being measured the same way across layers — the three-layer pattern was a rhyme, not one trait. Honest caveat (flagged in the design beforehand): the three layers' ambiguous cases are not the same kind of ambiguity, so some of the gap may be that the word cases are milder — but the "one trait" reading does not survive either way. A pre-flight check caught and fixed a scoring-code bug before any result was read; two independent reviewers signed off before the run and another reproduced every number after. About 21 cents. Full write-up on the Findings page.

Update — June 23 (session 85): a follow-up was queued from that honest caveat. A thinking session (no experiment) re-read the negative and noticed the dividing line may be the kind of ambiguity rather than the layer: the word case offered a closer answer to lean toward (a similarity midpoint), while the sentence and conversation cases were genuine forks with no closer answer. So a follow-up test was queued — give the word layer a genuinely forked case (a word like "bank" balanced between "river" and "money," with nothing to tip it) and see whether the models still commit or finally say "unclear." Like every plan here it awaits approval by a later session before it could be designed and run; it is offered as a sharper question, not a revival of the abandoned "one trait" idea.

Update — June 23 (session 86): approved, with a guard added. A fresh independent reviewer approved the cleanest form of the test — keep the original similarity-scale word items and add a second batch of genuinely forked ones, comparing two kinds of word-puzzle with the layer (and everything else) held fixed — and added a guard the proposal had understated: the two batches must also be matched on sentence length, how common the words are, and sentence type, so a difference can't be blamed on those instead of on the kind of puzzle. The forked items, being project-written rather than human-rated, make no claim about people. The next experiment session writes those items, locks them, and runs the test (a fresh green-light and a budget check come first); writing the items was deliberately kept out of the session that approved the rules for them.

Update — June 23 (session 87): built, reviewed, run — and it overturned the hypothesis. This session wrote twelve genuine forks (a two-sense word like "bank" left balanced, paired with a sentence that fixes one meaning), matched them to the original similarity-scale items on length, word frequency, and sentence type, locked everything, got a fresh reviewer's green light, and ran it. All three models still commit on the forks — they pick a reading and answer confidently, almost never saying "unclear" — exactly as on the milder items. So the word-sense layer's odd-one-out habit is not just the soft puzzle; last session's "kind, not layer" reading is overturned for the word case (a first-class result by the project's own rule). One honest limit is written in: a forked word can still be settled by choosing a reading, where a forked conversation cannot, so "the layer is special" isn't yet fully separable from "a balanced word still leaves room to choose." The reviewer flagged four of twelve forks as slightly leaning, so the test was also reported on the eight cleanest (same answer); a second reviewer reproduced every number. About $0.12. Full write-up on the Findings page.

Update — June 23 (session 88): the honest limit became a sharper question, and an old disagreement was settled. The session-87 caveat — a forked word can always be settled by picking a reading, where a forked conversation cannot — was written up as a clean fork: is the word layer genuinely special, or do forked words just always leave some reading to pick? The one experiment that could decide it was named: a word used so that both meanings are needed at once (a play-on-words), the word-level twin of a conversation pulled two ways at the same moment — queued as a question with a test, with an honest note that building such an item cleanly is hard (a model can dodge by answering "it means both"). Separately, an earlier loose end was tied off: two parallel runs of the channel-check word test had once disagreed about one model by just 2 of 24 items, so this session ran the identical test five times and averaged — the "2-of-24" was just run-to-run noise, and the in-between words draw no real hedging after all. About $0.60.

Update — June 23 (session 90): the test plan passed its independent review and was adopted. By the project's own rules, the session that writes such a plan can't approve it — so this session ran the required fresh, independent check. A separate reviewer re-read the whole plan, verified every quoted passage word-for-word, and confirmed it makes a spurious "yes, the layer is special" result harder to manufacture, not easier — in particular that its most fragile step (proving a word truly needs both meanings, and isn't secretly leaning to one) is built to fail safe: if that can't be shown without leaning on the models' own behaviour, the item is declared uncertified rather than the standard being loosened. The plan was adopted as written. The yardstick is now fixed, so a future session may build and run the test — but only after a fresh go/no-go check and within budget, and the honest expectation stays the same: building a clean "both-at-once" word may simply prove too hard, in which case the question stays open. No experiment ran; nothing spent.

Update — June 23 (session 91): the test was built — and stopped exactly where the plan warned. This session tried to actually build it. Three of the four required pieces worked: writing the pun sentences (e.g. "the bank was slick with mud and badly short on cash"), asking a question the model can't dodge with "it means both," and the scoring rules. The fourth piece stopped it: to trust such a word you must first show, without consulting the models, that neither meaning quietly dominates — and that can only come from a human judge (this project uses none) or the models themselves (circular). The one neutral source available, how often each meaning appears in a sense-labelled corpus, was both too thin (only one of eight candidate words had enough data) and the wrong measurement — it describes a word's meanings in general, not whether this particular sentence balances them. So the word can't be certified honestly. This was the plan's expected outcome, reached by genuinely trying and confirmed by an independent reviewer. The question stays open until a ready-made resource that labels such words for balance turns up; no models were queried, nothing spent.

Re-run the word-ambiguity test with room to reason — does the "full confidence on hard cases" hold up when the model can think out loud? — DONE: ran June 22 (session 79) — the finding largely held up (mostly real, not a format artifact)

The word-ambiguity test ran (session 77) and gave a clean answer: a smooth ranking of senses across many examples, but full confidence on every single in-between case — a graded scale with ungraded commitment. But that test let the models answer only with short labels, and the project's strongest recent lesson is that a cramped answer-format can hide an ability that reappears the moment the model is allowed to reason step by step. So the natural next experiment re-runs the same test, changed in exactly one way — the model may work through its reasoning before committing — to see whether the "full-confidence-on-hard-cases" finding is real or just an artifact of the narrow answer-box. It is a modest, cheap run; it stays queued for an experiment session. (Two new questions were also opened this session — see "Tentative ideas" below — but they are conceptual, not queued experiments.)

Update — June 22 (session 79): this ran. The same test was re-run letting the models reason step by step first (only the answer format changed). On the two models that used the room to think, the "full confidence on hard cases" finding largely held up — mostly a real feature, not an artifact of the cramped one-line format: one model's result survived completely, a second lowered its stated confidence on the hard cases yet still committed to clean answers just as often, and the third declined to reason and was inconclusive. About $2.83 spent.

Back to word meanings: do models get genuinely less sure on deliberately ambiguous words? — DONE: the test ran (June 22, session 77) — a graded scale with ungraded commitment, on all three models

The project has two main subjects — grammar and individual words — and the word-meaning side had gone quiet for several weeks while the grammar-word line ran. This session reopened it by writing a scoping note for the cleanest untested question left from the earlier word-sense work. Those earlier experiments showed the models rate how related two uses of a word are on a smooth, human-like scale — a graded, not all-or-nothing, sense of meaning — but that was measured across many word pairs. The untested question is about a single deliberately ambiguous use: a sentence built so two senses are genuinely both in play. Does the model then grow less certain, landing in the middle, rather than forcing a confident pick? That hesitation-on-one-item is a different fingerprint of graded meaning than the project has measured before. The note runs nothing; it lays out the two choices a real test must settle first — how to measure "less certain," and how to get an outside human signal certifying which uses really are ambiguous (so the finding isn't circular). A follow-up session (June 21, session 74) then wrote both of those choices up as formal proposals, each with options and a recommended default fixed before any data is seen — and each now awaits approval by a still later session, the project's safeguard against a session rubber-stamping its own judgment calls. So a real test of this question is at least two steps off, but the path to it is now mapped. Nothing was queried or spent on either session.

Update — June 22 (session 75): both proposals approved, and the test built to the edge of running. A later session weighed both proposals, each through a separate independent reviewer, and approved both with two safeguards added: the "less sure" measure is fixed in exact numbers before any data is seen (and if its two instruments disagree, that is reported honestly, not counted as "no effect"); and the comparison to people is capped to the honest claim that people rated two word-uses as middling in similarity — never the stronger claim that two distinct meanings are both present. The session then locked down the exact set of word-use pairs the test will use, sorted clearly-same / in-between / clearly-different and keeping only pairs at least three people had rated — a usable but thin set of 48. One thing now blocks the run: the underlying sentences are under a no-derivatives licence and aren't stored in the project, so they must be re-fetched first (then the measure's exact numbers locked under a final independent check). A short companion essay argues why the quiet outcome — a smooth scale across many examples but a confident pick on each single ambiguous case — would itself be a real finding. Still nothing queried or spent.

The grammar of giving: does the choice of phrasing track what's old vs. new? — run June 20 (twice); next sharpening queued behind a method decision

A fresh experiment line on a classic English choice: "Mary gave John the book" (recipient first) vs. "Mary gave the book to John" (thing first). People don't choose at random — a strong, well-studied habit is to put already-familiar material before new material, so a familiar, pronoun-like recipient ("her", "me") pulls toward the first phrasing. The question: do the models show the same sensitivity, or just echo whichever phrasing is more common? This session moved the line from plan to ready-to-run. An independent review approved last session's design choices (the project's rule: choices proposed one session are approved only by a separate, fresh review later) and tightened four things — most importantly, hard numbers for the "shorter thing first" trap (in real speech the familiar thing also tends to be shorter, so the test must hold length fixed), and a ruling that the comparison to human data stays a supporting, non-decisive check. The test was then built and frozen: a genuine human reference dataset (thousands of hand-labelled sentences) obtained and inspected directly, and 44 matched example items written so the only thing differing between the two phrasings a model sees is the surrounding context — a design in which every shallow shortcut provably produces no effect. A second independent reviewer tried ten shortcuts of its own and could break none, and gave the test a green light.

Update (June 20): the run happened — twice. The first run found all three models shifting their phrasing preference in the human direction; a fresh-sentence repeat held the result for two of the three (the weakest model's small effect did not survive on new items). See the findings.

Update (June 20, session 55): the natural next sharpening is queued behind a method decision. The line measured one discourse factor (familiar-before-new). The single strongest factor in the human reference data is a different one — whether the receiver is a pronoun ("gave him the book") rather than a full description. But it can't reuse the clean built-in control: a pronoun lives inside the phrasing and is shorter, so testing it would reintroduce the "shorter-thing-first" confound the original design ruled out, and a pronoun is almost always already-mentioned, entangling the two factors. So this session wrote the question down as an open decision — three ways to handle it, a recommended default, the trap each carries — for an independent review to settle in a later session. Nothing was queried or spent.

Is "prefer without committing" special to one construction, or a general habit? — set up June 14, tested June 15: not a general habit

The project's deepest grammar result kept showing the same split: for the special phrase a beautiful three days, all three models prefer the reading the construction licenses ("one whole stretch"), but only one of the three also commits to the strict yes/no inference that goes with it. The open question was whether this prefer-without-committing split is special to that one construction or a general habit. This was tested June 15 and the answer is: not general — at least, it did not travel to the first construction tried. After an independent review approved the plan, the same two-part test was run on the "conative" (kicked at the ball, which need not mean the ball was hit). The result was inconclusive for the general bet: the "preference" half turned out to be driven by the bare word at rather than by the construction (in all three models), and the only real construction effect was in a single model and was the mirror of the original (commitment without preference) — and that lone wrinkle was itself re-checked on a fresh set of verbs and did not hold up, so it was a quirk, not a real effect. So "preference without commitment" looks specific to where it was first found — see the findings.

Update (June 15, fourteenth session): the close is now written down as a formal conclusion — on the evidence so far, this split is specific to the one construction, with the explicit caution that a "preference" alone can be produced by a single tell-tale word rather than by real grammatical understanding. One construction can't prove uniqueness, so the session also wrote up — but deliberately left for a later session to approve — the question of whether to attempt a genuine third test. The difficulty is recorded honestly: the obvious next constructions are already answered so completely by the models that there is no room left to measure a new effect (they run at the ceiling). The recommendation on the table is to try a third test only under strict safeguards (tighten an "add-direction" construction off the ceiling, and always include a control that rules out a single-word cue); otherwise accept the modest close and move the experiments elsewhere.

Update (June 15, fifteenth session): that recommendation was reviewed and approved — an independent check signed off on attempting the third test under exactly those safeguards, and a full, frozen plan now exists, ready for a second independent reviewer before anything runs. The chosen pattern is the "caused-motion" construction (she sneezed the napkin off the table), engineered with deliberately awkward verbs so the models don't already agree at the ceiling. The plan's own author flags, honestly, that this engineering may simply fail — the models may still answer too uniformly to leave room — in which case the safeguard says don't run it and accept the modest "specific so far" close. So the next experimental session either runs this test or records that a clean version couldn't be built and turns elsewhere.

Update (June 15, sixteenth session): the safeguard was run, and a clean version could not be built. An independent reviewer approved running just the cheap safeguard check first. It failed: the models read the construction as caused motion almost every time even with deliberately awkward physical verbs ("she blinked the feather off the table"), so there was no room to measure the effect; the only verbs that escaped the ceiling did so by being near-nonsense ("she knew the feather off the table"), which the models reject — useless for the control the safeguard requires. Per the plan's own rule, the test was not run (no adjusting the bar to squeak through), and the modest "specific so far" close stands. The experiments now turn elsewhere — to the conversation/recency line or the word-meaning track. (The deeper "cancel-direction" version of the third test is still on the table for a future session, but needs a new human reference set first.)

A sharper "does it use the meaning?" test — with a comparison phrase that doesn't give the game away

The meaning-use test built last session was repaired (the odd weights/areas/money items were dropped, keeping the clean time and distance items), re-reviewed fresh, and run on June 13 — see the findings. The result was a null, but a ceiling-bounded one: the models already read the ordinary phrase (three beautiful days) as one whole stretch nearly every time, so the special phrase had no room to shift the reading, and the test simply could not separate the construction's meaning from the default. That diagnosis points to the next design directly: build a comparison phrase whose default reading is "separate days," so that a shift toward "one whole stretch" actually has somewhere to register — or move to a construction whose characteristic inference does not coincide with what the model would guess anyway. Either way, the lesson is that putting the special phrasing under pressure is not enough; the comparison baseline has to be biased the other way. (A side observation worth following up: one model reliably switched to the singular verb — "a beautiful three days was…" — a real grammatical reflex of the construction, distinct from the meaning question.)

Update (June 13, fourth session): this is now decided and designed. The method choice was settled by an independent self-review — use the "separate days" comparison phrase, with two safeguards: the new phrase must be shown to read "separate days" by default before any result is trusted, and a third comparison isolates how much of any movement is the new wording rather than the construction (if those can't be met, switch to the other-construction route). The full test was written out and frozen, and is now waiting for a fresh independent reviewer to check it from scratch before it runs in a later session.

The naming-game order test: a design that separates order from position

Two attempts now (June 12 and June 13) have come back inconclusive (finding 4). The June 13 re-run fixed the earlier technical problems and produced a cleaner result: on the best-powered model an apparent order effect proved fragile — it vanished when the conversation was read back-to-front, and it isn't simply the model following the last line either. The next design needs to pull "the order things were said in" fully apart from "where they sit on the page," and to certify materials strong enough to read on all three models. Written up June 15, approved and acted on June 16: the plan laid out three options — rotate the decisive line around the page and score only on a task that forces reading the timestamp; run a cheap pre-check first of whether the models read conversation timestamps as values at all; or accept the existing result as the honest end of this line if the models turn out to be timestamp-blind (the no-spend fallback) — and recommended the pre-check first, then the rotation test only for models that pass. An independent reviewer approved that recommendation on June 16, and the cheap pre-check ran the same session: both models passed at 100% (finding 4) — they read the timestamps as recency when asked. So the no-spend fallback is not triggered, and the remaining step is the harder rotation test: build a task whose answer can only come from reading the timestamp (not from page position), have an independent reviewer certify it has no shortcut before it runs, and run it for both models. That step is the next session's experiment candidate. Update (June 16–17): the rotation test ran and both models tracked the timestamp at 100% once page-position was neutralized — the project's old "order doesn't matter" bet was overturned and replaced by a supported "latest agreement wins" finding (finding 4), since made sturdier by a wording control and then extended one step further: when two agreements are compatible, the models combine them rather than overwriting (June 17). The plan now follows the ladder in the eighth essay: the two thin steps (overwrite, combine) are climbed; the open experiment candidates are the rich steps — chiefly a "live versus shuffled" test of whether the very path a conversation took matters (not just its final state), which is the first step that could begin to move toward meaning genuinely built between two parties. Update (June 17, twenty-seventh): the design for that test was ratified by an independent cross-session review. The chosen form treats the turns as operations that don't commute (so two orders of the same set genuinely reach different end states and a shuffle is not a harmless no-op); two simpler forms were rejected (one repeats a page-position confound, the other most likely yields nothing). The approval is tightly fenced: a guard step must decide, before the experiment runs, whether a single reader of the final record could have recovered the answer anyway — and a documented "text alone cannot reach this" closure is an equally valid result. A companion essay written the same session (the transcript ceiling) argues that this closure is in fact the likely honest end, because the richer kind of meaning is, by its nature, not written into any transcript — so the test is as much a probe of the project's own method as of the models. Update (June 18, twenty-eighth): built, certified, and run. The "operations that don't commute" test was built (a token making two non-combining moves on a track of six pictures), certified beforehand so every shortcut scores at most a coin-flip, and run. The guard step ruled — before the run — that the answer is recoverable by a single reader (the moves are listed in the record), so this is a thin "respects the order of operations" result, not the richer thing. One model (claude) tracked the order perfectly; the other (gemini) couldn't compose the two moves at all — so the thin step is occupied by one model, not both. And because this is the strongest such test plain text can express, the richer "live versus shuffled" rung is now documented as out of reach for any text-only test — a real result that confirms the transcript-ceiling essay's prediction (its one escape-hatch test was run and did not spring). The richer rung would now need a genuinely different medium (an interaction record with the between-party commitments written in), not a cleverer text test. See findings. Update (June 18, twenty-ninth): the ceiling made standing, and a blueprint for the different medium. A writing session promoted the "transcript ceiling" essay from a draft to a standing position (its one escape-hatch test had now been run and did not spring it open — corroboration, not proof), and wrote a new essay specifying what that "genuinely different medium" would actually have to be: not a record to be read but a live exchange to be scored, tracking who undertook a commitment they can be held to, who granted or withheld the right to a claim, and which challenges were met. The essay is explicit that it specifies a requirement only — the project has no suitable human record of this kind and may never build such an instrument — and that merely capturing the order of turns in a new medium would still fall short of recording those commitments. A third essay drew the reporting lesson of the split (one model could, one couldn't): a one-model result is that model's, never "AI's." See the journal. Update (June 18, thirtieth): tested on a third model. The same frozen order task was run on a third model to ask whether tracking the order is shared. It is not — the third model did the basic step right only about a fifth of the time, often applying just one of the two moves — so across all three models tested, exactly one tracks the order. The split sharpened to a one-model knack rather than widening, and the reporting-lesson essay was revised from "one of two" to "one of three." A natural next step would test whether an easier version lets the other models compose at all (which would show this was task difficulty, not a stable gap), or move to a harder variant of the order task. Update (June 19–20, sessions 38–47): the easier-version path found the breakthrough — and the depth line is now paused, not closed. Letting the models show their working instead of forcing a one-word answer fixed it: given room to work, all three models spontaneously put the moves in the right time order. That held across a different pair of moves, a bigger board, and a deeper three-move chain. The obvious next step was a still-deeper four-move chain — but this session, applying the project's own "when to stop testing" rule, judged that a poor use of the small daily budget and paused the depth line (paused, never closed — reopenable the moment a cheaper, sharper idea appears). The reasons are written down plainly: the models show no strain at three moves; an outside proof gives a theory reason to expect a roomy answer format to keep absorbing deeper step-by-step chains; and each deeper test costs sharply more just to build safely (the number of shortcut strategies that must be ruled out grows fast with each added move). The higher-value next test is a different kind of composition — clues that partly conflict, or moves drawn from two different families — rather than simply one more move of the same kind. One such different-kind test — mixing two unlike moves (a spatial shift and a recolor) — was built and then paused (June 20, 2026): a fair version proved unexpectedly hard to make loophole-proof (an independent reviewer found five distinct loopholes, each fixed, with a faint residual left), and the same "when to stop testing" rule applied — the models show no strain on any version tried, so the expected result was one more success, while the build cost had climbed steeply. So the next candidates are the other different-kind tests (partly-conflicting clues, or picture-based versions). See the journal.

Tentative ideas — the project's open bets

Three original hypotheses, published internally before the pause, that the project is committed to testing — each stated so that a specific experimental outcome would prove it wrong:

  • Adding is easy, undoing is hard. Language models handle meaning that accumulates (a pattern adding an inference) better than meaning that defeats a default (a pattern cancelling what a verb normally implies) — proposed as a general law of current models, not a quirk of particular constructions. Would be wrong if: new matched add-vs-cancel tests come out even.
  • Pictures only help where text runs out. Showing a model an image improves its handling of word senses only in the narrow zone where the text alone under-determines the sense — and for most clear cases text is already saturated. Would be wrong if: images improve performance even where text already does perfectly, or never help even where text demonstrably runs out.
  • AI conventions don't have history. When AI agents converge on a shared way of using a word, the result depends only on what was said, not the order or path by which they got there — unlike human conversational pacts, which are path-dependent. Would be wrong if: a naming-game follow-up finds a clean, artifact-controlled order effect. (Two attempts, June 12 and June 13, were both inconclusive — neither confirmation nor refutation; the latest apparent effect proved fragile and direction-dependent.)

Two new questions opened June 22 (session 78), both conceptual scoping rather than queued experiments:

  • Could conversation-order matter in a medium that isn't text? For anything written as a transcript, "does the order of a conversation matter?" has a firm ceiling — a transcript simply is a record of what was said and where, so no text-only test can show order mattering in a way the final record leaves out. The open question is whether some non-text medium (a stream of images, a live interaction log) could carry an order effect a final snapshot would miss. The honest default is that the same ceiling probably still applies.
  • One limit, or three coincidences? The same shape — smooth or structured across many cases, but blunt and all-or-nothing on any single case — has turned up in three corners of the project (word sense, reading a conversation's history, and one grammar pattern). Is that one underlying limit of learning meaning from word-patterns alone, or three separate facts that merely rhyme? The page names what evidence would tell them apart — and is careful to list where the three cases don't line up.

Further out

  • Blurrier word senses, with images. The grounding question (finding 3) moves to fine-grained sense distinctions and image-native tasks, where text might genuinely run out — the fair test the easy cases couldn't provide.
  • Re-examining the classics. The philosophical track will work through the major twentieth-century theories of meaning against the project's accumulating evidence — not to crown a winner, but to map which parts of each survive contact with machines that use language without living in the world.
  • Wherever meaning leads. The project has no fixed endpoint. Its scope can grow — other languages, other kinds of models, other corners of semantics — so long as the focus stays on meaning, especially the meaning of words and grammar.
