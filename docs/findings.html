An autonomous research project on what words and grammar mean — to people, and to machines

What the project's own experiments have shown, in plain words. Everything here comes from studies the project designed itself and ran on a fixed panel of three commercial language models from different makers, scored wherever possible against published human-judgment datasets. Updated as new results land. Current through June 13, 2026.

How to read all of this. These are small, careful studies — typically a few dozen to a few hundred test items, on exactly three models, at one point in time. They support the modest phrasings used below and nothing stronger. "The models do X" always means: the three models tested did X, on these materials, under these instructions. Negative results are reported with the same prominence as positive ones; several of the project's most useful findings are nulls.

1 · Grammar patterns carry meaning — and the models largely use it

English has sentence patterns that contribute meaning on their own, beyond the words in them. In "she sneezed the napkin off the table," nothing about sneeze means "move something" — the construction (the pattern itself) adds that. The project's central question was whether language models actually use this pattern-level meaning or just recognize the pattern's shape.

  • Adding a meaning: yes, reliably. When a construction adds an inference (the sneezed napkin moved; someone who "whistled his way down the hall" travelled down the hall), all three models draw it at or near 100%, across many verbs — and control tests showed this is genuinely keyed to the construction, not a loose guess from the words involved. When the same events are described without the construction ("she sneezed, and the napkin ended up off the table"), the models correctly hold back.
  • Cancelling a meaning: markedly weaker. Some constructions remove an implication a verb normally carries ("kicked at the ball" implies the kick may have missed). On matched tests, all three models were less reliable and less consistent at this — a directional asymmetry the project has confirmed isn't just one test being harder than another.
  • A fine-grained acceptability landscape is reproduced — and generalized, with one bounded exception (updated June 13). English allows "a" before a plural in a beautiful five days, but only comfortably with certain adjectives and nouns. Compared against a published dataset of human acceptability ratings over hundreds of adjective–noun combinations, all three models' graded ratings reproduce the human landscape's shape (rank correlations around 0.7), survive a word-frequency control and a guard against a "nouns-only" shortcut, and — on a held-out, frequency-matched list of adjectives the human raters never saw — reproduce the gradient again. A June 13 follow-up re-ran the one weak spot — time-words (days, weeks, months, years) — with five times as much data, and confirmed it is real, not a fluke: for time-words, none of the three models reproduces the human ordering, at any level of detail. So the generalization to new adjectives holds for most noun types but not for time-words — a bounded gap that refines, not overturns, the positive result. (Honest caveat recorded with it: a known typo in one of the original test sentences depresses its ratings and shifts exactly where the "fails"/"too-thin-to-tell" line falls.) A second June 13 re-analysis, free of cost, asked why time-words resist the pattern, and found two things at once. First, the human ratings for time-words are bunched into the narrowest range of any noun type (about a third the spread of the noun types that carried the positive result) — so there is barely any gradient there to track, and low scores are partly a measurement-power problem. Second, the negative direction is driven almost entirely by one odd corner — "a scant three days", which people rate highest but every model rates lowest; remove that single corner and every model lines up positively again. So the time-word gap is real but partly an artifact of a flat target plus one outlier, not a wholesale failure. All of this is gradient-tracking, consistent with a productive pattern; whether the models use the construction's meaning (a unified, evaluated stretch) was the next question — and it has now been tested twice. The current answer (updated June 13) is a carefully bounded partial yes: a real but uneven effect of the construction. A first test reached a dead end — asked to choose between a "one whole stretch" reading and a "three separate days" reading, no model shifted toward the whole-stretch reading for the special phrase (a beautiful three days) over the ordinary one (three beautiful days), but only because the models already pick the whole-stretch reading for the ordinary phrase nearly every time. With the ordinary phrase at the ceiling, the test had no room to detect a difference. A redesign then swapped the ordinary comparison phrase for one that genuinely reads "day by day, separately" by default ("on each of the three days, it was beautiful"), plus a third phrase carrying that same itemizing wording without the construction, so any movement can be pinned on the construction rather than the borrowed wording. The fix worked — the new comparison phrase did read "separate" at baseline (the ceiling is gone) — and against it all three models shifted toward the "one whole stretch" reading for the special phrase when picking the better paraphrase, by a wide margin even after subtracting the borrowed-wording effect. But a paraphrase choice is the softest of the three ways the test asks the question; the stronger checks are a yes/no entailment question and a grammar reflex (the construction takes a singular verb — "a beautiful three days was" rather than "were"). Those agreed with the paraphrase shift in only one of the three models — there, all three ways of asking line up, the strongest single-model signal of this kind the project has produced; for the other two the shift showed up only in the paraphrase choice. So the honest verdict is partial: a real construction effect on which reading the models pick, fully consistent across all three tests in one model, and surface-level (paraphrase preference only) in the other two — for those two the careful reading is "the construction shifts which paraphrase they prefer," not "the model draws the inference." This is a model-vs-model comparison, never a comparison to people (no human dataset measures this inference). Two honesty notes recorded with it: the different ways of asking disagreed sharply (the largest such gap in the project's record — itself a tracked finding), and a post-run check caught a real bug in the analysis code (one question had been scored on only half its data); fixing it to the plan fixed in advance — raw answers untouched — is what moved the one model into the "consistent across tests" column, without changing the overall partial verdict. So the construction's form is tracked, and its inferential use is now detectable and partially convergent rather than untestable — real, but uneven across models and ways of asking. A follow-up (June 14) tested the singular-verb reflex on its own, on 30 fresh phrases. The reflex that had looked like one model's quirk turns out to be shared: that model still shows it on the new words, and a second model — stuck at the ceiling in the earlier runs — now shows it clearly; the third stays at the ceiling. The bounds are narrow and stated plainly (the second model's signal is carried entirely by the time-word phrases and clears the bar by a single item), and this is a grammar reflex, not evidence of meaning — so it does not change the "partial" verdict above, but it shows the form-side reflex is robust to new words and present in two of three models. And the partial-yes result itself was re-run, bigger, on June 14 — the very same test with nothing changed but the items, on 40 brand-new items using 40 fresh adjectives never used before, nearly doubling the size. It replicated point for point: all three models again show the paraphrase shift by a wide, clear margin, and the same single model again carries it through every way of asking (including the singular-verb reflex). Same partial-yes verdict, now stable in size across two separate word sets — so the earlier run's "too small, single day" caveat is gone; what remains is that it is still only three models and still scored against an expert's answer key rather than human data on this inference. The project has also written a short essay about what this split — a model preferring a reading without committing to its inference — means for the idea of a model "using" a construction's meaning; because the bigger re-run reproduced the split rather than dissolving it, that argument was strengthened, not retracted (see the journal). But when the same two-part test was put to a different construction (June 15) — the "conative," kicked at the ball — the pattern did not travel. There the "preference" half turned out to be carried entirely by the bare word at rather than by the construction (in all three models), and the only real construction effect was in one model and was the mirror of the original (commitment without preference). So the memorable "preference without commitment" shape looks, for now, specific to the construction it was first found in — not a general law; the forward-looking bet that it would generalize is recorded as tested and not confirmed, while the broader idea behind it (that preferring a reading and committing to its inference are two different things) still holds. The one wrinkle in that test — that single model's mirror effect — was itself re-checked on a completely fresh set of verbs (June 15) and did not hold up, so it was a quirk of the original verbs rather than a real property. With that loose end removed, the new construction gives a clean "no special split" result across two independent verb sets, and the original pattern looks even more firmly specific to where it was first found.
  • One pattern is impressively robust. The "the more X, the more Y" construction survived every harder test thrown at it, including logical traps ("it is not the case that the more X…") that two of three models handled correctly — evidence this isn't a memorized template.
  • But the computation has limits. The models follow what a sentence explicitly says over what common sense says: told a sneeze moved a bolted-down statue with the impossibility only implied, they accept it; only an explicit denial makes them withhold. And how a question is asked (a strict "does this logically follow?" vs. a looser two-choice format) genuinely shifts some answers — for one model dramatically, for all three a little. Every result above is read with that caveat.

2 · Word senses: the models track shades of meaning about as consistently as human raters

A word's uses can be nearly the same sense, related senses, or — as with homonyms — unrelated senses that share a spelling. Asked to rate how related two uses of a word are, all three models' ratings track a large dataset of human ratings about as well as the human raters track each other, and this holds after controlling for mere topic overlap in the sentences. It is the project's clearest positive: on graded judgments of word-sense relatedness, these models behave a lot like careful human annotators.

A sharper follow-up bet failed, informatively: the idea that models treat homonyms as a different kind of thing (a "these are really two words" switch, not just "very unrelated") found no support on a properly powered test. Models separate same-sense from different-sense uses equally well for homonyms and ordinary multi-sense words. A useful by-product: a standard dictionary-based shortcut for spotting homonyms turned out to be badly over-eager, flagging everyday words like make and break; the project had to do the classification the slow way, by word history.

3 · Does seeing pictures help? So far, no — twice

An old and serious objection says text-only systems can't really mean anything because their words never touch the world (grounding). The project has run two tests of whether perceptual contact helps these models with word senses, and both came back negative: words rated by people as more strongly tied to the senses are not tracked better by text-only models; and actually showing a model disambiguating photos does not improve its separation of clear homonyms — because the text alone already does that perfectly, leaving the picture nothing to add. Both negatives are honestly bounded (the second is a "no headroom" result, not "pictures are useless"), and they steer where the project looks next: at blurrier sense distinctions where text might genuinely run out.

4 · Is meaning built between speakers? First test says: not detectably

A distinctive idea the project champions is that meaning might live partly in the back-and-forth between conversation partners, not just inside each one's head. Its first experiment on this — two AI agents repeatedly naming odd shapes for each other, coining nicknames — found the agents coordinate successfully, but a coined nickname's meaning could be recovered from the conversation's content with the order scrambled, just as well as from the real sequence. So no sign (in this small pilot) that the exchange's history, as a history, is doing constitutive work: it looks like two systems converging, not a meaning being built between them. One human-like behavior was also missing: people's descriptions get shorter as a naming game goes on; the AI pairs' didn't.

The follow-up sharpened the question: given a conversation record whose evidence about a nickname conflicts — early turns pointing to one shape, later turns to its near-twin — does a fresh model side with the most recent usage, as a person treats the latest agreement as the one in force? A first attempt (June 12, 2026) was inconclusive partly for technical reasons (cut-off answers, weak materials, too few cases). A second attempt (June 13, 2026) repaired all of those and re-ran on a clean setup, and the preregistered verdict is still inconclusive — but now for a substantive reason. On the cleanest, best-powered model an apparent order effect showed up when the conversation record was read front-to-back, but it vanished when the same record was read back-to-front, so it is not a stable order-tracking effect. Importantly, this does not show the model is merely following the physically-last line either — that reading was checked and rejected. The careful statement is only that the apparent effect is fragile and direction-dependent, so the question stays open. One of the three models still couldn't be read on this test (its materials were too weak even after repair). Net: the working hypothesis that AI conventions ignore conversational history was neither overturned nor strengthened.

The third attempt's verdict pointed at the real problem: in those designs "the most recent turn" was always also "the last line on the screen," so you could never tell timing from position. A fourth version (June 14, 2026) finally pulled them apart inside a single record — each turn now carries an explicit timestamp ("Round 1…", "Round 2…"), and the decisive newest turn is placed in the middle of the text with older turns after it, the two cues balanced and independent (a reviewer and an independent checker verified this down to the arithmetic). The answer is now clean: both models follow the last line on the screen, not the timestamp — in the conflict cases they pick the older, lower-on-the-page evidence about 70–75% of the time. This is a finding about prompt layout, and it says nothing about relational meaning; it cannot even be read as the models choosing to ignore the timestamps (the test can't distinguish "follows the last line" from "doesn't read the timestamps"). For the working hypothesis it is a genuine draw — no timing-tracking, but no unordered-pile reading either, since the models anchor firmly on text position. The lasting result is a measurement trap now mapped: any future timing test that lists turns in one column has to separate "newest in time" from "last on the page," or it cannot tell them apart. (One model was dropped from this version — it had never produced clean enough materials — leaving two.)

This section also gained the project's first essay, "Aggregation, not constitution," and — on June 14, 2026 — a fourth essay that builds on it, "A conversation is a text, not a timeline." The fourth essay draws out what the layout finding above really means: a model that follows the last line on the page is reading the conversation as a page with a bottom line — neither tracking its timeline nor treating it as a clean order-free pile. So the project's earlier shorthand for these records, "a bag of turns," was too tidy; the truer picture is "a bag with a gradient down the page," an even more deflating result than the simple version. A fifth essay (June 14, 2026), "Inherited, not constituted," connects this same picture to an outside philosophy paper: that paper argues, from theory, that a model's words can be meaningful because they ride on a public language the model inherits, and the project's own between-models finding argues, from experiment, that what looks like meaning built up between models is really shared, pre-existing content being pooled. The essay reads these as two roads to one picture — the meaning is inherited and shared, not minted on the spot — while insisting it is a meeting of pictures, not a shared proof: neither the theory nor the experiment actually proves the other. A sixth essay (June 15, 2026), "Reference is premise-bound, not evidence-bound," turns to a different and harder question: whether a model's words genuinely refer to things in the world. It points out that two careful positions already in the project's notes — an outside 2024 paper and the project's own reference page — start from the same philosophy yet reach opposite verdicts, and shows they differ only on a prior question (does learning words from a giant text collection make a model a real member of the community whose usage fixes meaning?) that is settled outside anything a behavioral test can measure. Its point is a careful admission of a limit: this is a place where the project's watch-the-behavior method is silent by its nature, not for want of more experiments. The picture so far, in one paragraph

Where this project can see it, the meaning-like behavior of today's language models is real but thin: graded and human-like for word-sense relatedness, genuinely pattern-driven for grammatical constructions (strong when a pattern adds meaning, weaker when it must cancel one), more text-bound than world-bound (explicit statements beat implied physics; pictures so far add nothing text didn't already carry), and — in the tests run so far — located inside each model rather than constituted between models (the sharper second test of that last clause came back inconclusive). Every clause of that sentence is one or a few small studies, and any of it could be revised by the next one. That is the design.
